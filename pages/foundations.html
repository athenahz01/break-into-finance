The Income Statement

The income statement (also called the Profit & Loss statement, or P&L) measures a company's financial performance over a period of time -- typically a quarter or a fiscal year. It answers one question: did the company make money or lose money during this period?

Every income statement follows the same logical flow: start with revenue at the top, subtract costs layer by layer, and arrive at net income at the bottom. This is why net income is called the "bottom line."

Key Concept
The income statement is an accrual-based document. Revenue is recognized when earned (not when cash is collected), and expenses are recognized when incurred (not when cash is paid). This distinction between accrual and cash accounting is fundamental to understanding all three statements.

Line-by-Line Walkthrough

Line ItemDescriptionExample ($M)
Revenue (Sales)Total value of goods sold or services rendered. Also called "top line." This is recognized when the earnings process is substantially complete, regardless of cash collection.$1,000
Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)Direct costs of producing goods or delivering services. Includes raw materials, direct labor, and manufacturing overhead. For a software company, this includes hosting costs and customer support. For a retailer, it is the wholesale cost of inventory sold.($400)
Gross ProfitRevenue minus COGS. Measures how efficiently the company produces its core product. A company with a high gross margin has pricing power or a low-cost production model.$600
Selling, General & Administrative (SG&A)Operating expenses not directly tied to production. Includes salaries for non-production staff, rent, marketing, insurance, office supplies, and legal fees. Sometimes broken out into "Sales & Marketing" and "G&A" separately.($200)
Research & Development (R&D)Costs of developing new products or improving existing ones. Typically expensed as incurred (not capitalized) under US GAAP, except for certain software development costs.($50)
EBITDAEarnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation & Amortization. Not a GAAP line item but universally used in finance. Proxy for operating cash flow before capital expenditures. Calculated as Gross Profit - SG&A - R&D (before D&A).$350
Depreciation & Amortization (D&A)Non-cash charges. Depreciation allocates the cost of tangible assets (buildings, equipment) over their useful lives. Amortization does the same for intangible assets (patents, acquired customer lists). D&A reduces taxable income but is NOT a cash outflow in the current period.($50)
EBIT (Operating Income)EBITDA minus D&A. Represents profit from core operations, ignoring how the business is financed (debt vs equity) and taxes. Also called Operating Profit.$300
Interest ExpenseThe cost of borrowing money. Paid to debt holders (banks, bondholders). This is why EBIT is "capital structure neutral" -- it is calculated before interest. Interest expense is tax-deductible, creating a "tax shield."($40)
Earnings Before Tax (EBT)EBIT minus Interest Expense (plus any Interest Income). Also called "pre-tax income."$260
Income TaxesCorporate income tax. The statutory US federal rate is 21%, but the effective tax rate varies due to state taxes, deductions, credits, and international operations. Effective rate is calculated as Tax Expense / EBT.($65)
Net IncomeThe "bottom line." Profit available to equity shareholders after all expenses, interest, and taxes. This flows directly to the Balance Sheet as an increase in Retained Earnings and is the starting point for the Cash Flow Statement.$195
Income Statement Structure
Revenue
- COGS
= Gross Profit
- SG&A - R&D
= EBITDA
- D&A
= EBIT (Operating Income)
- Interest Expense
= EBT (Earnings Before Tax)
- Taxes
= Net Income
Pro Tip: EBITDA is not on the income statement
You will not find EBITDA as a formal line item on any GAAP income statement. It is a non-GAAP metric that analysts calculate separately. Companies often report "Adjusted EBITDA" which adds back stock-based compensation and other one-time charges. Always check what adjustments have been made -- companies love to make Adjusted EBITDA look as large as possible.
Common Mistake
Do not confuse EBIT and EBITDA. EBIT includes D&A (so it is lower), while EBITDA adds D&A back. In an interview, if someone asks "what is operating income?", the answer is EBIT, not EBITDA. EBITDA is an approximation of cash flow; EBIT is the GAAP operating profit figure.
Worked Example: Building an Income Statement

A company sells 100,000 widgets at $50 each. Each widget costs $20 to manufacture. The company has $1.5M in SG&A, $0.5M in R&D, $0.3M in D&A, $0.2M in interest expense, and a 25% tax rate.

  • Revenue: 100,000 x $50 = $5,000,000
  • COGS: 100,000 x $20 = $2,000,000
  • Gross Profit: $5,000,000 - $2,000,000 = $3,000,000 (60% gross margin)
  • EBITDA: $3,000,000 - $1,500,000 - $500,000 = $1,000,000
  • EBIT: $1,000,000 - $300,000 = $700,000
  • EBT: $700,000 - $200,000 = $500,000
  • Taxes: $500,000 x 25% = $125,000
  • Net Income: $500,000 - $125,000 = $375,000

The Balance Sheet

The balance sheet is a snapshot at a single point in time (e.g., December 31, 2025). It shows what a company owns (assets), what it owes (liabilities), and the residual value belonging to shareholders (equity). The fundamental equation is:

The Accounting Equation
Assets = Liabilities + Shareholders' Equity

This equation must always balance -- hence the name "balance sheet." Every single transaction a company makes affects at least two accounts on the balance sheet (double-entry bookkeeping).

Assets (What the Company Owns)

Assets are listed in order of liquidity -- how quickly they can be converted to cash. Current assets are expected to be converted within one year; non-current assets take longer.

Current Assets

Line ItemDescription
Cash & Cash EquivalentsPhysical cash, bank deposits, money market funds, and any instrument maturing within 90 days (like T-bills). This is the most liquid asset.
Short-term InvestmentsMarketable securities (stocks, bonds) that the company intends to sell within one year. Also called "marketable securities."
Accounts Receivable (A/R)Money owed to the company by customers who have received goods or services but have not yet paid. Think of it as an IOU from customers. Reported net of an allowance for doubtful accounts (estimated uncollectible amounts).
InventoryGoods held for sale. Includes raw materials, work-in-progress, and finished goods. Service companies may have little or no inventory. Valued at the lower of cost or net realizable value.
Prepaid ExpensesPayments made in advance for future goods or services (e.g., prepaid rent, insurance premiums). As the benefit is consumed, the prepaid asset decreases and an expense is recognized.

Non-Current (Long-term) Assets

Line ItemDescription
Property, Plant & Equipment (PP&E)Tangible long-lived assets: land, buildings, machinery, vehicles, furniture. Reported net of accumulated depreciation (historical cost minus total depreciation taken to date). PP&E = Gross PP&E - Accumulated Depreciation.
Intangible AssetsNon-physical assets with value: patents, trademarks, copyrights, customer relationships, technology. Amortized over their useful lives (except for indefinite-lived intangibles like certain trademarks).
GoodwillThe premium paid above the fair value of net identifiable assets in an acquisition. If Company A buys Company B for $500M and Company B's net identifiable assets are worth $350M, goodwill is $150M. Goodwill is NOT amortized; it is tested annually for impairment.
Long-term InvestmentsEquity stakes in other companies, long-term bonds, or other securities the company intends to hold for more than one year.

Liabilities (What the Company Owes)

Current Liabilities

Line ItemDescription
Accounts Payable (A/P)Money the company owes to its suppliers for goods or services received but not yet paid for. The mirror image of A/R.
Accrued ExpensesExpenses that have been incurred but not yet paid: accrued wages, accrued interest, accrued taxes. These are recognized as liabilities because the obligation exists even though cash has not left the door.
Deferred RevenueCash received from customers for goods or services not yet delivered. Also called "unearned revenue." Common in subscription businesses (SaaS companies collect annual fees upfront). It is a liability because the company owes the customer a product or service.
Short-term DebtBorrowings due within one year, including the current portion of long-term debt and revolving credit facilities.

Non-Current Liabilities

Line ItemDescription
Long-term DebtBonds payable, term loans, notes payable, and other borrowings due beyond one year. The most significant non-current liability for most companies.
Deferred Tax LiabilitiesTaxes that are owed but not yet due. Arise when taxable income is less than book income in the current period (e.g., due to accelerated depreciation for tax purposes). The company will pay more taxes in the future.
Pension ObligationsThe present value of retirement benefits owed to employees under defined benefit plans, net of plan assets.
Operating Lease LiabilitiesSince ASC 842, companies must recognize the present value of future lease payments as a liability on the balance sheet.

Shareholders' Equity (The Residual)

Line ItemDescription
Common Stock & APICPar value of shares issued plus Additional Paid-In Capital (the amount investors paid above par value when shares were issued). This represents the capital shareholders originally invested in the company.
Retained EarningsCumulative net income earned since inception, minus cumulative dividends paid. This is where net income from the income statement flows into the balance sheet. Retained Earnings(end) = Retained Earnings(begin) + Net Income - Dividends.
Treasury StockCost of shares that the company has repurchased from the open market. Reported as a NEGATIVE number (contra-equity). Share buybacks reduce equity.
Accumulated Other Comprehensive Income (AOCI)Unrealized gains and losses not flowing through the income statement: foreign currency translation adjustments, unrealized gains/losses on certain securities, and pension adjustments.

Working Capital

Working Capital
Working Capital = Current Assets - Current Liabilities

In practice, analysts focus on Net Working Capital (NWC):
NWC = Accounts Receivable + Inventory - Accounts Payable

Working capital measures a company's short-term financial health and operational efficiency. A company with positive working capital can meet its short-term obligations. Negative working capital is not necessarily bad -- companies like Amazon and Costco operate with negative working capital because they collect from customers before paying suppliers.

Why Working Capital Matters for Cash Flow
An increase in net working capital is a use of cash. If A/R goes up by $10M, that means you recognized $10M in revenue that you haven't collected yet -- you have less cash than the income statement suggests. If A/P goes up by $10M, that means you owe suppliers $10M more but haven't paid yet -- you have more cash than the income statement suggests. This linkage is critical on the Cash Flow Statement.

The Cash Flow Statement

The cash flow statement reconciles the difference between net income (accrual-based) and the actual change in cash during the period. It answers a simple but critical question: where did cash come from, and where did it go?

The cash flow statement has three sections, each capturing a different type of cash movement:

I. Cash Flow from Operations (CFO)

CFO measures the cash generated by the company's core business activities. It starts with net income and adjusts for non-cash items and changes in working capital. Most companies use the indirect method:

Cash Flow from Operations (Indirect Method)
Net Income
+ Depreciation & Amortization (non-cash expense, add back)
+ Stock-Based Compensation (non-cash expense, add back)
+ Deferred Taxes (non-cash portion of tax expense)
+/- Changes in Working Capital:
  - Increase in Accounts Receivable (use of cash)
  - Increase in Inventory (use of cash)
  + Increase in Accounts Payable (source of cash)
  + Increase in Deferred Revenue (source of cash)
  + Increase in Accrued Expenses (source of cash)
= Cash Flow from Operations
The Logic of Working Capital Adjustments

The rule is simple: increases in current assets are uses of cash (you spent cash or are owed more cash that you haven't received), and increases in current liabilities are sources of cash (you received cash or owe cash that you haven't paid).

  • A/R increases $5M: Revenue was $5M higher than cash collected. Subtract $5M.
  • Inventory increases $3M: You bought $3M more inventory than you sold. Subtract $3M.
  • A/P increases $2M: You bought $2M of goods you haven't paid for yet. Add $2M.

II. Cash Flow from Investing (CFI)

CFI captures cash spent on or received from long-term investments. This section is typically negative for growing companies because they are investing in their future.

Line ItemCash Impact
Capital Expenditures (CapEx)Negative (cash outflow to buy PP&E)
AcquisitionsNegative (cash paid to acquire other companies)
Purchases of InvestmentsNegative (buying securities or other investments)
Proceeds from Asset SalesPositive (cash received from selling assets)
Proceeds from Investment SalesPositive (selling securities)
Free Cash Flow (FCF)
Free Cash Flow = Cash Flow from Operations - Capital Expenditures

FCF represents the cash available to pay debt holders and equity holders after maintaining and expanding the asset base. This is the single most important metric in valuation because DCF models discount future FCF to determine a company's value.

III. Cash Flow from Financing (CFF)

CFF tracks cash flows between the company and its capital providers -- both debt holders and equity holders.

Line ItemCash Impact
Debt Issuance (Borrowings)Positive (company receives cash from lenders)
Debt RepaymentsNegative (company pays back lenders)
Equity IssuancePositive (company sells new shares for cash)
Share Repurchases (Buybacks)Negative (company buys back its own shares)
Dividends PaidNegative (cash distributed to shareholders)
The Cash Reconciliation
Beginning Cash Balance
+ Cash Flow from Operations
+ Cash Flow from Investing
+ Cash Flow from Financing
= Ending Cash Balance

The ending cash balance must equal the cash line on the balance sheet at period end.
Pro Tip: Why CFO Matters More Than Net Income
A company can report positive net income while burning cash (e.g., if A/R is growing rapidly because customers are not paying). Conversely, a company can report a net loss but generate positive cash flow (e.g., large D&A charges on a cash-generating asset base). Investors and analysts focus on cash flow because you cannot pay bills, make acquisitions, or return capital with accounting profits -- you need actual cash.
Worked Example: Building a Simple Cash Flow Statement

Using our income statement example: Net Income = $375K, D&A = $300K, A/R increased $100K, Inventory decreased $50K, A/P increased $75K, CapEx = $400K, Debt repayment = $200K, Dividends = $50K.

  • CFO: $375K + $300K - $100K + $50K + $75K = $700K
  • CFI: -$400K (CapEx only) = ($400K)
  • CFF: -$200K - $50K = ($250K)
  • Net Change in Cash: $700K - $400K - $250K = $50K
  • FCF: $700K - $400K = $300K

Self-Test: The Three Financial Statements

1. Depreciation is a non-cash expense. Where does it appear and how does it affect cash flow?

It reduces revenue on the income statement and increases cash on the CFS
It only appears on the balance sheet as a reduction to PP&E
It reduces operating income on the IS, reduces PP&E on the BS, and is added back on the CFS because it is non-cash
It increases EBITDA on the income statement
Depreciation flows through all three statements: it reduces EBIT on the income statement (it is an expense below EBITDA), it reduces the net PP&E balance on the balance sheet (via accumulated depreciation), and it is added back to net income on the cash flow statement because it is a non-cash charge -- no cash actually left the company when depreciation was recorded.

2. A company collects $500K from customers this quarter but only recognizes $400K in revenue (the rest was for services not yet delivered). How does the $100K difference appear on the balance sheet?

$100K increase in Accounts Receivable
$100K increase in Deferred Revenue (a liability)
$100K increase in Retained Earnings
$100K decrease in Cash
When cash is collected before revenue is recognized, it creates Deferred Revenue -- a liability. The company has received cash ($100K increase in cash on the asset side) but owes the customer a future product or service ($100K increase in deferred revenue on the liability side). The balance sheet stays balanced: Assets +$100K = Liabilities +$100K.

3. If Accounts Receivable increases by $20M year-over-year, what is the effect on Cash Flow from Operations?

CFO decreases by $20M (it is subtracted)
CFO increases by $20M (it is added)
No effect on CFO; it only affects CFI
CFO decreases by $20M only if the company has negative net income
An increase in A/R means the company recognized revenue that it has not yet collected in cash. Since net income includes this revenue, but the cash has not actually been received, we subtract the $20M increase in A/R from net income to arrive at actual cash generated. This is always the adjustment regardless of whether net income is positive or negative.

How the Three Statements Link

The #1 Interview Question in Finance
"Walk me through how the three financial statements are linked" is asked in virtually every investment banking, private equity, and corporate finance interview. Master this completely.

The Core Linkages

The three statements are connected through several key bridges:

  1. Net Income from the Income Statement flows into Retained Earnings on the Balance Sheet and is the starting point for the Cash Flow Statement.
  2. Depreciation & Amortization on the Income Statement reduces PP&E / Intangible Assets on the Balance Sheet and is added back on the Cash Flow Statement.
  3. Capital Expenditures on the Cash Flow Statement increase PP&E on the Balance Sheet.
  4. Changes in Working Capital items (A/R, Inventory, A/P) appear on both the Balance Sheet and the Cash Flow Statement.
  5. Debt Issuance/Repayment on the Cash Flow Statement changes the Debt balance on the Balance Sheet, and the debt balance drives Interest Expense on the Income Statement.
  6. Dividends and Share Repurchases on the Cash Flow Statement reduce Retained Earnings / Equity on the Balance Sheet.
  7. The Net Change in Cash from the Cash Flow Statement equals the change in the Cash balance on the Balance Sheet.

The Classic Walk-Through: "$10 Increase in Revenue"

Assume a company has a 20% COGS rate, a 25% tax rate, depreciation of $0, and the $10 in revenue is collected in cash immediately (no change in A/R).

Step-by-Step: $10 Revenue Increase Flows Through All Three Statements ▶
1

Income Statement

  • Revenue increases by $10
  • COGS increases by $2 (20% of $10)
  • Gross Profit increases by $8
  • Pre-tax income increases by $8 (no other expenses affected)
  • Taxes increase by $2 (25% of $8)
  • Net Income increases by $6
2

Cash Flow Statement

  • Start with Net Income: +$6
  • No non-cash adjustments (no depreciation in this example)
  • No working capital changes (collected in cash, no A/R change)
  • CFO increases by $6
  • No investing or financing activity
  • Net change in cash: +$6
3

Balance Sheet

  • Assets: Cash increases by $6
  • Liabilities: No change (assume taxes are paid immediately)
  • Equity: Retained Earnings increases by $6 (from Net Income)
  • Check: Assets (+$6) = Liabilities ($0) + Equity (+$6). It balances.

The Depreciation Linkage

Now assume the company buys a machine for $100 (5-year straight-line depreciation = $20/year). In Year 1:

Depreciation Flow-Through Example ▶

At the time of purchase:

  • CFS (CFI): CapEx of -$100
  • Balance Sheet: Cash -$100, PP&E +$100 (net zero change in total assets)

Each year for 5 years (the depreciation):

  • Income Statement: D&A expense of $20 reduces EBIT and pre-tax income by $20. At 25% tax rate, taxes decrease by $5. Net Income decreases by $15.
  • CFS (CFO): Start with Net Income (-$15 impact). Add back D&A (+$20). Net CFO impact: +$5 (the tax savings from the depreciation deduction).
  • Balance Sheet: PP&E decreases by $20 (accumulated depreciation). Retained Earnings decreases by $15 (lower net income). Cash increases by $5 (the tax shield). Check: Assets decrease by $15 ($20 PP&E down + $5 Cash up). Equity decreases by $15 ($15 lower Retained Earnings). Balanced.

The net cash impact of depreciation is the tax shield: D&A x Tax Rate = $20 x 25% = $5 per year.

The Working Capital Linkage

What if the $10 Revenue is Not Collected in Cash? ▶

If the $10 sale is on credit (customer has not paid yet):

  • Income Statement: Identical -- Revenue +$10, Net Income +$6. The IS does not care when cash is collected.
  • CFS: Net Income +$6, but A/R increased by $10 (a use of cash). Working capital adjustment: -$10. But wait -- COGS of $2 may have been paid in cash (reducing the A/R adjustment's net effect depends on other working capital items). Simplifying: if the A/R increase related to this sale is $10, CFO = $6 - $10 = -$4. You actually have less cash despite the sale.
  • Balance Sheet: Cash -$4, A/R +$10. Total Assets +$6. Retained Earnings +$6. Balanced.

This is exactly why high-growth companies with long payment terms can be cash-flow negative despite strong revenue growth.

The Debt / Interest Linkage

How Issuing $100 of Debt Flows Through ▶

When debt is issued:

  • CFS (CFF): +$100 debt proceeds
  • Balance Sheet: Cash +$100, Long-term Debt +$100. Assets = Liabilities; balanced.
  • Income Statement: No immediate impact.

Each period (interest at 5% = $5/year):

  • Income Statement: Interest Expense of $5 reduces EBT. Tax savings: $5 x 25% = $1.25. Net Income decreases by $3.75.
  • CFS (CFO): Net Income -$3.75. Interest is a cash expense (no add-back needed since it is already in CFO via net income). CFO = -$3.75. Some companies classify interest paid in CFO, others in CFF.
  • Balance Sheet: Cash -$3.75 (interest paid net of tax savings). Retained Earnings -$3.75. Balanced.

When debt is repaid:

  • CFS (CFF): -$100 debt repayment
  • Balance Sheet: Cash -$100, Long-term Debt -$100. Balanced.
  • Income Statement: No impact (principal repayment is not an expense).
Common Interview Mistake
Candidates often forget that principal repayment of debt is NOT an expense on the income statement. Only the interest is an expense. The principal repayment appears solely on the CFS (Cash Flow from Financing) and the Balance Sheet (reducing the debt balance). This is a frequent source of confusion.

Self-Test: Statement Linkages

1. A company records $50M in depreciation expense. Assuming a 25% tax rate, what is the net cash impact of this depreciation?

Cash decreases by $50M
Cash increases by $12.5M (the depreciation tax shield)
Cash increases by $50M
No net cash impact
Depreciation is non-cash, so it does not directly reduce cash. However, it reduces taxable income, which lowers the company's tax bill. The cash savings = D&A x Tax Rate = $50M x 25% = $12.5M. On the CFS: Net Income is lower by $37.5M (after-tax impact of depreciation), but D&A of $50M is added back. Net CFO impact = -$37.5M + $50M = +$12.5M.

2. A company repays $30M of debt principal. What happens to the three statements?

IS: Interest expense decreases by $30M. BS: Debt -$30M, Cash -$30M. CFS: CFO -$30M.
IS: No impact. BS: Debt -$30M, Retained Earnings -$30M. CFS: CFF -$30M.
IS: No impact. BS: Debt -$30M, Cash -$30M. CFS: CFF -$30M.
IS: $30M expense. BS: Debt -$30M. CFS: CFO -$30M.
Debt principal repayment has NO income statement impact (it is not an expense). On the Balance Sheet, cash decreases by $30M and debt decreases by $30M (both sides go down equally -- assets and liabilities). On the CFS, it appears in Cash Flow from Financing as -$30M. Future interest expense will be lower (since the debt balance is reduced), but that is a separate, future-period effect.

Key Financial Ratios & Metrics

Financial ratios transform raw financial data into comparable, meaningful metrics. They allow you to compare companies of different sizes, track performance over time, and identify strengths and weaknesses. Ratios are grouped into four categories.

Profitability Ratios

These measure how efficiently a company converts revenue into profit at various levels.

RatioFormulaWhat It Tells YouTypical "Good" Range
Gross MarginGross Profit / RevenueHow much profit is left after direct production costs. High gross margin = pricing power or low production costs.40-80% (software), 20-40% (manufacturing), 25-35% (retail)
EBITDA MarginEBITDA / RevenueOperational profitability before D&A, interest, and taxes. The most commonly used profitability metric in finance.15-25% is solid; >30% is excellent; varies by industry
Net Profit MarginNet Income / RevenueBottom-line profitability after ALL expenses.10-20% is strong for most industries
Return on Equity (ROE)Net Income / Average Shareholders' EquityHow effectively the company uses shareholder capital to generate profit. Higher = more efficient.15-25% is strong; >25% is excellent
Return on Assets (ROA)Net Income / Average Total AssetsHow efficiently the company uses ALL assets (debt + equity funded) to generate profit.5-10% for most; >10% is strong; asset-light businesses are higher
Return on Invested Capital (ROIC)NOPAT / Invested CapitalThe return earned on all capital invested in the business (debt + equity, net of cash). NOPAT = EBIT x (1 - Tax Rate). Invested Capital = Total Debt + Equity - Cash. The best single metric for measuring value creation.ROIC > WACC means the company creates value. 15%+ is excellent.
DuPont Analysis (Decomposing ROE)
ROE = Net Margin x Asset Turnover x Equity Multiplier

ROE = (Net Income / Revenue) x (Revenue / Assets) x (Assets / Equity)

This shows three levers to improve ROE: (1) earn more profit per dollar of revenue, (2) generate more revenue per dollar of assets, or (3) use more leverage (more debt relative to equity).

Liquidity Ratios

These measure a company's ability to meet its short-term obligations.

RatioFormulaWhat It Tells You"Good" Range
Current RatioCurrent Assets / Current LiabilitiesCan the company cover its short-term debts? A ratio above 1.0 means current assets exceed current liabilities.1.5 - 3.0 (too high may mean inefficient use of assets)
Quick Ratio(Cash + Short-term Investments + A/R) / Current LiabilitiesLike the current ratio but excludes inventory (which may be hard to liquidate quickly). More conservative measure.>1.0 is healthy

Leverage Ratios

These measure how much debt a company uses relative to its earnings or equity.

RatioFormulaWhat It Tells You"Good" Range
Debt / EBITDATotal Debt / EBITDAHow many years of EBITDA would it take to repay all debt. THE most important leverage metric in banking and credit analysis.<2.0x (investment grade), 3-5x (leveraged), >6x (highly leveraged)
Debt / EquityTotal Debt / Total EquityHow much debt vs equity finances the company. Higher = more financial risk.0.5-1.5x for most industries
Interest CoverageEBIT / Interest ExpenseHow easily the company can pay interest on its debt. Higher = safer.>3.0x is comfortable; <1.5x is dangerous
Net Debt / EBITDA(Total Debt - Cash) / EBITDALeverage adjusted for cash on hand. Net Debt is more meaningful than gross debt because cash could be used to repay debt.<2.0x for investment grade

Efficiency Ratios (Activity Ratios)

These measure how efficiently a company manages its working capital and assets.

RatioFormulaWhat It Tells You
Asset TurnoverRevenue / Average Total AssetsHow efficiently assets generate revenue. A higher ratio means the company generates more revenue per dollar of assets.
Inventory TurnoverCOGS / Average InventoryHow many times inventory is sold and replaced per year. Higher = faster-moving inventory. Retailers target 8-12x; grocery stores 15-20x.
Days Sales Outstanding (DSO)(Accounts Receivable / Revenue) x 365Average number of days to collect payment from customers. Lower = faster collection. 30-45 days is typical for B2B.
Days Payable Outstanding (DPO)(Accounts Payable / COGS) x 365Average number of days the company takes to pay its suppliers. Higher = the company holds onto cash longer (favorable).
Days Inventory Outstanding (DIO)(Inventory / COGS) x 365Average number of days inventory sits before being sold. Lower = more efficient.
Cash Conversion Cycle (CCC)
CCC = DSO + DIO - DPO

The CCC measures the number of days between paying for raw materials and collecting cash from customers. A shorter cycle means the company converts its investments into cash more quickly.

Example: DSO = 45 days, DIO = 30 days, DPO = 60 days
CCC = 45 + 30 - 60 = 15 days

A negative CCC (like Amazon's) means the company collects from customers BEFORE paying suppliers -- a very favorable position.
Worked Example: Ratio Analysis for Company XYZ

Company XYZ: Revenue $500M, COGS $200M, EBITDA $150M, Net Income $75M, Total Assets $800M, Total Equity $400M, Total Debt $300M, Cash $50M, A/R $60M, Inventory $40M, A/P $35M, Interest Expense $20M, EBIT $120M.

  • Gross Margin: ($500M - $200M) / $500M = 60%
  • EBITDA Margin: $150M / $500M = 30%
  • Net Margin: $75M / $500M = 15%
  • ROE: $75M / $400M = 18.75%
  • Debt/EBITDA: $300M / $150M = 2.0x
  • Net Debt/EBITDA: ($300M - $50M) / $150M = 1.67x
  • Interest Coverage: $120M / $20M = 6.0x
  • DSO: ($60M / $500M) x 365 = 43.8 days
  • DIO: ($40M / $200M) x 365 = 73.0 days
  • DPO: ($35M / $200M) x 365 = 63.9 days
  • CCC: 43.8 + 73.0 - 63.9 = 52.9 days

Assessment: Strong profitability (60% gross, 30% EBITDA margin), moderate leverage (2.0x Debt/EBITDA, well-covered interest at 6.0x), but the cash conversion cycle of ~53 days suggests capital is tied up in working capital for nearly two months.

Time Value of Money

The time value of money (TVM) is the foundational principle of all of finance. The core idea: a dollar today is worth more than a dollar tomorrow. Why? Because a dollar today can be invested to earn a return, so its future value will be greater than $1. Equivalently, a future dollar is worth less than $1 today because you sacrifice the opportunity to invest it in the meantime.

Present Value (PV) and Future Value (FV)

Future Value
FV = PV x (1 + r)^n

Where: PV = present value, r = interest rate per period, n = number of periods
Present Value
PV = FV / (1 + r)^n

This is "discounting" -- converting a future cash flow into today's dollars.
Worked Example: Present Value

You will receive $1,000 in 3 years. The appropriate discount rate is 8%. What is the present value?

PV = $1,000 / (1.08)^3 = $1,000 / 1.2597 = $793.83

This means you would be indifferent between receiving $793.83 today and $1,000 in 3 years, if you can earn 8% per year on your investments.

Worked Example: Future Value

You invest $5,000 today at 6% annual return for 10 years. What will it be worth?

FV = $5,000 x (1.06)^10 = $5,000 x 1.7908 = $8,954.24

Your $5,000 grows to nearly $9,000 over 10 years through compound interest.

Net Present Value (NPV)

Net Present Value
NPV = -Initial Investment + CF1/(1+r)^1 + CF2/(1+r)^2 + ... + CFn/(1+r)^n

NPV = Sum of PV of all future cash flows minus the initial investment cost

NPV answers the question: "Is this investment worth more than it costs?" If NPV > 0, the investment creates value. If NPV < 0, it destroys value. The discount rate used should reflect the riskiness of the cash flows (the required rate of return).

Worked Example: NPV Calculation

A project costs $10,000 upfront and generates cash flows of $3,000, $4,000, $4,500, and $3,500 over 4 years. The required return is 10%.

  • Year 0: -$10,000
  • Year 1: $3,000 / (1.10)^1 = $2,727.27
  • Year 2: $4,000 / (1.10)^2 = $3,305.79
  • Year 3: $4,500 / (1.10)^3 = $3,380.92
  • Year 4: $3,500 / (1.10)^4 = $2,390.51

NPV = -$10,000 + $2,727.27 + $3,305.79 + $3,380.92 + $2,390.51 = $1,804.49

The NPV is positive ($1,804.49), so this project creates value. You should invest.

Internal Rate of Return (IRR)

Internal Rate of Return
IRR is the discount rate that makes NPV = 0.

0 = -Initial Investment + CF1/(1+IRR)^1 + CF2/(1+IRR)^2 + ... + CFn/(1+IRR)^n

There is no closed-form solution; IRR must be solved iteratively (or using Excel's IRR function).

IRR represents the annualized return of an investment. The decision rule: if IRR > required return (hurdle rate), accept the project. For the example above, the IRR is approximately 18.0% -- well above the 10% hurdle rate, confirming the positive NPV.

The Intuition Behind Discounting

Discounting captures three things simultaneously:

  1. Opportunity cost: You could invest the money elsewhere. A future dollar has to be "penalized" for the lost opportunity.
  2. Risk: Future cash flows are uncertain. The higher the risk, the higher the discount rate, and the lower the present value of those uncertain cash flows.
  3. Inflation: A dollar in the future buys less than a dollar today due to inflation.

The discount rate bundles all three factors together. Riskier cash flows are discounted at higher rates, making their present value lower -- which is exactly what we want.

IRR Pitfalls
  • Multiple IRRs: If cash flows change sign more than once (positive, negative, positive), there can be multiple IRRs. Use NPV in these cases.
  • Scale problem: A project with 50% IRR and $100 NPV is worse than a project with 20% IRR and $10,000 NPV. IRR does not account for the size of the investment.
  • Reinvestment assumption: IRR implicitly assumes all cash flows are reinvested at the IRR, which is often unrealistic for high-IRR projects.

Cost of Capital (WACC)

The Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC) is the blended cost of all capital (debt and equity) used to finance a company's operations. It represents the minimum return a company must earn on its existing asset base to satisfy its creditors and shareholders. WACC is the discount rate used in a DCF (Discounted Cash Flow) analysis to value unlevered free cash flows.

WACC Formula
WACC = (E/V) x Re + (D/V) x Rd x (1 - T)

Where:
E = Market Value of Equity
D = Market Value of Debt
V = E + D (Total Capital)
Re = Cost of Equity
Rd = Cost of Debt
T = Corporate Tax Rate

The (1 - T) factor on debt reflects the tax shield: interest expense is tax-deductible, so the after-tax cost of debt is lower than the stated interest rate.

Cost of Equity (Re) via CAPM

The Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM) is the most widely used method to estimate the cost of equity:

CAPM Formula
Re = Rf + Beta x (Rm - Rf)

Where:
Rf = Risk-Free Rate (typically the 10-year US Treasury yield)
Beta = Measure of the stock's volatility relative to the market
(Rm - Rf) = Equity Risk Premium (ERP) -- the excess return of the market over the risk-free rate

Beta of 1.0 = moves with the market
Beta > 1.0 = more volatile than the market (e.g., tech stocks, beta ~1.2-1.5)
Beta < 1.0 = less volatile (e.g., utilities, beta ~0.5-0.7)
Understanding Each CAPM Component
  • Risk-Free Rate (Rf): The return on a "riskless" investment, proxied by the 10-year US Treasury bond yield. As of late 2025, this is approximately 4.0-4.5%.
  • Equity Risk Premium (ERP): The extra return investors demand for holding stocks instead of risk-free bonds. Historical average is approximately 5-6%, though estimates vary. Duff & Phelps and Damodaran publish widely-used estimates.
  • Beta: Measures systematic (market) risk. Sourced from Bloomberg, Capital IQ, or calculated by regressing the stock's returns against market returns over 2-5 years. Use levered beta for a specific company; unlever and relever beta when comparing across different capital structures.

Cost of Debt (Rd)

The cost of debt is the effective interest rate a company pays on its borrowings. It can be estimated as:

  • Yield to Maturity (YTM) on the company's existing bonds (most accurate, market-based)
  • Credit rating-based approach: Look at the yield on bonds with a similar credit rating
  • Interest Expense / Total Debt as a rough approximation using financial statements

Remember: the relevant cost of debt in WACC is the after-tax cost: Rd x (1 - T). If a company's pre-tax cost of debt is 5% and the tax rate is 25%, the after-tax cost is 5% x (1 - 0.25) = 3.75%.

Capital Structure Weights

WACC uses market values, not book values, for the weights:

  • Equity weight (E/V): Market Capitalization (share price x shares outstanding) divided by total capital.
  • Debt weight (D/V): Market value of debt (or book value as a proxy, since debt market values are close to book for investment-grade companies) divided by total capital.
Common Mistake: Using Book Value of Equity
Always use market value of equity (market cap), not book value. Book value of equity represents historical invested capital and can be vastly different from what the market values the company at. A company like Apple has a book equity of ~$60B but a market cap exceeding $3 trillion.

Worked WACC Calculation

Worked Example: Full WACC Calculation

Company data:

  • Share price: $50, Shares outstanding: 200M. Market Cap = $10B
  • Total Debt (market value): $4B
  • Total Capital (V) = $10B + $4B = $14B
  • Risk-Free Rate: 4.0%
  • Beta: 1.2
  • Equity Risk Premium: 5.5%
  • Pre-tax Cost of Debt: 5.5%
  • Tax Rate: 25%

Step 1: Cost of Equity

Re = 4.0% + 1.2 x 5.5% = 4.0% + 6.6% = 10.6%

Step 2: After-tax Cost of Debt

Rd x (1 - T) = 5.5% x (1 - 0.25) = 5.5% x 0.75 = 4.125%

Step 3: Weights

E/V = $10B / $14B = 71.4%

D/V = $4B / $14B = 28.6%

Step 4: WACC

WACC = (0.714 x 10.6%) + (0.286 x 4.125%)

WACC = 7.57% + 1.18% = 8.75%

This company's WACC is 8.75%. In a DCF, you would discount unlevered free cash flows at this rate.

Pro Tip: Sensitivity Analysis
WACC is highly sensitive to its inputs -- especially the equity risk premium and beta. A small change in either can significantly alter your DCF output. Always run a sensitivity table showing valuation at different WACC assumptions (e.g., 7.5% to 10.0%). This is standard practice in investment banking pitchbooks.

Self-Test: WACC & Cost of Capital

Interest expense is deductible from taxable income, which creates a "tax shield." If a company pays $100 in interest and the tax rate is 25%, the interest deduction saves $25 in taxes. The true after-tax cost is only $75. The formula Rd x (1 - T) captures this benefit. This is one of the primary advantages of debt financing over equity financing.

Enterprise Value vs Equity Value

Understanding the difference between Enterprise Value (EV) and Equity Value is essential for valuation. These are not interchangeable, and confusing them is one of the most common mistakes in finance.

Equity Value (Market Capitalization)

Equity Value
Equity Value = Share Price x Diluted Shares Outstanding

Also called Market Capitalization (Market Cap). This represents the total value of the company to equity holders only.

Equity Value represents the value of the company that belongs to shareholders. If you bought every single share of the company at the current market price, this is what you would pay.

Enterprise Value

Enterprise Value (The EV Bridge)
Enterprise Value = Equity Value + Total Debt + Minority Interest + Preferred Stock - Cash & Cash Equivalents

Or equivalently:
EV = Equity Value + Net Debt + Minority Interest + Preferred Stock
where Net Debt = Total Debt - Cash

Enterprise Value represents the total value of the company to ALL capital providers -- both debt holders and equity holders. Think of it as the theoretical acquisition price: if you bought the whole company, you would pay the equity holders (market cap) and also assume the company's debt obligations, but you would get to keep the company's cash.

Each Component Explained

ComponentWhy It Is Added/Subtracted
+ Total DebtAn acquirer assumes the company's debt obligations. Debt holders have a claim on the company's operating value, so their claim must be included.
+ Minority InterestThe portion of a subsidiary's equity NOT owned by the parent. Since the parent consolidates 100% of the subsidiary's revenue and EBITDA, the corresponding enterprise value must include the minority's claim.
+ Preferred StockPreferred equity is more like debt (fixed dividends, priority over common stock). It represents a claim on enterprise value that is senior to common equity.
- Cash & EquivalentsCash can be used to immediately pay down debt, reducing the net cost of an acquisition. An acquirer effectively "gets" the cash when buying the company.

When to Use EV vs Equity Value

The Matching Principle for Multiples

The rule is simple: the numerator and denominator must be consistent.

  • Enterprise Value multiples use metrics BEFORE interest (available to all capital providers): EV/Revenue, EV/EBITDA, EV/EBIT. These metrics are "capital structure neutral" -- they do not depend on how the company is financed.
  • Equity Value multiples use metrics AFTER interest and applicable only to equity holders: P/E (Price/Earnings), P/Book, Price/FCF to Equity. Net income is "after interest" so it belongs to equity holders only.

Never divide Equity Value by EBITDA or Enterprise Value by Net Income. This is a mismatched multiple and will produce meaningless numbers.

Worked Example: Calculating EV

Company data: Share Price = $75, Shares Outstanding = 100M, Total Debt = $2B, Cash = $500M, Minority Interest = $200M, Preferred Stock = $0.

  • Equity Value: $75 x 100M = $7.5B
  • EV: $7.5B + $2.0B + $0.2B + $0 - $0.5B = $9.2B

If EBITDA is $1.5B:

  • EV/EBITDA: $9.2B / $1.5B = 6.1x

If Net Income is $800M:

  • P/E: $7.5B / $800M = 9.4x (use Equity Value, not EV)
Common Mistakes with EV
  • Forgetting to subtract cash: Cash reduces the effective cost of acquiring the business. If a company has $5B market cap and $3B cash, the EV is much lower than the market cap (assuming no debt).
  • Using book value of equity instead of market cap: EV always starts from market capitalization (market value of equity), never book equity.
  • Ignoring minority interest: If the company consolidates a subsidiary, 100% of EBITDA is in the financials, so 100% of the subsidiary's value (including the minority share) must be in EV.
  • Double-counting: Do not add operating leases to EV if they are already on the balance sheet as debt (post-ASC 842).

Self-Test: EV vs Equity Value

Because cash generates no return
Because cash is already included in equity value
Because an acquirer receives the cash and can use it to offset the acquisition cost (e.g., pay down debt)
When you acquire a company, you get its cash. If a company has a $10B market cap and $2B in cash, the true net cost to the acquirer is lower because they can use that $2B immediately. Enterprise Value represents the net cost to acquire the operating business: you pay equity holders, assume debt, but receive cash. Hence, EV = Equity Value + Debt - Cash.

2. Which of the following multiples is INCORRECTLY matched?

EV / Net Income
P/E (Equity Value / Net Income)
EV / Revenue
EV / Net Income is mismatched. Enterprise Value represents the value to ALL capital providers (debt + equity), but Net Income is only available to equity holders (it is calculated AFTER subtracting interest, which goes to debt holders). The correct pairing is: EV goes with pre-interest metrics (Revenue, EBITDA, EBIT) and Equity Value goes with post-interest metrics (Net Income, EPS).

Capital Structure

Capital structure refers to the mix of debt and equity a company uses to finance its operations. The choice between debt and equity has profound implications for a company's risk, return, cost of capital, and valuation.

Debt vs Equity: Key Differences

CharacteristicDebtEquity
Cost to the companyInterest payments (contractual, tax-deductible)Dividends + capital appreciation expectations (not tax-deductible)
Priority in liquidationSenior -- paid firstResidual -- paid last
Required returnLower (less risk for the investor)Higher (more risk for the investor)
Tax treatmentInterest is tax-deductible (creates tax shield)Dividends are NOT tax-deductible
ControlNo voting rights (usually)Voting rights and ownership
ObligationMandatory interest and principal paymentsDividends are discretionary
DilutionNo dilution to existing shareholdersNew shares dilute existing shareholders
Risk to the companyFinancial distress if unable to pay; default riskNo default risk from equity

Modigliani-Miller Theorem (The Foundation)

In 1958, Franco Modigliani and Merton Miller proposed a groundbreaking theorem. In a world with no taxes, no bankruptcy costs, no transaction costs, and efficient markets:

M&M Proposition I (No Taxes)
The value of a firm is independent of its capital structure. It does not matter how you divide the pie between debt and equity -- the total pie stays the same. The value of a company is determined solely by its operating assets and the cash flows they generate.

However, in the real world, taxes and bankruptcy costs DO exist, which modifies the conclusion:

M&M Proposition I (With Taxes)
Because interest is tax-deductible, debt creates a "tax shield" that increases firm value. The value of a levered firm = Value of an unlevered firm + PV of the tax shield. This suggests companies should use as much debt as possible to maximize the tax shield.

Optimal Capital Structure: The Trade-Off Theory

If the tax shield were the only consideration, every company would be 100% debt-financed. But debt also introduces costs:

Benefits of Debt

  • Tax shield: Interest expense reduces taxable income, saving Tax Rate x Interest in taxes each year.
  • Lower cost of capital: Debt is cheaper than equity (lower required return + tax deductibility).
  • Discipline: Mandatory payments force management to be efficient with cash (reduces agency costs).
  • No dilution: Existing shareholders maintain their ownership percentage.
  • Leverage amplifies returns: If ROA > cost of debt, leverage increases ROE.

Costs of Debt

  • Financial distress: If the company cannot meet debt payments, it faces bankruptcy. Direct costs (legal fees) and indirect costs (lost customers, employee attrition) can be enormous.
  • Reduced flexibility: Debt covenants restrict the company's operational freedom (limits on additional borrowing, required financial ratios).
  • Higher required returns on equity: As leverage increases, equity becomes riskier, so shareholders demand a higher return.
  • Agency costs: Conflicts between debt holders and equity holders (e.g., risk shifting, underinvestment).

The optimal capital structure is the debt-equity mix that minimizes WACC (and therefore maximizes firm value). This occurs at the point where the marginal benefit of the tax shield equals the marginal cost of financial distress.

Leverage Effects on Returns

Worked Example: How Leverage Amplifies Returns (and Risk)

A company has $100M in assets that earn a 15% return (EBIT = $15M). The tax rate is 25%.

Scenario A: All Equity ($100M equity, no debt)

  • EBIT: $15M. Interest: $0. EBT: $15M. Tax: $3.75M. Net Income: $11.25M.
  • ROE = $11.25M / $100M = 11.25%

Scenario B: 50% Debt ($50M equity, $50M debt at 6%)

  • EBIT: $15M. Interest: $3M. EBT: $12M. Tax: $3M. Net Income: $9M.
  • ROE = $9M / $50M = 18.0%

Scenario C: 75% Debt ($25M equity, $75M debt at 6%)

  • EBIT: $15M. Interest: $4.5M. EBT: $10.5M. Tax: $2.625M. Net Income: $7.875M.
  • ROE = $7.875M / $25M = 31.5%

Notice how ROE increases dramatically with leverage -- from 11.25% to 31.5%. But this works in reverse too: if EBIT drops to $5M in a recession, the highly leveraged company would barely cover interest, while the all-equity company still earns a positive return. Leverage amplifies BOTH gains and losses.

Pro Tip: Industry Norms
Different industries have vastly different "normal" leverage levels. Real estate (REITs) and utilities often operate at 50-70% debt. Technology companies often have very little debt (0-15%). Banks have extremely high leverage (90%+ debt-to-assets) due to the nature of their business model. When analyzing a company's capital structure, always compare it to industry peers.

Basic Accounting Concepts

Understanding accounting is not optional in finance. Every model, every valuation, and every investment decision is built on accounting data. These concepts are the grammar of the financial language.

Accrual vs Cash Accounting

Accrual Accounting

Revenue is recognized when earned (goods delivered, services performed), regardless of when cash is received. Expenses are recognized when incurred, regardless of when cash is paid.

All public companies and most large private companies use accrual accounting (required by GAAP and IFRS).

Example: A law firm completes $50K of work in December but does not get paid until January. Under accrual accounting, the $50K revenue is recorded in December.

Cash Accounting

Revenue is recognized when cash is received. Expenses are recognized when cash is paid.

Used primarily by very small businesses and for personal finances. Simpler but does not provide an accurate picture of economic performance over a period.

Example: Under cash accounting, the law firm would not record the $50K until January when the cash arrives.

Revenue Recognition

Revenue recognition determines WHEN revenue appears on the income statement. Under ASC 606 (the current US GAAP standard), revenue is recognized when a company satisfies a performance obligation -- i.e., when it transfers a promised good or service to a customer.

The five-step model:

  1. Identify the contract with a customer
  2. Identify the performance obligations in the contract
  3. Determine the transaction price
  4. Allocate the transaction price to the performance obligations
  5. Recognize revenue when (or as) the entity satisfies a performance obligation
Example: SaaS Revenue Recognition

A software company sells a $120,000 annual subscription, paid upfront on January 1. Even though the company receives all $120K in cash on Day 1, it can only recognize $10,000 per month as revenue ($120K / 12 months), because the performance obligation (providing access to the software) is delivered evenly over the year.

On January 1: Cash +$120K, Deferred Revenue +$120K (a liability). Each month: Revenue +$10K, Deferred Revenue -$10K.

The Matching Principle

Expenses should be recognized in the same period as the revenues they help generate. This is why inventory costs are not expensed when purchased -- they are expensed (as COGS) when the corresponding revenue is recognized (when the product is sold).

Example: Matching Principle

A retailer buys 1,000 units of inventory at $10 each ($10,000 total) in November. In December, it sells 600 units at $25 each. The COGS for December is $6,000 (600 x $10), not $10,000. The remaining $4,000 (400 unsold units) stays on the balance sheet as inventory until those units are sold.

Depreciation Methods

Depreciation allocates the cost of a tangible asset over its useful life. The choice of method affects the timing of expenses (and therefore net income and taxes) but NOT total cumulative depreciation over the asset's life.

Straight-Line Depreciation ▶
Straight-Line Depreciation
Annual Depreciation = (Cost - Salvage Value) / Useful Life

Equal expense each year. The simplest and most common method for GAAP financial reporting.

Example: Machine costs $100,000, salvage value $10,000, useful life 10 years.

Annual Depreciation = ($100,000 - $10,000) / 10 = $9,000 per year

Accelerated Depreciation (Double-Declining Balance) ▶
Double-Declining Balance (DDB)
Annual Depreciation = (2 / Useful Life) x Beginning Book Value

Higher depreciation expense in early years, lower in later years. Commonly used for TAX purposes (MACRS in the US) because it front-loads the tax deduction.

Example: Same machine ($100K cost, 10-year life). Rate = 2/10 = 20%.

  • Year 1: 20% x $100,000 = $20,000 (Book value: $80,000)
  • Year 2: 20% x $80,000 = $16,000 (Book value: $64,000)
  • Year 3: 20% x $64,000 = $12,800 (Book value: $51,200)

Notice how expense decreases each year. Companies often use straight-line for GAAP reporting and accelerated methods for tax returns, creating deferred tax liabilities.

Amortization

Amortization is the same concept as depreciation but applies to intangible assets. Intangibles with a finite useful life (patents, customer lists, acquired technology) are amortized over that life, typically using straight-line. Intangibles with an indefinite life (goodwill, certain trademarks) are NOT amortized; instead, they are tested for impairment annually.

Goodwill

Goodwill arises only from acquisitions. It represents the excess purchase price over the fair market value of the target's identifiable net assets. It captures intangible value like brand reputation, customer relationships, and synergies that cannot be separately identified.

Goodwill Calculation
Goodwill = Purchase Price - Fair Value of Net Identifiable Assets

Example: Acquirer pays $800M for a target whose assets are worth $600M and liabilities are $200M.
Net identifiable assets = $600M - $200M = $400M
Goodwill = $800M - $400M = $400M

Goodwill is not amortized under US GAAP. Instead, it is tested for impairment at least annually. If the fair value of the reporting unit falls below its carrying value (including goodwill), the goodwill is written down -- an impairment charge that hits the income statement as a non-cash expense.

Deferred Revenue

Deferred revenue (unearned revenue) is a liability that arises when a company receives payment before delivering a good or service. It is very common in subscription-based and software businesses.

  • When cash is received: Cash increases (asset), Deferred Revenue increases (liability). No revenue on the income statement.
  • When the service is delivered: Deferred Revenue decreases (liability), Revenue increases (income statement).

Deferred revenue is a source of cash on the cash flow statement. An increase in deferred revenue means the company collected more cash than it recognized as revenue -- favorable for cash flow.

Deferred Taxes

Deferred taxes arise when there is a timing difference between book accounting (GAAP) and tax accounting (IRS rules). The total tax paid over the asset's life is the same, but the timing differs.

Deferred Tax Liability (DTL)

Arises when book tax expense EXCEEDS cash taxes paid in the current period. The company "owes" taxes in the future.

Most common cause: Accelerated depreciation for taxes (higher depreciation reduces taxable income now) but straight-line for books (lower depreciation means higher book income now). In future years, the situation reverses.

Deferred Tax Asset (DTA)

Arises when cash taxes paid EXCEED book tax expense in the current period. The company has "prepaid" taxes.

Most common cause: Net Operating Losses (NOLs). If a company has losses, it can carry them forward to reduce future taxable income. The potential tax savings are recorded as a DTA.

Example: Deferred Tax Liability from Depreciation

A company buys a $100K machine. For GAAP (books): straight-line over 10 years = $10K/year. For taxes: 5-year MACRS, Year 1 expense = $20K. Tax rate = 25%.

  • Book depreciation: $10K. Tax depreciation: $20K. Difference: $10K.
  • Tax savings now: $10K x 25% = $2,500 (the company pays $2,500 less in cash taxes than book tax expense).
  • This $2,500 is recorded as a Deferred Tax Liability -- the company will pay this $2,500 in extra taxes in the future when the situation reverses (book depreciation exceeds tax depreciation in later years).
Pro Tip: Why Deferred Revenue Matters in Tech M&A
When a company acquires a SaaS business, the acquirer must "step up" the deferred revenue to its fair value, which is typically lower than its book value (because fair value only includes the cost to fulfill the obligation, not the profit margin). This means the acquirer will recognize LESS revenue from these pre-acquisition contracts, creating a "deferred revenue haircut" that depresses reported revenue in the quarters following the acquisition. This is a common adjustment analysts make when evaluating post-acquisition performance.

Depreciation applies to tangible assets; amortization applies to intangible assets
Depreciation is a cash expense; amortization is non-cash
Depreciation is tax-deductible; amortization is not
Depreciation uses declining balance; amortization uses straight-line
Depreciation applies to tangible fixed assets (PP&E: buildings, machinery, equipment), while amortization applies to intangible assets (patents, customer lists, acquired technology). Both are non-cash expenses, both are tax-deductible, and both can use various methods (though straight-line is most common for amortization).

3. A company uses straight-line depreciation for books and accelerated depreciation for taxes. In the early years, this creates:

A Deferred Tax Asset (DTA)
A Deferred Tax Liability (DTL)
No deferred tax impact
Higher cash taxes than book taxes
Accelerated depreciation for taxes means higher tax deductions now, so the company pays LESS in cash taxes than its book tax expense suggests. The company is deferring taxes to the future = a Deferred Tax Liability. In later years, when book depreciation exceeds tax depreciation, the DTL reverses and the company pays the deferred amount.
