What Investment Bankers Actually Do

Investment bankers serve as financial intermediaries who help companies, governments, and institutions raise capital and execute transactions. They are the deal-makers of the financial world. Understanding their role is the foundation of everything else in this module.

The Typical M&A Deal Process (Sell-Side)

A sell-side M&A process is the most common and instructive deal type to understand. Here is how a typical "broad auction" unfolds over 4-6 months:

1

Engagement & Preparation (Weeks 1-4)

The bank is hired (often after a "bake-off" where multiple banks pitch). The team prepares a Confidential Information Memorandum (CIM) describing the company, financial model projections, and management presentation. A buyer list is assembled and tiered by strategic fit and likelihood of closing.

2

Marketing & First Round (Weeks 4-8)

Potential buyers sign NDAs and receive the "teaser" (1-page anonymous summary) then the full CIM. Interested parties submit Indications of Interest (IOIs) with preliminary valuation ranges. The bank shortlists the most credible and highest-value bidders for the second round.

3

Due Diligence & Second Round (Weeks 8-16)

Shortlisted bidders access a virtual data room (VDR) with detailed financial, legal, and operational data. Management presentations are held. Bidders submit final bids (Letters of Intent or LOIs) with a specific purchase price, deal structure, financing details, and a markup of the purchase agreement.

4

Negotiation & Signing (Weeks 16-20)

The bank selects the winning bidder and negotiates the final purchase agreement, including reps & warranties, indemnities, escrow provisions, and closing conditions. Once signed, the deal is announced publicly.

5

Closing (Weeks 20-28)

Regulatory approvals (antitrust, CFIUS if applicable) are obtained. Shareholder votes if required. Financing is finalized. Funds transfer, and the deal closes. Fees are paid.

Product Groups vs. Industry Groups

Industry Groups (Coverage)

Organized by sector: Technology, Media & Telecom (TMT), Healthcare, Financial Institutions Group (FIG), Industrials, Consumer & Retail, Energy, Real Estate. They "cover" clients in that sector, maintain relationships, and pitch deals. They are the face of the bank to the client.

Advantage: You become an expert in an industry and build deep relationships. Better for long-term client development and for exit opportunities into that industry.

Product Groups (Execution)

Organized by deal type: M&A, Leveraged Finance, Equity Capital Markets (ECM), Debt Capital Markets (DCM), Restructuring. They provide execution expertise across all sectors. When a TMT client wants to do an M&A deal, the TMT coverage team brings in the M&A product group.

Advantage: You become an expert in a specific transaction type. M&A and LevFin groups tend to have the best technical modeling experience and the strongest exits into PE.

Career Tip

For PE exits, M&A and Leveraged Finance product groups are most valued because they give you direct experience with the types of analysis PE firms perform daily. Industry group analysts with strong deal flow can also exit well, especially into sector-focused funds.

DCF Valuation (Discounted Cash Flow)

The Discounted Cash Flow analysis is the cornerstone of intrinsic valuation. It values a company based on the present value of its expected future cash flows. Unlike relative valuation (comps), a DCF derives value from fundamentals rather than market pricing, making it theoretically the "purest" valuation method.

Core Principle

A dollar today is worth more than a dollar tomorrow. The DCF discounts all future cash flows back to the present at a rate that reflects the riskiness of those cash flows (the Weighted Average Cost of Capital, or WACC). The sum of those discounted cash flows equals the enterprise value of the business.

Step 1: Project Unlevered Free Cash Flow (UFCF)

Unlevered Free Cash Flow represents the cash available to ALL capital providers (both debt and equity holders) before any financing costs. This is critical because we want to value the entire enterprise first, then back into equity value later.

Unlevered Free Cash Flow (UFCF)
UFCF = EBIT x (1 - Tax Rate)
     + Depreciation & Amortization
     - Capital Expenditures (CapEx)
     - Change in Net Working Capital (ΔNWC)

Let's break down each component:

  • EBIT (Earnings Before Interest & Taxes): Also called Operating Income. This is revenue minus COGS minus operating expenses. We use EBIT, not Net Income, because we want pre-financing cash flows.
  • EBIT x (1 - t): This gives us NOPAT (Net Operating Profit After Tax). We tax EBIT directly because we assume a hypothetical scenario where the company has no debt (hence no interest tax shield at this stage). The tax shield is captured in WACC.
  • + D&A: Depreciation and amortization are non-cash charges. They reduced EBIT but no cash actually left the company, so we add them back.
  • - CapEx: Capital expenditures are actual cash outflows for purchasing/maintaining fixed assets. This is real cash leaving the business.
  • - ΔNWC: Changes in net working capital. If a company grows, it typically needs more working capital (more inventory, more receivables, partially offset by more payables). An increase in NWC is a cash outflow.
Net Working Capital
NWC = Current Operating Assets - Current Operating Liabilities
    = (Accounts Receivable + Inventory + Prepaid Expenses)
      - (Accounts Payable + Accrued Expenses)
Common Mistake

Do NOT include cash or short-term debt in working capital for DCF purposes. These are financing items, not operating items. Only include operating current assets and operating current liabilities.

To project UFCF, you need to forecast 5-10 years of financial statements. Key assumptions include:

  • Revenue growth: Based on market research, historical trends, management guidance, and industry benchmarks.
  • Operating margins: Will margins expand, contract, or stay flat? Consider economies of scale, competitive dynamics, and input costs.
  • CapEx as % of revenue: Maintenance CapEx (just to sustain operations) vs. growth CapEx (new capacity). In terminal value, only maintenance CapEx should be assumed.
  • Working capital as % of revenue: Typically modeled as days sales outstanding (DSO), days inventory outstanding (DIO), and days payable outstanding (DPO).

Step 2: Calculate WACC (Weighted Average Cost of Capital)

WACC represents the blended cost of all the company's capital (debt and equity), weighted by their proportion in the capital structure. It is the discount rate used to bring future cash flows to present value.

Weighted Average Cost of Capital
WACC = (E/V) x Re + (D/V) x Rd x (1 - t)

Where:
E = Market value of equity    D = Market value of debt
V = E + D (total capital)      Re = Cost of equity
Rd = Cost of debt             t = Corporate tax rate

Cost of Equity: The CAPM

The Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM) is the standard approach for estimating the cost of equity.

CAPM (Cost of Equity)
Re = Rf + Beta x (Rm - Rf)

Where:
Rf = Risk-free rate (10-year Treasury yield, typically ~4-5%)
Beta = Levered beta of the company (measures systematic risk)
Rm - Rf = Equity risk premium (ERP, historically ~5-7%)

Risk-Free Rate (Rf): Use the 10-year U.S. Treasury yield as a proxy. For longer-duration assets, some analysts use the 20-year or 30-year yield.

Equity Risk Premium (ERP): The incremental return investors demand for holding equities over risk-free bonds. The Duff & Phelps recommended ERP and academic estimates typically range from 5.0% to 7.0%. Many bankers use 6.0%.

Beta: Beta measures a stock's sensitivity to overall market movements. A beta of 1.0 means the stock moves in line with the market. Higher beta = more volatile = higher cost of equity. If the company is private, you use comparable company betas.

Unlevering and Relevering Beta (Hamada Equation)
Unlevered Beta = Levered Beta / [1 + (1 - t) x (D/E)]

Relevered Beta = Unlevered Beta x [1 + (1 - t) x (D/E)]

When using comparable companies, you unlever each comp's beta to remove the effect of their capital structure, take the median unlevered beta, then relever it using the target company's capital structure.

Cost of Debt (Rd)

The cost of debt is the interest rate the company pays on its borrowings. For publicly traded debt, you can observe the yield-to-maturity on the company's outstanding bonds. For private companies, use comparable bond yields for the same credit rating. Note: the cost of debt in WACC is the after-tax cost because interest payments are tax-deductible.

Capital Structure Weights

Use the target or optimal capital structure, not necessarily the current one. For public companies, equity value = share price x shares outstanding. Debt value = book value of total debt (often a reasonable proxy for market value unless the company is distressed).

Practical Tip

WACC for most mature companies in developed markets falls in the 8-12% range. If you get a WACC below 6% or above 15%, double-check your inputs. Technology/high-growth companies tend toward the higher end; utilities and regulated industries toward the lower end.

Step 3: Terminal Value

You cannot project cash flows forever, so after your explicit forecast period (usually 5-10 years), you calculate a terminal value (TV) to capture the company's value from that point to perpetuity. Terminal value typically represents 60-80% of a company's total DCF value, making it the single most important and sensitive assumption.

Method 1: Gordon Growth Model (Perpetuity Growth)

Terminal Value (GGM)
TV = FCF(n) x (1 + g) / (WACC - g)

Where:
FCF(n) = Final year projected FCF
g = Perpetuity growth rate (typically 2-3%)
WACC = Weighted avg cost of capital

When to use: This is the theoretically "cleaner" method. The growth rate should approximate long-term GDP growth or inflation (2-3%). Using a growth rate above 4% is generally not defensible for a perpetuity assumption.

Critical Check

The terminal growth rate (g) MUST be less than WACC; otherwise the formula produces a negative or infinite value. Also, g should not exceed long-term nominal GDP growth, since no company can grow faster than the economy forever.

Method 2: Exit Multiple

Terminal Value (Exit Multiple)
TV = Final Year EBITDA x Exit Multiple

Where:
Exit Multiple = EV/EBITDA multiple at exit
(derived from comparable companies)

When to use: This is more practical and commonly used in banking. The exit multiple is typically based on the current trading multiples of comparable companies. It implicitly embeds growth and risk assumptions from the market.

Best Practice

Run both methods and cross-check them. If the Gordon Growth method at a 2.5% terminal growth rate implies a 12x exit multiple, but the comparable companies trade at 8x, something is off in your assumptions. The two methods should converge.

Step 4: Discount Back to Present Value

Once you have your projected UFCF and terminal value, discount everything back to today. The discount factor for each year is:

Present Value Calculation
PV of FCF(t) = FCF(t) / (1 + WACC)^t

PV of Terminal Value = TV / (1 + WACC)^n

Enterprise Value = Sum of PV of FCFs + PV of Terminal Value

Use mid-year convention if cash flows are assumed to arrive evenly throughout the year (multiply discount factor by (1 + WACC)^0.5). This gives a slightly higher valuation than year-end discounting because it assumes cash arrives at mid-year on average, rather than all at year-end.

Step 5: Enterprise Value to Equity Value Bridge

The DCF gives you Enterprise Value (EV). To get to Equity Value (what equity shareholders own), you need to perform the equity bridge:

EV to Equity Value Bridge
Equity Value = Enterprise Value
    - Total Debt
    - Preferred Stock
    - Minority Interest (Noncontrolling Interest)
    + Cash & Cash Equivalents
    - Unfunded Pension Obligations (if material)
    - Capital Leases (if not already in debt)

Implied Share Price = Equity Value / Fully Diluted Shares Outstanding
Fully Diluted Shares

Use the Treasury Stock Method to calculate diluted shares. For each tranche of options/warrants that are "in the money" (exercise price < current share price): Diluted Shares = Basic Shares + In-the-Money Options - Shares Repurchased with Proceeds. Shares repurchased = (Options x Exercise Price) / Current Share Price.

Comparable Company Analysis (Trading Comps)

Comparable Company Analysis (commonly called "trading comps") is a relative valuation method. Instead of building a cash flow model from scratch, you look at what the market is currently paying for similar companies and apply those multiples to your target. It answers the question: "Based on how similar companies are valued by the public markets right now, what should this company be worth?"

Step 1: Select the Comparable Universe

This is arguably the most important step. A bad comp set leads to a misleading valuation. Screen for companies that are similar on these dimensions:

  • Industry & Business Model: Same sector, similar products/services, comparable customers
  • Size: Revenue range, market cap range (a $500M revenue company shouldn't be comped against a $50B one without adjustment)
  • Growth Profile: Similar revenue growth rates. High-growth companies trade at much higher multiples than low-growth ones.
  • Margin Profile: Companies with structurally higher margins deserve higher multiples
  • Geography: Same markets, similar regulatory environments
  • Capital Structure: Comparable leverage levels (for equity multiples)

Typically aim for 5-10 companies. Too few gives a noisy result; too many waters down the quality.

Step 2: Gather Financial Data & Calendarize

For each comparable company, gather LTM (Last Twelve Months) and NTM (Next Twelve Months) financial data from public filings, research estimates (consensus from FactSet, Bloomberg, Capital IQ). If companies have different fiscal year ends, calendarize them to the same period (e.g., if one FY ends in March and another in December, create a pro-rata LTM figure).

Adjustments to Make

Before calculating multiples, adjust for non-recurring items: one-time restructuring charges, litigation settlements, asset write-downs, gains/losses on asset sales. You want "clean" operating metrics. Also adjust for stock-based compensation if comparing GAAP vs. non-GAAP metrics (banks increasingly present SBC-adjusted figures).

Step 3: Calculate Multiples

MultipleFormulaWhen to UseProsCons
EV / RevenueEnterprise Value / RevenueHigh-growth, unprofitable companies (SaaS, biotech)Works even when EBITDA is negativeIgnores profitability entirely
EV / EBITDAEnterprise Value / EBITDAMost common; works for most industriesCapital structure neutral, pre-D&ACan be distorted by D&A differences

Key Rule

Enterprise Value multiples (EV/Revenue, EV/EBITDA, EV/EBIT) must use Enterprise Value in the numerator because the denominators (Revenue, EBITDA, EBIT) are pre-debt metrics available to all capital providers. Equity Value multiples (P/E, P/B) use Equity Value because Net Income and Book Value are after-debt metrics belonging to equity holders only. Never mix them (e.g., never divide market cap by EBITDA).

Step 4: Calculate Implied Valuation

Take the median (or mean) multiple from your comp set and apply it to the target company's corresponding metric to get an implied enterprise value, then bridge to equity value.

Precedent Transactions Analysis

Precedent transactions analysis (also called "transaction comps" or "deal comps") values a company by looking at the prices paid in historical M&A transactions for similar companies. Unlike trading comps, these multiples reflect actual acquisition prices, which include a control premium.

How It Differs from Trading Comps

FactorTrading CompsPrecedent Transactions
Based onCurrent market prices (minority interests)Actual deal prices (controlling interests)
Control PremiumNot includedIncluded (typically 20-40% over unaffected price)
SynergiesNot includedPartially baked into the price the buyer paid
TimingReal-time snapshotHistorical; may reflect different market conditions
AvailabilityAlways available for public companiesLimited by disclosure; private transactions may not be fully reported
ResultValue on a minority, non-control basisValue on a controlling interest basis

The Control Premium Concept

Control Premium

When an acquirer buys a company, they gain control: the ability to set strategy, allocate capital, realize synergies, and make operational changes. This control is valuable, so acquirers pay a premium above the current trading price. Control premiums typically range from 20% to 40% of the unaffected share price (the price before any acquisition rumors). In hot markets or competitive processes, premiums can exceed 50%.

Finding & Selecting Relevant Transactions

  • Use databases: Capital IQ, Bloomberg, MergerMarket, PitchBook, Thomson Reuters
  • Filter by: same industry, similar target size, transaction type (strategic vs. financial), geography
  • Time decay of relevance: More recent transactions are more relevant. Deals older than 3-5 years may reflect very different market conditions (interest rates, multiples, industry dynamics). Weight recent deals more heavily.
  • Aim for 5-10 comparable transactions
  • Note the deal context: Was it competitive (higher premium)? Hostile? Distressed? These factors affect the multiples.

Premiums Paid Analysis

In addition to multiples, banks perform a "premiums paid" analysis: what premium over the unaffected share price did acquirers pay in comparable transactions? If the median premium is 30%, and TechCorp's unaffected price is $12.00, the implied acquisition price would be $12.00 x 1.30 = $15.60. This is a quick sanity check independent of multiples.

LBO Model (Leveraged Buyout)

A Leveraged Buyout is the acquisition of a company using a significant amount of debt to fund the purchase price. Private equity firms are the primary users of LBOs. The fundamental idea: buy a company with mostly borrowed money, use the company's cash flows to pay down that debt over 3-7 years, then sell the company at a similar or higher multiple. The equity investors' returns are amplified by the leverage.

Three Levers of LBO Returns
  1. EBITDA Growth: Growing the earnings of the business through revenue growth, margin improvement, or both.
  2. Multiple Expansion: Selling at a higher EV/EBITDA multiple than you bought at (e.g., buy at 8x, sell at 10x). This is risky to underwrite but common in practice.
  3. Debt Paydown (De-leveraging): Using free cash flow to pay down debt, which increases the equity portion of the capital structure. At exit, more of the enterprise value flows to equity holders.

Sources & Uses of Funds

Every LBO starts with a sources & uses table. Sources (where the money comes from) must exactly equal Uses (where the money goes).

Sources (Where Money Comes From)

  • Senior Secured Debt (Term Loan): Lowest interest rate (SOFR + 200-400bps), first lien on assets, typically 3-5x EBITDA
  • Senior Unsecured / Second Lien: Higher rate, subordinated to first lien
  • Subordinated Debt (Mezzanine): 12-20% interest, may include equity warrants (PIK toggles), sits below senior in the capital stack
  • Revolving Credit Facility: Like a corporate credit card, drawn as needed for working capital
  • Sponsor Equity: The PE firm's contribution, typically 30-50% of the purchase price
  • Management Rollover: Existing management reinvesting some equity in the deal

Uses (Where Money Goes)

  • Purchase Enterprise Value: The agreed price for the company
  • Refinance Existing Debt: Pay off the target's current debt
  • Transaction Fees: Advisory fees, financing fees, legal costs (typically 2-4% of EV)
  • Cash to Balance Sheet: Minimum cash needed to run the business

The LBO Operating Model & Debt Schedule

The core of the LBO model is projecting the company's operations forward and tracking how much debt gets paid down each year:

  1. Build the operating model: Project revenue, EBITDA, CapEx, working capital changes, and Free Cash Flow for the hold period (typically 5 years).
  2. Build the debt schedule: Start with the initial debt load. Each year, calculate interest expense for each tranche, apply mandatory amortization, then use remaining free cash flow for optional debt paydown (cash sweep). Track remaining debt balances.
  3. Calculate exit value: Apply an exit multiple to the final year's EBITDA to get exit EV. Subtract remaining debt to get exit equity value.
  4. Calculate returns: IRR and MOIC on the sponsor's initial equity investment.
Return Metrics
MOIC (Multiple on Invested Capital) = Exit Equity / Entry Equity

IRR (Internal Rate of Return) = (MOIC)^(1/years) - 1   [simplified]

PE firms target: 20%+ IRR and 2.5x+ MOIC over a 5-year hold

Paper LBO: Practice Problem (click to expand) ▶

Scenario: A PE firm is acquiring WidgetCo for $800M (8.0x LTM EBITDA of $100M). The deal is financed with 50% debt (4.0x) at 6% interest and 50% equity ($400M). EBITDA grows 5% annually. Solve:

  1. Year 5 EBITDA: $100M x (1.05)^5 = $127.6M
  2. Annual FCF (after interest): Year 1: EBITDA - CapEx - Interest = $100 x 1.05 - $20 - ($400 x 0.06) = $105 - $20 - $24 = $61M. Each subsequent year, EBITDA grows so FCF grows, interest declines as debt is repaid.
  3. Quick approximation: Assume ~$75M average annual FCF applied to debt paydown. Over 5 years, ~$375M of debt is repaid. Remaining debt: $400M - $375M = ~$25M.
  4. Exit EV: $127.6M x 8.0 = $1,021M
  5. Exit Equity: $1,021M - $25M = ~$996M
  6. MOIC: $996M / $400M = ~2.5x
  7. IRR: (2.5)^(1/5) - 1 = ~20%

This is the level of mental math expected in PE interviews. Practice until you can do this in under 5 minutes on paper.

Key LBO Risk

The biggest risk in an LBO is over-leveraging. If the business underperforms and cannot service its debt, it faces covenant violations, restructuring, or bankruptcy. PE firms must balance using enough leverage to amplify returns while maintaining a margin of safety on debt coverage ratios.

M&A Analysis (Merger Model)

A merger model (also called an accretion/dilution model) analyzes what happens to the acquirer's Earnings Per Share (EPS) when it acquires a target company. The core question: will this deal be accretive (increase EPS) or dilutive (decrease EPS) to the acquirer?

Accretion / Dilution Analysis: Step by Step

1

Start with the acquirer's standalone EPS

Project the acquirer's net income and shares outstanding. Calculate standalone EPS = Net Income / Diluted Shares.

2

Add the target's Net Income

Add the target's projected net income to the acquirer's. This represents the combined earnings.

3

Add synergies (after-tax)

Apply estimated cost synergies (e.g., headcount reduction, facility consolidation) and/or revenue synergies (cross-selling). Use after-tax amounts since we're measuring impact on net income.

4

Subtract incremental interest expense / add foregone interest income

If the deal is financed with debt, subtract the after-tax interest cost of new borrowings. If financed with cash, subtract the after-tax income lost from that cash.

5

Add new shares issued (if stock deal)

If paying with stock, calculate new shares issued: Purchase Price / Acquirer's Share Price. Add these to the share count.

6

Calculate Pro Forma EPS

Pro Forma EPS = Combined Adjusted Net Income / New Diluted Share Count. If Pro Forma EPS > Standalone EPS, the deal is accretive. If lower, it is dilutive.

Sources of Value in M&A

Cost Synergies (More Certain)

  • Eliminate duplicate corporate functions (HR, finance, IT)
  • Consolidate facilities and real estate
  • Renegotiate supplier contracts with greater purchasing power
  • Reduce overlapping sales teams
  • Typically realized within 1-3 years
  • Usually 75-100% credibility in banker estimates

Revenue Synergies (Less Certain)

  • Cross-selling products to each other's customer bases
  • Entering new geographies using the other's distribution
  • Bundling products for higher pricing power
  • Accessing new customer segments
  • Takes 2-5 years to fully realize (if ever)
  • Usually 25-50% credibility; banks discount these heavily

Purchase Price Allocation & Goodwill

When an acquirer pays more than the target's book value of net assets (which is almost always the case), the excess must be allocated under accounting rules:

  1. First, write up identifiable assets to fair value (real estate, customer relationships, patents, technology, brand names). These create identifiable intangible assets that are amortized over their useful lives.
  2. The remaining excess = Goodwill. Goodwill is not amortized but is tested for impairment annually. If the acquired business underperforms, goodwill is written down (a non-cash charge that hits the income statement).
Goodwill Calculation
Goodwill = Purchase Price - Fair Value of Net Identifiable Assets

Example: Pay $1,000M for a company with $300M net assets (at fair value)
Goodwill = $1,000M - $300M = $700M

Worked Example: MegaCorp Acquires TechCorp

Merger Model: Accretion / Dilution Analysis

Acquirer (MegaCorp): Net Income = $600M, Shares = 200M, EPS = $3.00, Share Price = $45

Target (TechCorp): Net Income = $62.5M, Shares = 100M, Offer = $20/share (Equity Value = $2,000M)

Deal: 50% cash / 50% stock. Cash portion ($1,000M) financed with new debt at 5% pre-tax. Tax rate = 25%. Cost synergies = $40M pre-tax (achieved Year 1).

Calculation:

  • MegaCorp Standalone Net Income: $600.0M
  • (+) TechCorp Net Income: $62.5M
  • (+) After-tax Synergies: $40M x (1 - 0.25) = $30.0M
  • (-) After-tax Interest on New Debt: $1,000M x 5% x (1 - 0.25) = ($37.5M)
  • Combined Net Income: $655.0M
  • New Shares Issued: $1,000M / $45 = 22.2M shares
  • Pro Forma Shares: 200M + 22.2M = 222.2M
  • Pro Forma EPS: $655.0M / 222.2M = $2.95

Standalone EPS: $3.00 vs. Pro Forma EPS: $2.95

The deal is dilutive by $0.05 (1.7%) in Year 1. However, as synergies are further realized and TechCorp grows, it may become accretive in Year 2+. A small amount of dilution (under 5%) is generally acceptable if strategic rationale is strong.

Quick Rule of Thumb

In an all-stock deal, if the target's P/E ratio is lower than the acquirer's P/E, the deal will be accretive (you're buying "cheap" earnings). If the target's P/E is higher, it will be dilutive. In an all-cash deal, if the target's earnings yield (1/P*E, or after-tax) exceeds the after-tax cost of debt, it's accretive.

Pitch Books

A pitch book is a presentation that investment bankers create to win mandates (get hired for a deal) or to present analysis to clients. Creating pitch books consumes a significant portion of an analyst's time. Understanding pitch book structure is essential.

Types of Pitch Books

  • General Credentials Pitch ("Credentials Deck"): Showcases the bank's capabilities, relevant deal experience, and team. Used when meeting a potential client for the first time or pitching for a mandate.
  • Deal-Specific Pitch: Tailored for a specific transaction (e.g., "Why you should sell your company now" or "Acquisition targets you should consider"). Contains detailed valuation analysis and strategic rationale.
  • Market Update: Periodic update on market conditions, deal activity, and valuation trends in the client's sector. Used to maintain the relationship and demonstrate expertise.
  • Discussion Materials: Presented during an active deal to guide the client's decision-making (e.g., bid comparison, negotiation strategy).

IPO Process

An Initial Public Offering (IPO) is the process of offering shares of a private company to the public for the first time on a stock exchange. The investment bank serves as the underwriter, managing the entire process from pricing to allocation.

IPO Process Timeline

1

Hiring Underwriters (6-12 months before)

The company selects a lead underwriter (bookrunner) and co-managers. The bookrunner runs the process, builds the order book, and takes the largest share of fees. Multiple banks may form a syndicate, especially for large IPOs. Banks compete in "bake-offs" by presenting their vision for the IPO, comparable company analysis, and their distribution capabilities.

2

Due Diligence & Registration (3-6 months before)

The company, bankers, and lawyers prepare the S-1 Registration Statement filed with the SEC. This includes the company's financials, risk factors, management discussion, and use of proceeds. The SEC reviews and provides comments; the company responds. This can go through multiple rounds.

3

Roadshow (2-3 weeks before pricing)

Company management and bankers travel to meet institutional investors (mutual funds, hedge funds, pension funds) in major financial centers. They present the investment thesis, answer questions, and gauge demand. The roadshow typically lasts 1-2 weeks with dozens of meetings.

4

Book Building & Pricing

During the roadshow, the bookrunner collects indications of interest (IOIs) from investors: how many shares they want and at what price. The initial filing includes a price range (e.g., $18-$22). Based on demand, the range may be narrowed, raised, or lowered. The final price is set the night before trading begins, typically in a meeting between the company and the bookrunner.

5

Allocation & First Day of Trading

Shares are allocated to institutional investors (the bookrunner decides who gets how much). On the first trading day, the stock opens on the exchange. A typical "pop" of 10-20% on the first day is expected; too much pop means the company left money on the table; no pop or a decline indicates poor pricing.

Key IPO Concepts

The Greenshoe Option (Over-Allotment)

The underwriter has the option to sell up to 15% more shares than the original offering size. If the stock trades above the IPO price, the underwriter exercises the greenshoe and sells extra shares (buying them from the company), earning additional fees. If the stock drops below the IPO price, the underwriter buys shares in the open market to support the price, using the greenshoe proceeds. This is a stabilization mechanism.

Lock-Up Period

Insiders (founders, employees, pre-IPO investors) are typically prohibited from selling shares for 90-180 days after the IPO. This prevents a flood of selling that could crash the stock price. The lock-up expiration date is an important event -- the stock often dips as insiders sell.

The Syndicate

Large IPOs involve multiple banks: the Lead Bookrunner (largest share of fees, runs the process), Joint Bookrunners (share in bookbuilding and allocation), Co-Managers (smaller role, help distribute shares to their investor clients). Fee hierarchy: Lead gets ~40-60% of the gross spread; others share the rest.

Restructuring Basics

Restructuring investment banking involves advising companies in financial distress or their creditors. When a company cannot meet its debt obligations, it needs to restructure its balance sheet. This is the counter-cyclical side of IB -- restructuring groups are busiest during economic downturns.

When Companies Need Restructuring

  • Revenue decline leading to inability to service debt
  • Overleveraged from a previous LBO or acquisition
  • Industry disruption (technological change, regulatory shifts)
  • Impending debt maturity with no ability to refinance ("maturity wall")
  • Covenant violations triggering default provisions
  • Liquidity crisis (running out of cash)

Chapter 11 Bankruptcy Basics

Chapter 11 is a section of the U.S. Bankruptcy Code that allows a company to reorganize while continuing to operate. Key features:

  • Automatic Stay: All creditor collection actions are frozen immediately upon filing. Creditors cannot seize assets or demand payment. This gives the company breathing room.
  • Debtor-in-Possession (DIP): Management continues to run the company (unlike Chapter 7 liquidation). The company can enter into new contracts, pay employees, and operate normally.
  • Plan of Reorganization: The company proposes a plan to restructure its debts (reduce principal, extend maturities, convert debt to equity). Creditors vote on the plan by class. The court confirms the plan if it meets legal requirements.
  • 363 Sales: The company can sell assets (or the entire business) through a court-supervised auction under Section 363, free and clear of liens and encumbrances.

DIP Financing

Debtor-in-Possession Financing

Companies in Chapter 11 often need new financing to continue operations during the bankruptcy process. DIP loans receive "super-priority" status, meaning they are repaid before all other claims (even existing senior secured debt). Because of this super-priority, DIP loans typically carry high interest rates but are relatively safe for the lender. DIP financing is critical because without it, the company would run out of cash and be forced into Chapter 7 liquidation.

Creditor Hierarchy (Priority of Claims)

In any restructuring or bankruptcy, the order in which creditors get paid is critical:

The Fulcrum Security

Fulcrum Security Concept

The fulcrum security is the most junior class of debt (or equity) that is expected to receive some (but not full) recovery in a restructuring. Classes above the fulcrum receive full recovery; classes below receive nothing. Distressed debt investors target the fulcrum security because its holders often receive equity in the reorganized company and thus control the new entity.

Example: Company has $500M enterprise value, $400M first lien debt, $200M second lien debt, and $100M equity. First lien holders are fully covered ($400M < $500M). Second lien holders receive the remaining $100M of value (50 cents on the dollar). Equity holders get nothing. The second lien debt is the fulcrum security.

IB-Specific Technical Questions

These are the 20 most commonly asked technical questions in investment banking interviews. Master all of them.

1. Walk me through a DCF. ▶

A DCF values a company based on the present value of its future free cash flows. First, I project the company's Unlevered Free Cash Flow (EBIT x (1-t) + D&A - CapEx - change in NWC) for 5-10 years. Second, I calculate the WACC using the CAPM for cost of equity (risk-free rate + beta x equity risk premium) blended with the after-tax cost of debt, weighted by the target capital structure. Third, I calculate a terminal value using either the Gordon Growth Model (final year FCF x (1+g) / (WACC - g)) or an exit multiple on terminal EBITDA. Fourth, I discount all projected FCFs and the terminal value back to the present at WACC to get Enterprise Value. Finally, I subtract net debt (and other EV-to-equity bridge items) to get Equity Value, and divide by diluted shares for an implied share price.

2. What are the main valuation methodologies? ▶

The three primary methodologies are: (1) Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) -- an intrinsic valuation based on projected future cash flows discounted to present value. (2) Comparable Company Analysis (Trading Comps) -- a relative valuation based on what similar public companies are currently trading at. (3) Precedent Transactions Analysis -- a relative valuation based on what acquirers have paid for similar companies in past M&A deals. A fourth common methodology is the LBO Analysis, which backs into a valuation based on the price a financial buyer (PE firm) could pay while achieving their target returns.

3. When would you not use a DCF? ▶

A DCF is unreliable when: (1) The company has negative or highly unpredictable cash flows (e.g., early-stage startups, biotech companies pre-revenue). (2) The company is in financial distress and may not survive as a going concern. (3) There is too much uncertainty in projecting future performance (e.g., cyclical commodity companies). (4) The company is a bank or financial institution with a fundamentally different cash flow structure (use dividend discount model or residual income instead). In these cases, relative valuation or asset-based approaches may be more appropriate.

4. Walk me through the three financial statements. ▶

The Income Statement shows the company's revenue, expenses, and profitability over a period. It starts with Revenue and subtracts COGS (giving Gross Profit), Operating Expenses (giving Operating Income/EBIT), Interest and Taxes (giving Net Income). The Balance Sheet is a snapshot at a point in time showing Assets = Liabilities + Shareholders' Equity. Assets include cash, receivables, inventory, PP&E, and intangibles. Liabilities include payables, debt, and deferred revenue. Equity includes retained earnings and common stock. The Cash Flow Statement reconciles net income to actual cash movement in three sections: Operating (starts with NI, adjusts for non-cash items and working capital changes), Investing (CapEx, acquisitions, asset sales), and Financing (debt issuance/repayment, equity issuance/buybacks, dividends). The ending cash balance flows back to the balance sheet.

5. How do the three financial statements link together? ▶

Net Income from the Income Statement flows into Retained Earnings on the Balance Sheet and is the starting point for the Cash Flow Statement (CFO section). Depreciation from the Income Statement is added back in CFO (non-cash) and reduces PP&E on the Balance Sheet. CapEx appears in CFI and increases PP&E. Debt issuance/repayment in CFF changes the debt balance on the Balance Sheet. The net change in cash from all three CFS sections updates the Cash balance on the Balance Sheet. Changes in working capital items (AR, inventory, AP) appear in both CFO and the Balance Sheet.

6. What is Enterprise Value and how do you calculate it? ▶

Enterprise Value (EV) represents the total value of a company's operations, available to all capital providers (debt + equity). EV = Equity Value (Market Cap) + Total Debt + Preferred Stock + Minority Interest - Cash & Cash Equivalents. You add debt because an acquirer would assume it. You subtract cash because the acquirer effectively "gets" the cash on hand. You add minority interest because EV-based metrics (EBITDA, Revenue) include 100% of subsidiary revenues, so the EV must as well. Think of EV as the "takeover price" -- what you'd pay to acquire the entire business and control all its operations.

7. Why do we use Enterprise Value / EBITDA rather than Price / Earnings? ▶

EV/EBITDA is preferred because it is capital-structure neutral. EBITDA is a pre-debt, pre-tax, pre-depreciation metric, making it comparable across companies with different leverage, tax situations, and depreciation policies. P/E, by contrast, is affected by leverage (more debt = more interest = lower NI = different P/E) and by different tax rates and D&A policies. EV/EBITDA allows you to compare the core operating performance of companies on a more apples-to-apples basis.

8. If you could only use one valuation methodology, which would you choose and why? ▶

I would choose Comparable Company Analysis (trading comps). While the DCF is theoretically the most rigorous, it is highly sensitive to assumptions (WACC, terminal growth rate) that are difficult to estimate precisely. Trading comps reflect the collective wisdom of the market (what investors are actually willing to pay), provide a real-time snapshot, and are relatively straightforward to compute. They also tell you what a company "should" trade at given where peers are valued. That said, in practice, you should always use multiple methodologies and triangulate the results.

9. How do you calculate WACC? ▶

WACC = (E/V) x Re + (D/V) x Rd x (1-t). First, determine the capital structure weights using market value of equity (market cap) and market/book value of debt. Then calculate Re using CAPM: Risk-free rate + Levered Beta x Equity Risk Premium. Use the 10-year Treasury for Rf, comparable company betas (unlevered and re-levered), and a 5-7% ERP. For Rd, use the yield-to-maturity on the company's existing bonds or comparable credit. The (1-t) adjustment on debt reflects the tax shield from deductible interest payments.

10. What happens to each valuation methodology when interest rates increase? ▶

DCF: Higher rates increase WACC (both cost of equity via higher Rf and cost of debt), which increases the discount rate and decreases present values, leading to lower valuations. Trading Comps: Higher rates generally compress multiples as investors demand higher returns, lowering stock prices and thus lowering comp-derived valuations. Precedent Transactions: Historical deals done in a low-rate environment may overstate valuations; you should weight more recent deals more heavily. LBO: Higher borrowing costs mean PE firms can support less debt, leading to lower purchase prices for the same return hurdle.

11. A company has $100M in revenue, 20% EBITDA margin, and trades at 10x EV/EBITDA. What is its Enterprise Value? ▶

EBITDA = $100M x 20% = $20M. Enterprise Value = $20M x 10 = $200M.

12. What is the difference between Equity Value and Enterprise Value? ▶

Equity Value represents the value belonging only to equity shareholders (Market Cap = Share Price x Shares Outstanding). Enterprise Value represents the value of the entire business to all capital providers (debt + equity). EV = Equity Value + Net Debt + Preferred + Minority Interest. The key conceptual difference: if you were buying the whole company, EV is what you'd pay (you take on the debt and get the cash). Equity Value is just the equity holders' residual claim after debts are satisfied.

13. If depreciation increases by $10, how does that flow through the financial statements (assume 40% tax)? ▶

Income Statement: EBIT decreases by $10. Tax decreases by $4 (10 x 40%). Net Income decreases by $6. Cash Flow Statement: CFO starts with NI (down $6), adds back D&A (up $10), so CFO increases by $4. CFF and CFI are unchanged. Balance Sheet: PP&E decreases by $10 (accumulated depreciation increases). Cash increases by $4 (from CFO). Retained Earnings decreases by $6 (lower NI). Assets are down $6 net, and Equity is down $6, so the balance sheet balances.

14. Why do we add back Depreciation & Amortization in the DCF? ▶

D&A is a non-cash expense. It reduces EBIT on the income statement, but no actual cash leaves the company. Since we are measuring cash flows (not accounting profits), we add it back. The actual cash outflow for acquiring the fixed asset was already captured in CapEx (either historically or in the CapEx projection). D&A is merely the accounting allocation of that past CapEx over the asset's useful life.

15. What is the difference between levered and unlevered free cash flow? ▶

Unlevered Free Cash Flow (UFCF) is the cash flow available to ALL capital providers (debt + equity) before any debt service. It starts with EBIT x (1-t) and ignores interest payments. It is used in DCF to calculate Enterprise Value. Levered Free Cash Flow (LFCF) is the cash flow available only to equity holders after all debt obligations (interest and principal) are paid. It starts with Net Income. UFCF is discounted at WACC; LFCF is discounted at cost of equity. Most DCFs use UFCF because it separates the operating value of the business from its financing decisions.

16. Why might two companies in the same industry trade at different EV/EBITDA multiples? ▶

Differences in: (1) Growth rate -- higher growth commands higher multiples. (2) Margins -- better margins imply better quality and efficiency. (3) Market position -- market leader vs. niche player. (4) Recurring revenue -- subscription/contracted revenue is more predictable and valued higher. (5) Customer concentration -- reliance on few customers increases risk and lowers multiples. (6) Management quality. (7) Geographic mix. (8) Capital intensity -- less CapEx-intensive businesses trade at higher multiples. (9) Cyclicality -- more stable businesses get premium multiples.

17. Walk me through an LBO model. ▶

First, determine the purchase price and build the Sources & Uses table (how much debt and equity is needed). Second, build a 5-year operating model projecting EBITDA, CapEx, working capital, and free cash flow. Third, build the debt schedule: calculate interest expense for each tranche, apply mandatory amortization, and use remaining cash flow to pay down debt. Fourth, at the end of the hold period, calculate the exit enterprise value by applying an exit multiple to the final year EBITDA. Fifth, subtract remaining debt to get exit equity value. Finally, calculate returns: MOIC = exit equity / entry equity, and IRR = (MOIC)^(1/n) - 1. PE firms target 20%+ IRR and 2.5x+ MOIC.

18. What makes a good LBO candidate? ▶

Ideal LBO targets have: (1) Stable, predictable cash flows to service debt. (2) Strong EBITDA margins. (3) Low capital expenditure requirements (asset-light). (4) Market leadership or strong competitive position. (5) Opportunities for operational improvement (cost cutting, margin expansion). (6) Low cyclicality. (7) Strong management team willing to stay. (8) Tangible assets for collateral (helpful but not required). (9) Potential for add-on acquisitions ("buy and build" strategy). Think: boring, cash-generative businesses with improvement potential.

19. When is a deal accretive vs. dilutive? ▶

A deal is accretive when the pro forma EPS is higher than the acquirer's standalone EPS. It is dilutive when pro forma EPS is lower. In an all-stock deal, the quick test is: if the target's P/E is lower than the acquirer's P/E, the deal is accretive (you're buying cheaper earnings). In an all-cash deal, if the target's after-tax earnings yield exceeds the after-tax cost of debt, the deal is accretive. In a mixed deal, you must run the full accretion/dilution math. Note: accretion/dilution should not be the primary driver of deal decisions -- strategic rationale and value creation matter more.

20. Walk me through a merger model (accretion/dilution). ▶

Start with the acquirer's standalone net income and EPS. Add the target's net income. Add after-tax synergies (cost savings x (1-t)). Subtract the after-tax cost of financing: if cash deal, subtract after-tax interest on new debt used to fund the acquisition; if stock deal, note there's no interest cost but more shares outstanding. Calculate new shares issued (for stock consideration: equity purchase price / acquirer share price). Compute pro forma net income / pro forma diluted shares = pro forma EPS. Compare to standalone EPS. If higher, it's accretive; if lower, dilutive. Also account for amortization of acquired intangibles (reduces pro forma net income) and any transaction fees.
