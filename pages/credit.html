The Debt Markets Landscape

Credit is the largest asset class in the world — the US bond market alone is worth $50+ trillion, dwarfing the equity market. Understanding debt is essential for every finance role, not just credit analysts. IB analysts structure debt in deals. PE associates model it in LBOs. VC associates encounter it in venture debt. Consultants advise on capital structure.

Memory Hook — The Debt Stack (Top to Bottom)
Senior Secured → Senior Unsecured → Subordinated → Mezzanine → Equity

Think of it as a building: Senior Secured is the foundation (safest, first to be repaid), then each floor up is riskier and has higher yield. Equity is the penthouse — best view (highest upside) but first to get destroyed in a fire (bankruptcy). In liquidation, you get paid from the foundation up.

Credit Analysis Framework

Credit analysis answers one question: can this borrower repay its debt? While equity investors care about upside potential, credit investors care about downside protection. The mindset is fundamentally different.

The Credit Analyst's Core Question

"In the worst plausible scenario, can this company still make its interest payments and eventually repay principal?" If the answer is yes, it's a good credit. If no, walk away — no matter how cheap the yield looks.

Memory Hook — The 5 C's

Imagine you're lending your friend $10,000. You'd want to know: Is this person trustworthy? (Character) Can they afford the payments? (Capacity) Do they have savings as a buffer? (Capital) Can I take their car if they don't pay? (Collateral) Is their job stable? (Conditions). Credit analysis at a bank is exactly the same thing, just with more zeros.

Credit Ratings & Rating Agencies

The three major rating agencies — S&P Global, Moody's, and Fitch — assign credit ratings that serve as shorthand for default risk. These ratings directly impact borrowing costs and investor access.

Memory Hook — The BBB- Cliff

The single most important line in all of credit is BBB- / Baa3. This is the boundary between investment grade and high yield (junk). Many institutional investors (pension funds, insurance companies) can ONLY hold investment grade bonds. A downgrade from BBB- to BB+ ("falling angel") triggers forced selling by these investors, causing bond prices to plummet. This is why companies fight so hard to maintain their IG rating — losing it is catastrophic for their borrowing costs.

Covenants & Credit Agreements

Covenants are the rules written into a loan agreement that protect the lender. They restrict what the borrower can do and set performance thresholds. Understanding covenants is critical for credit analysis, LBO modeling, and restructuring.

Types of Covenants

Maintenance Covenants

Must be met at all times (tested quarterly). If breached, it triggers a default event.

  • Maximum Leverage: Total Debt / EBITDA must stay below X.x (e.g., 5.5x)
  • Minimum Interest Coverage: EBITDA / Interest must stay above X.x (e.g., 2.0x)
  • Minimum FCCR: (EBITDA - CapEx) / (Interest + Amort) above 1.1x

Found in: traditional bank loans, middle-market credit

Incurrence Covenants

Only tested when the borrower takes a specific action (like taking on more debt or paying a dividend).

  • Debt Incurrence Test: Can only raise more debt if pro forma leverage stays below X.x
  • Restricted Payments: Can only pay dividends if certain conditions are met
  • Asset Sale Proceeds: Must use proceeds to repay debt (or reinvest within a time frame)

Found in: high-yield bonds, covenant-lite leveraged loans

Covenant-Lite ("Cov-Lite") Loans

Since 2020, the majority (80%+) of new leveraged loans are "covenant-lite" — meaning they have incurrence covenants only, with no maintenance tests. This is great for borrowers (more flexibility) but risky for lenders (fewer early warning signals of distress). In a downturn, cov-lite loans mean problems are detected later, when there's less time and fewer options to fix them.

Memory Hook — Maintenance vs Incurrence

Maintenance covenants are like a speed limit — you must obey it at all times, and you get a ticket (default) if caught speeding. Incurrence covenants are like a weight limit on a bridge — only checked when you try to cross (take a new action). You can be overweight at home and nobody cares, but you can't cross the bridge.

The Debt Waterfall & Priority of Claims

When a company goes bankrupt, its assets are distributed to claimants in a strict priority order called the "waterfall." Understanding this waterfall is fundamental to credit analysis — it determines recovery rates for each tranche of debt.

Priority of Claims (Highest to Lowest)

1

Secured Creditors (First Lien)

Backed by specific collateral (assets, receivables, inventory). If the company defaults, these lenders can seize the collateral. Typical recovery: 70-90%.

2

Second Lien Creditors

Also secured, but with a subordinate claim on the same collateral. First lien gets paid first from collateral proceeds. Typical recovery: 30-60%.

3

Senior Unsecured Creditors

No specific collateral backing. Includes most corporate bonds. Paid from remaining assets after secured claims are satisfied. Typical recovery: 30-50%.

4

Subordinated / Mezzanine Debt

Contractually junior to senior debt. Often includes equity warrants as additional compensation for the higher risk. Typical recovery: 10-30%.

5

Preferred Equity

Senior to common equity but junior to all debt. Typical recovery in bankruptcy: 0-10%.

6

Common Equity

Last in line. Gets whatever (if anything) is left after all creditors are paid. Typical recovery in bankruptcy: 0-5%. Equity is often wiped out entirely.

Memory Hook — "First In Line, Last to Lose"

The debt waterfall follows one simple rule: the safer you are (higher priority), the less you earn (lower yield). The more risk you take (lower priority), the more you earn — but you might lose everything. This risk-return tradeoff is the single unifying principle of all credit markets. Senior secured debt has low yield but high recovery. Equity has infinite upside but gets wiped out first.

Direct Lending & Private Credit

Direct lending (also called private credit) is the fastest-growing segment of the credit market. AUM has grown from ~$400B in 2018 to over $1.7 trillion in 2025. Understanding this space is increasingly important because it's where many of the jobs are.

How Direct Lending Works

The Basic Model

Credit funds (like Ares, Owl Rock/Blue Owl, Golub, Monroe) raise capital from institutional investors (pensions, endowments, insurance companies) and lend directly to mid-market companies — typically PE-backed businesses doing LBOs. Unlike syndicated loans (arranged by banks and sold to many investors), a direct lender provides the entire loan from a single fund, offering speed and certainty of execution.

Memory Hook — Why Private Credit Is Booming

Three forces: (1) Banks retreated after 2008 regulations made lending less profitable for them, (2) PE deal volume exploded, creating massive demand for financing, (3) Investors want yield in a world where traditional bonds don't pay enough. Direct lending fills the gap — it's basically "PE for debt." Same LP/GP structure, same 10-year fund life, but you earn yield from interest payments instead of equity appreciation.

Distressed Debt & Restructuring

Distressed investing is the art of buying the debt of troubled companies at deep discounts and profiting from the restructuring process. It's intellectually demanding and combines credit analysis, legal knowledge, and negotiation.

Distressed Investment Strategies

StrategyHow It WorksTarget Return
Loan-to-OwnBuy senior debt at a discount, convert to equity through bankruptcy, control the restructured company20-30% IRR
TradingBuy distressed bonds/loans at deep discounts, sell when price recovers (or before bankruptcy resolution)15-25%
DIP FinancingProvide new financing to companies IN bankruptcy (Debtor-in-Possession), earning super-senior priority + high yields12-18%
Rescue FinancingProvide capital to avoid bankruptcy, negotiating favorable terms (high yield + warrants + covenants)15-25%
Memory Hook — The Fulcrum Security

In any restructuring, there's a "fulcrum security" — the tranche of debt where the value breaks. Everything above it gets repaid in full. Everything below it gets impaired (partially repaid or converted to equity). Everything well below it gets wiped out. Identifying the fulcrum security is THE key analytical task in distressed investing, because that's where you have the most influence over the restructuring outcome and where the risk/reward is highest.

Credit Interview Questions

Credit interview questions test whether you think like a lender — focused on downside protection, not upside potential.

⚡ Speed Round: Credit Technical Questions
What's the difference between investment grade and high yield?IG is BBB-/Baa3 or above. HY is BB+/Ba1 or below. The line determines which institutional investors can buy the bonds.
What's a maintenance covenant vs an incurrence covenant?Maintenance = tested continuously (quarterly). Incurrence = tested only when borrower takes a specific action (e.g., issuing new debt).
What is the FCCR and why do lenders care?Fixed Charge Coverage Ratio = (EBITDA - CapEx) / (Interest + Mandatory Amort). Shows if the company can cover ALL fixed obligations from operating cash. Below 1.0x = can't cover.
What happens when a company's debt is downgraded from BBB- to BB+?It becomes a "fallen angel." Institutional investors who can only hold IG bonds must sell, causing a price crash and higher borrowing costs.
What is the fulcrum security?The tranche in the capital structure where enterprise value "runs out" — debt above is fully covered, debt below is impaired. It's where equity conversion happens in a restructuring.
Why is EBITDA not a perfect proxy for cash flow?It ignores CapEx (real cash outflow), working capital changes, taxes actually paid, and one-time items. A company can have strong EBITDA but negative free cash flow.
What's the difference between secured and unsecured debt?Secured debt is backed by specific collateral (assets the lender can seize). Unsecured has no collateral — recovery depends on remaining assets after secured claims. Secured = lower yield, higher recovery.
How does a credit analyst think differently from an equity analyst?Equity: "How much upside is there?" Credit: "What's the worst case, and can they still pay me back?" Credit = downside-focused, base/stress case modeling, margin of safety.
