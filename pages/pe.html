1. What Is Private Equity?

Definition

Private equity is an asset class consisting of equity investments in companies that are not publicly traded on a stock exchange. PE firms raise capital from institutional investors and high-net-worth individuals, pool it into a fund, and use that capital to acquire, operate, and eventually sell companies for a profit.

How Private Equity Works

At its core, private equity is about buying businesses, making them better, and selling them at a higher price. The typical PE model involves:

  • Raising a fund from limited partners (pensions, endowments, sovereign wealth funds, family offices)
  • Deploying capital by acquiring controlling stakes in mature, cash-flow-generating businesses
  • Using leverage (debt) to amplify equity returns
  • Improving operations during a 3-7 year hold period
  • Exiting investments through sales, IPOs, or recapitalizations to realize returns

The Fund Lifecycle

A PE fund typically has a 10-year life (with optional 1-2 year extensions), divided into distinct phases:

PhaseTimelineActivities
FundraisingYears 0-1GP markets the fund, secures commitments from LPs, holds first close and final close
Investment PeriodYears 1-5Capital calls drawn down as deals are sourced, diligenced, and closed. Typically 8-15 portfolio companies acquired.
Value Creation / HarvestingYears 3-8Active management of portfolio companies. Operational improvements, add-on acquisitions, preparing for exit.
Exit / Wind-DownYears 5-10+Portfolio companies sold, proceeds distributed to LPs. Fund eventually terminates.

GP vs. LP Structure

General Partner (GP)

The PE firm itself. The GP manages the fund, makes investment decisions, sources deals, and runs portfolio companies. The GP earns management fees and carried interest. The GP typically commits 1-5% of the fund's total capital (known as the "GP commitment") to align interests with LPs.

Limited Partners (LPs)

The investors who provide the vast majority (95-99%) of the capital. LPs are "limited" because they have no control over investment decisions and their liability is limited to their committed capital. Common LPs include: public pension funds (CalPERS, OTPP), university endowments (Yale, Harvard), sovereign wealth funds (GIC, ADIA), insurance companies, fund-of-funds, and family offices.

The J-Curve

Understanding the J-Curve

The J-curve describes the typical pattern of returns for a PE fund over its life. In the early years, the fund shows negative returns because:

  • Management fees are charged on committed capital from day one
  • Investments are held at cost or marked down initially
  • Fund expenses (legal, organizational) are incurred upfront
  • No exits have occurred yet to generate realized gains

As the fund matures and begins exiting investments, returns swing positive and (ideally) accelerate upward, creating the characteristic "J" shape. A well-performing fund typically crosses back to positive returns by years 3-5 and reaches peak returns by years 7-10.

Interview Tip: If asked about the J-curve, mention that it is why PE returns should always be evaluated at maturity (vintage year analysis), not during the early investment period. Also note that the J-curve effect is one reason LPs diversify across vintage years, committing to new funds every 2-3 years to smooth out the return profile.

2. Fund Economics

Understanding PE fund economics is critical for both working in PE and interviewing. The standard fee structure is often called "2 and 20" but the details are far more nuanced.

Management Fee

2% Management Fee

The management fee compensates the GP for running the fund. Key details:

  • During investment period (years 1-5): Typically 2% of committed capital
  • After investment period (years 6-10): Typically 2% of invested capital (or net invested capital), which declines as investments are exited
  • Management fees are meant to cover salaries, office rent, travel, and operational expenses
  • For a $5B fund, the management fee generates $100M per year during the investment period
  • Some LPs negotiate fee discounts for large commitments ("most favored nation" clauses)

Carried Interest

20% Carried Interest ("Carry")

Carry is the GP's share of profits above a minimum return threshold. This is the primary economic incentive for the PE team.

  • Standard carry: 20% of profits above the hurdle rate
  • Top-tier firms may charge 25-30% carry
  • Carry is distributed to the GP's investment professionals according to an internal carry allocation plan
  • Carry typically vests over 3-5 years to retain talent
  • Carry is taxed at long-term capital gains rates (currently ~20% in the US) rather than ordinary income rates, which is a significant tax advantage

Hurdle Rate (Preferred Return)

Hurdle Rate

The hurdle rate (typically 8% per annum) is the minimum return LPs must receive before the GP earns any carry. This ensures the GP only profits when LPs have earned a satisfactory base return.

Example: On a $100M investment that returns $150M after 3 years, the LP must receive at least $100M x (1.08)^3 = $125.97M before the GP earns carry on the remaining ~$24M of profit.

GP Commitment

The GP typically commits 1-5% of fund capital alongside the LPs. This "skin in the game" ensures the GP's interests are aligned with LPs. For a $5B fund, this means the GP team invests $50M-$250M of their own money.

Clawback Provisions

Clawback: If the GP receives carry on early profitable exits but later investments perform poorly, the GP may be required to return excess carry so that the overall fund economics are fair. This is the "clawback" provision. It ensures the GP does not benefit from timing luck on early exits if the overall fund underperforms the hurdle rate.

Waterfall Distributions

The "waterfall" defines how profits flow between LPs and the GP. There are two primary structures:

StepEuropean (Whole-Fund) WaterfallAmerican (Deal-by-Deal) Waterfall
1. Return of Capital LPs receive back all invested capital across the entire fund LPs receive back invested capital on the specific deal
2. Preferred Return LPs receive 8% annual return on all capital LPs receive 8% annual return on that deal's capital
3. GP Catch-Up GP receives 100% of profits until GP has 20% of total profits (the "catch-up") GP receives 100% of profits until GP has 20% of deal profits
4. 80/20 Split Remaining profits split 80% LP / 20% GP Remaining profits split 80% LP / 20% GP
Key Difference: The European waterfall is more LP-friendly because the GP does not receive carry until all LP capital across the entire fund has been returned plus the preferred return. The American waterfall allows the GP to earn carry on each profitable deal individually, even if other deals in the fund have lost money. Most institutional LPs prefer the European model.

Key Fund Performance Metrics

MetricFormulaWhat It Measures
DPI (Distributed to Paid-In) Cumulative Distributions / Paid-In Capital Cash actually returned to LPs. DPI > 1.0x means LPs have their money back. The "gold standard" of PE performance.
RVPI (Residual Value to Paid-In) Remaining NAV / Paid-In Capital Value still locked in unrealized investments. Subject to GP valuation estimates.
TVPI (Total Value to Paid-In) DPI + RVPI = (Distributions + NAV) / Paid-In Capital Total fund multiple including unrealized value. A TVPI of 2.0x means LPs have doubled their money (on paper + cash).
Net IRR Internal rate of return net of fees and carry Annualized time-weighted return. The primary performance benchmark for PE. Top-quartile buyout funds target 20%+ net IRR.

Worked Example: Fund Economics

Fund Economics Walkthrough

Scenario: PE firm raises a $1 billion fund with a 10-year life.

  • Management Fee: 2% of $1B = $20M/year during investment period (years 1-5) = $100M total. After year 5, fee drops to 2% of invested capital.
  • GP Commitment: 2% = $20M invested by the GP team
  • Net Investable Capital: $1B - $100M (management fees reserved) = ~$900M deployable
  • Investments: 10 deals averaging $90M equity each

Fund Returns (after 7 years, European waterfall):

  1. Total Proceeds: $2.5B from all exits
  2. Return of Capital: LPs get back $980M (their paid-in capital, net of GP's $20M)
  3. Preferred Return (8%): LPs receive $980M x [(1.08)^7 - 1] = ~$699M in preferred return
  4. Total LP priority: $980M + $699M = $1,679M
  5. Remaining profit: $2,500M - $1,679M = $821M
  6. GP Catch-Up: GP receives 100% until GP has 20% of total profits. Total profits = $2,500M - $1,000M = $1,500M. GP's 20% = $300M. GP receives $300M from the catch-up.
  7. Remaining after catch-up: $821M - $300M = $521M, split 80/20
  8. LP gets: $980M + $699M + $521M x 80% = $980M + $699M + $417M = $2,096M
  9. GP gets: $300M + $521M x 20% = $300M + $104M = $404M in carry (plus $100M in management fees earned)

Fund Metrics:

  • TVPI: $2,500M / $1,000M = 2.5x
  • DPI: $2,500M / $1,000M = 2.5x (fully realized)
  • Gross IRR: ~14% (on total fund)
  • Net IRR (to LPs): ~11% (after management fees and carry)

3. LBO Model — Complete Deep Dive

The leveraged buyout model is the single most important technical skill in private equity. Every PE associate must be able to build an LBO from scratch. This section walks through every component in detail.

What Is an LBO?

A Leveraged Buyout is the acquisition of a company using a significant amount of borrowed money (debt) to fund the purchase price. The target company's cash flows and assets serve as collateral for the debt. The PE firm contributes equity (typically 30-50% of the purchase price) and finances the rest with various layers of debt.

The fundamental insight: by using leverage, the PE firm can amplify equity returns. If the company's value increases, all the upside accrues to the equity holders (the PE firm), while debt holders receive only their fixed interest payments.

3.2 Operating Model

The operating model projects the target company's financial performance over the hold period. Key components:

Revenue Build

  • Top-down: Market size x market share x pricing
  • Bottom-up: Units sold x average selling price, or customers x revenue per customer
  • Typical PE targets: 3-8% organic revenue growth
  • Add-on acquisition revenue modeled separately

EBITDA Margin Drivers

  • COGS: Raw materials, direct labor, manufacturing overhead. Model as % of revenue.
  • SG&A: Sales, general & administrative. Includes fixed and variable components.
  • Operating leverage: Fixed costs become a smaller % of revenue as revenue grows, expanding margins
  • Cost savings initiatives: Procurement, headcount optimization, facility consolidation

Capital Expenditures (CapEx)

  • Maintenance CapEx: Required to sustain current operations (typically 2-4% of revenue)
  • Growth CapEx: Investments to expand capacity or enter new markets
  • Model CapEx as % of revenue or absolute dollar amount

Working Capital

Net Working Capital Change

NWC = Current Assets - Current Liabilities

Change in NWC = NWC(t) - NWC(t-1)

An increase in NWC is a use of cash (negative cash flow). A decrease in NWC is a source of cash. Key drivers:

  • Accounts Receivable: Days Sales Outstanding (DSO) = (AR / Revenue) x 365
  • Inventory: Days Inventory Outstanding (DIO) = (Inventory / COGS) x 365
  • Accounts Payable: Days Payable Outstanding (DPO) = (AP / COGS) x 365
  • Cash Conversion Cycle: DSO + DIO - DPO

The debt schedule tracks all outstanding debt, mandatory payments, optional payments, and interest expense.

MechanismDescription
Mandatory AmortizationRequired principal repayments, typically 1-5% of original principal per year for Term Loan A. Term Loan B usually has minimal amortization (1% per year) with a bullet payment at maturity.

3.5 Returns Analysis at Exit

Key Return Metrics

MOIC (Multiple of Invested Capital):

MOIC = Exit Equity Value / Initial Equity Investment

IRR (Internal Rate of Return):

IRR = (MOIC)^(1/years) - 1   (simplified, assuming no interim cash flows)

Exit Analysis — $500M LBO, Year 5 Exit

Exit EBITDA: $100.4M

Exit Multiple: 8.0x (same as entry)

Exit Enterprise Value: $100.4M x 8.0x = $803.2M

Less: Remaining Debt: ($167.5M)

Exit Equity Value: $803.2M - $167.5M = $635.7M

Initial Equity Investment: $200.0M (sponsor) + $40.0M (rollover) = $240.0M

Returns to Sponsor ($200M equity):

  • Sponsor's share of exit equity (pro-rata): $200M / $240M x $635.7M = $529.8M
  • MOIC: $529.8M / $200M = 2.6x
  • IRR: (2.6x)^(1/5) - 1 = 21.5%

Decomposition of Returns:

  • EBITDA Growth ($62.5M to $100.4M): Contributed ~1.0x MOIC
  • Debt Paydown ($300M to $167.5M): Contributed ~0.6x MOIC
  • Multiple Expansion (0x in this case): Contributed 0.0x MOIC
  • FCF to Equity: Contributed ~0.0x MOIC (used for debt paydown)

4. Paper LBO — Interview in 5 Minutes

What Is a Paper LBO?

A paper LBO is a simplified, back-of-the-envelope LBO calculation done on paper (or a whiteboard) during a PE interview. The interviewer provides you with basic information about a target company, and you have 5-10 minutes to estimate the returns. No Excel, no calculator -- just mental math and a pen.

Step-by-Step Paper LBO Framework

The 6-Step Paper LBO

Step 1: Calculate the Purchase Price

Purchase Price = EBITDA x Entry Multiple

Tip: Round to easy numbers. $50M EBITDA x 8x = $400M.

Step 2: Determine Sources (Debt and Equity)

Total Debt = Purchase Price x Leverage %

Sponsor Equity = Purchase Price - Debt

Tip: 60% leverage on $400M = $240M debt, $160M equity.

Step 3: Project Exit EBITDA

Exit EBITDA = Entry EBITDA x (1 + growth)^years

Tip: Use the Rule of 72. 10% growth doubles in ~7 years. 7% growth doubles in ~10 years. For 5 years at ~10% growth, use a factor of roughly 1.6x.

Step 4: Calculate Exit Enterprise Value

Exit EV = Exit EBITDA x Exit Multiple

Tip: Use the same multiple as entry unless told otherwise.

Step 5: Estimate Debt Paydown

Estimate cumulative free cash flow over the hold period used for debt paydown.

Remaining Debt = Initial Debt - Cumulative Debt Paydown

Tip: Estimate annual FCF as ~40-50% of EBITDA minus interest. Use an average EBITDA over the period.

Step 6: Calculate Returns

Exit Equity = Exit EV - Remaining Debt

MOIC = Exit Equity / Initial Equity

IRR = MOIC^(1/years) - 1

Mental Math Shortcuts

IRR Quick Reference Table:
2.0x in 3 years = ~26% IRR
2.0x in 4 years = ~19% IRR
2.0x in 5 years = ~15% IRR
2.5x in 5 years = ~20% IRR
3.0x in 5 years = ~25% IRR
3.0x in 3 years = ~44% IRR
4.0x in 5 years = ~32% IRR

Growth Rule of Thumb: For 5-10% growth over 5 years, EBITDA grows by roughly 25-60%. For quick math, 5% annual growth for 5 years = ~1.28x, and 10% annual growth for 5 years = ~1.61x.

Practice Problem 1

Practice Problem 1: The Widget Company

Prompt: You are buying Widget Co. for $200M (10x $20M EBITDA). You use 50% debt at 6% interest. EBITDA grows 8% per year for 5 years. Assume 40% of EBITDA converts to free cash flow (after interest, taxes, CapEx). Exit at 10x. What are the returns?

Step 1: Purchase Price = $200M

Step 2: Sources

  • Debt: $200M x 50% = $100M
  • Equity: $200M - $100M = $100M

Step 3: Exit EBITDA

  • $20M x (1.08)^5 = $20M x 1.47 = $29.4M

Step 4: Exit EV

  • $29.4M x 10x = $294M

Step 5: Debt Paydown

  • Average EBITDA over 5 years is roughly ($20M + $29.4M) / 2 = ~$24.7M
  • Annual FCF = 40% x $24.7M = ~$9.9M per year
  • Cumulative FCF over 5 years = ~$49.5M used to pay down debt
  • Remaining Debt = $100M - $49.5M = ~$50.5M

Step 6: Returns

  • Exit Equity = $294M - $50.5M = $243.5M
  • MOIC = $243.5M / $100M = 2.4x
  • IRR = (2.4)^(1/5) - 1 = ~19%

Practice Problem 2: HealthCo Services

Prompt: HealthCo has $40M EBITDA. You pay 9x ($360M). Leverage is 5.0x EBITDA ($200M debt at 7% average rate). EBITDA grows 5% per year. Hold for 5 years. FCF conversion is 45% of EBITDA. Exit at 9x. What is the IRR?

Step 1: Purchase Price = $360M

Step 2: Sources

  • Debt: $200M (5.0x EBITDA)
  • Equity: $360M - $200M = $160M

Step 3: Exit EBITDA

  • $40M x (1.05)^5 = $40M x 1.276 = $51.1M

Step 4: Exit EV

  • $51.1M x 9x = $459.9M

Step 5: Debt Paydown

  • Average EBITDA = ~$45.5M
  • Annual FCF = 45% x $45.5M = ~$20.5M
  • Cumulative paydown over 5 years = ~$102.5M
  • Remaining Debt = $200M - $102.5M = ~$97.5M

Step 6: Returns

  • Exit Equity = $459.9M - $97.5M = $362.4M
  • MOIC = $362.4M / $160M = 2.3x
  • IRR = (2.3)^(1/5) - 1 = ~18%

  • IRR = (3.3)^(1/5) - 1 = ~27%

This is a strong return driven by all three value creation levers: EBITDA growth (nearly doubled), multiple expansion (11x to 12x), and significant debt paydown.

5. Debt Structures in Detail

Understanding the capital structure is essential for PE. Debt is layered in a "priority waterfall" -- in a bankruptcy, senior secured lenders get paid first, and equity holders get paid last.

Debt TypeSecurityTypical RateMaturityKey Features
Revolver Senior Secured (1st lien) SOFR + 200-300 bps 5 years Undrawn at close, used for working capital. Commitment fee on unused portion (~0.25-0.50%). Can be drawn and repaid repeatedly.
Term Loan A (TLA) Senior Secured (1st lien) SOFR + 200-300 bps 5-6 years Amortizing (20-25% per year). Held by banks. Tighter covenants. Lower spread because of faster payback.
Term Loan B (TLB) Senior Secured (1st lien) SOFR + 300-500 bps 6-7 years Minimal amortization (1% per year), bullet at maturity. Held by institutional investors (CLOs, hedge funds). Most common LBO debt tranche. Typically includes SOFR floor (e.g., 0.50-1.00%).
Second Lien Term Loan Senior Secured (2nd lien) SOFR + 600-800 bps 7-8 years Secured by same collateral as 1st lien but subordinated in priority. No amortization, bullet payment. Intercreditor agreement governs relationship with 1st lien.
Senior Unsecured Notes Unsecured 6-9% fixed 7-10 years No collateral. Issued in the high-yield bond market. Fixed rate, typically callable after 3-5 years with a call premium. 144A private placement or public offering.
Subordinated Debt Unsecured, subordinated 10-14% 8-10 years Junior to all senior debt. May include equity kickers (warrants). Common in mid-market deals.
Mezzanine Debt Unsecured, deeply subordinated 12-18% (cash + PIK) 8-10 years Often structured as part cash-pay interest, part PIK. May include equity co-invest or warrants for additional upside. Provided by specialty mezzanine funds.
PIK Notes Unsecured 12-16% PIK 8-10 years All interest is added to principal (no cash interest payments). Preserves cash flow but debt balance grows over time. Often used in aggressive capital structures.
High-Yield Bonds Unsecured or 2nd lien 6-10% fixed 7-10 years Publicly traded (or 144A). Rated below investment grade (BB+ or lower). Non-call periods, make-whole premiums. Used in larger LBOs for size and diversification.

Covenants

Maintenance vs. Incurrence Covenants

Maintenance Covenants are tested on a regular schedule (typically quarterly). If the borrower fails to meet the covenant, it triggers a default. These are stricter and more protective for lenders.

  • Maximum Leverage Ratio: Total Debt / EBITDA must not exceed a specified level (e.g., 5.5x, stepping down to 4.5x over time)
  • Minimum Interest Coverage: EBITDA / Interest Expense must exceed a specified level (e.g., 2.0x)
  • Minimum Fixed Charge Coverage: (EBITDA - CapEx) / (Interest + Mandatory Amortization + Taxes) must exceed a specified level

Incurrence Covenants are only tested when the borrower takes a specific action (e.g., incurring new debt, making an acquisition, or paying a dividend). More flexible for the borrower.

  • Tested only when the borrower wants to do something (e.g., "You may incur additional debt only if pro forma leverage is below 4.0x")
  • "Covenant-lite" (cov-lite) loans have only incurrence covenants and no maintenance covenants. These have become the standard for large TLBs since ~2013.

Intercreditor Agreements

Who Gets Paid First?

When multiple layers of debt exist, an intercreditor agreement (ICA) governs the rights of each lender class in the event of a default or bankruptcy:

  • 1st lien holders have the right to control enforcement actions (foreclosure, asset sales)
  • 2nd lien holders typically have a "standstill period" (90-180 days) during which they cannot take enforcement action
  • 2nd lien holders may have the right to purchase the 1st lien debt at par (the "purchase option")
  • Unsecured and subordinated lenders are restricted from taking action until senior debt is fully repaid
  • The absolute priority rule (APR) governs bankruptcy distributions: senior secured first, then unsecured, then subordinated, then equity

6. Value Creation Framework

PE returns come from a combination of levers that drive equity value growth. Understanding and quantifying each lever is central to both investment decision-making and interview discussions.

The Four Value Creation Levers

1. Revenue Growth

Increasing the top line through organic growth (pricing, volume, new products, geographic expansion) and inorganic growth (add-on acquisitions). Revenue growth directly increases EBITDA if margins are maintained or expanded.

  • Organic: Sales force effectiveness, pricing optimization, customer retention, new product launches, market expansion
  • Inorganic (Buy-and-Build): Acquiring smaller competitors or adjacent businesses at lower multiples (e.g., buying at 5-6x EBITDA and getting credit at the platform's 8-10x multiple -- "multiple arbitrage")

2. Margin Expansion

Improving profitability by reducing costs or improving operating efficiency. Common initiatives:

  • Procurement optimization: Renegotiating supplier contracts, centralizing purchasing, leveraging scale
  • Headcount rationalization: Eliminating redundant positions, outsourcing non-core functions
  • Facility consolidation: Closing underperforming locations, optimizing real estate
  • IT/Systems upgrades: Implementing ERP systems, automating manual processes
  • Pricing strategies: Reducing discounting, implementing value-based pricing

3. Multiple Expansion

Selling the company at a higher EV/EBITDA multiple than what was paid at entry. This can be driven by:

  • Growing the company into a larger size category (larger companies trade at higher multiples)
  • Improving the quality and predictability of earnings (e.g., shifting to recurring revenue)
  • Diversifying the customer base (reducing concentration risk)
  • Building a platform with a demonstrated buy-and-build track record
  • Timing the exit during favorable market conditions

Note: Prudent underwriting assumes no multiple expansion (exit at entry multiple). Multiple expansion is "gravy" -- not something to rely on.

4. Debt Paydown (De-Leveraging)

As the company generates free cash flow and pays down debt, the equity value increases dollar-for-dollar even if the enterprise value stays flat. This is the "mechanical" return driver inherent in an LBO structure.

  • For every $1 of debt paid down, equity value increases by $1
  • This is why PE firms prioritize businesses with strong, predictable free cash flow
  • Debt paydown contributes more to returns in higher-leverage deals

Value Creation Attribution Analysis

PE firms decompose their returns to understand how much each lever contributed. This is called a "value creation bridge" or "equity bridge."

Attribution Formula

Total Equity Value Created = Entry Equity to Exit Equity

This decomposes into:

  • EBITDA Growth Effect: (Exit EBITDA - Entry EBITDA) x Entry Multiple
  • Multiple Expansion Effect: (Exit Multiple - Entry Multiple) x Exit EBITDA
  • Debt Paydown Effect: Entry Debt - Exit Debt
  • FCF to Equity Effect: Any dividends or cash accumulated on balance sheet

Attribution Example

Bought at $500M EV (8x on $62.5M EBITDA), $300M debt, $200M equity. Sold at $900M EV (9x on $100M EBITDA), $150M remaining debt.

  • Exit Equity: $900M - $150M = $750M
  • Equity Created: $750M - $200M = $550M (MOIC: 3.75x)

Attribution:

  • EBITDA Growth: ($100M - $62.5M) x 8x = $300M (54.5% of value created)
  • Multiple Expansion: (9x - 8x) x $100M = $100M (18.2% of value created)
  • Debt Paydown: $300M - $150M = $150M (27.3% of value created)
  • Total: $300M + $100M + $150M = $550M (check: matches)

7. Due Diligence Process

Due diligence is the comprehensive investigation of a target company before closing an acquisition. PE firms typically spend 4-8 weeks on exclusivity-phase diligence, involving multiple third-party advisors and significant cost ($2-5M+ for a large deal).

Commercial Due Diligence

Market & Competitive Analysis

Usually conducted by a consulting firm (Bain, McKinsey, BCG, L.E.K., or specialized boutiques). Evaluates:

  • Total Addressable Market (TAM): Market size, growth rate, key drivers
  • Competitive positioning: Market share, barriers to entry, competitive advantages (moat)
  • Customer analysis: Concentration, retention rates, NPS scores, switching costs, wallet share
  • Industry trends: Regulatory changes, technology disruption, secular tailwinds/headwinds
  • Pricing power: Ability to pass through cost increases, competitive pricing dynamics

Financial Due Diligence

Quality of Earnings (QoE) Report

Performed by an accounting firm (Big 4 or specialized firms). The QoE is the single most important diligence workstream. It answers: "Is the reported EBITDA real, sustainable, and recurring?"

A Quality of Earnings report typically contains:

  • Adjusted EBITDA: Starting with reported EBITDA and making adjustments:
    • Add-backs: Non-recurring expenses (one-time litigation, restructuring, transaction costs, owner perks)
    • Subtractions: Non-recurring revenue (one-time contracts, unsustainable pricing), understated expenses
    • Normalizations: Adjusting for above/below market rent, related-party transactions, run-rate of recent hires/losses
  • Revenue quality: Recurring vs. non-recurring, customer concentration, contract renewal rates
  • Working capital analysis: Normalized working capital levels, seasonal patterns, peg amount for purchase agreement
  • CapEx analysis: Maintenance vs. growth, deferred maintenance liabilities
  • Debt-like items: Unfunded pensions, deferred revenue, off-balance-sheet liabilities
  • Pro forma adjustments: Impact of recent acquisitions, facility closures, contract wins/losses

Legal Due Diligence

  • Material contracts and change-of-control provisions
  • Pending or threatened litigation
  • Intellectual property portfolio and protection
  • Employment agreements, non-competes, severance obligations
  • Regulatory compliance (industry-specific licenses, permits)
  • Environmental liabilities
  • Tax structure and potential exposures

Operational Due Diligence

  • IT infrastructure and cybersecurity assessment
  • Supply chain resilience and vendor dependencies
  • Facility condition assessments
  • HR: organizational structure, key person risk, compensation benchmarking
  • Insurance coverage adequacy

Management Assessment

  • Extensive interviews with CEO, CFO, and key executives
  • Reference checks (formal and "back-channel")
  • Assessment of whether management can execute the value creation plan
  • Identification of gaps requiring new hires post-close
  • Alignment of management incentives (rollover equity, option pool)

Red Flags to Watch For

Due Diligence Red Flags:
  • Aggressive revenue recognition: Booking revenue before delivery, channel stuffing
  • Excessive add-backs: If adjusted EBITDA is 30-50%+ above reported, something is wrong
  • Customer concentration: Top customer representing more than 20% of revenue
  • Declining core metrics: Falling same-store sales, shrinking customer base, increasing churn
  • Deferred maintenance: Underinvesting in CapEx to inflate short-term EBITDA
  • Key person dependency: Business relies heavily on the founder/owner with no succession plan
  • Related-party transactions: Revenue from affiliated entities, above-market leases to owner
  • Working capital manipulation: Stretching payables or accelerating collections to inflate cash at a point in time
  • Environmental or regulatory exposure: Contamination, pending regulatory changes, compliance gaps
  • Pending major customer loss or contract expiration: Known but undisclosed risks

Initial Screening Criteria

PE firms evaluate hundreds of opportunities per year but invest in only a handful. The initial screen rapidly filters down to a shortlist. Common criteria:

Typical Screening Criteria

  • Revenue Threshold: Minimum $50-100M+ revenue (varies by fund size). Lower mid-market funds may target $10-50M.
  • EBITDA Margins: Prefer 15%+ EBITDA margins (ideally 20-30%+). Higher margins = more room for error and better cash conversion.
  • Market Position: #1 or #2 in its niche. Defensible competitive advantages. Not easily displaced by larger players or technology.
  • Growth: Revenue growing at least GDP+ (3-5% minimum). Secular growth tailwinds preferred.
  • Defensibility: High switching costs, recurring revenue, long-term contracts, regulatory barriers, network effects, or proprietary technology.
  • Cash Flow: Consistent, predictable free cash flow generation. Low capital intensity. Minimal working capital requirements.
  • Fragmented Market: Opportunities for add-on acquisitions (buy-and-build strategy).
  • Manageable Cyclicality: Business performance is relatively stable through economic cycles. Avoid highly cyclical industries unless specialized (distressed funds).
  • Clean Management/Ownership: Professional management team or clear plan to upgrade. No litigation or regulatory overhang.
  • Clear Exit Path: Multiple potential exit routes (strategic buyers, other PE firms, IPO).

Investment Thesis Development

Once a deal passes initial screening, the PE team develops a detailed investment thesis, which typically covers:

  1. Why this business? What makes it attractive and defensible?
  2. Why now? What is the catalyst or inflection point?
  3. What is the value creation plan? Specific, measurable operational improvements
  4. What are the key risks? And how will they be mitigated?
  5. How do we exit? Who are the likely buyers? What multiple is achievable?
  6. What are the returns? Base, upside, and downside cases with IRR and MOIC targets

9. Exit Strategies

The exit is how PE firms realize returns. A well-planned exit maximizes value and completes the investment cycle. PE firms begin thinking about the exit on the day they make the investment.

Exit TypeDescriptionProsConsTypical Timeline
Strategic Sale (M&A) Selling to a corporate buyer (a company in the same or adjacent industry) Highest multiples (strategic premium for synergies), clean exit, full liquidity Regulatory risk, integration concerns, fewer potential buyers in niche markets 3-6 months process
Secondary Buyout (SBO) Selling to another PE firm Quick, well-understood process. Buyer is financially sophisticated. Growing market. May signal "PE firms cannot find a strategic buyer." Often lower multiples. LPs may question recycling fees. 3-5 months process
Initial Public Offering (IPO) Listing shares on a public stock exchange Potentially highest valuation. Public currency for future acquisitions. Prestige. Lengthy and expensive process. Market timing risk. PE firm typically cannot sell all shares at IPO (lock-up). Ongoing public company costs and scrutiny. 6-12 months prep + lock-up
Dividend Recapitalization The portfolio company takes on additional debt and pays a special dividend to the PE sponsor Returns capital to LPs without selling the company. PE firm retains upside. May reduce or eliminate equity at risk. Increases leverage (risk). May be viewed negatively. Not a true "exit" -- the PE firm still owns the company. 2-3 months to execute

Preparing for Exit

Exit Preparation (12-18 Months Before Sale)

  • Clean up financials: Ensure audited statements are clean, EBITDA adjustments are defensible, and reporting is institutional quality
  • Vendor Due Diligence (VDD): Commission a sell-side QoE report to proactively address buyer questions and accelerate the process
  • Growth story: Demonstrate recent positive momentum in revenue and EBITDA. Buyers pay the most for businesses that are growing and accelerating.
  • Management continuity: Ensure key management is retained and incentivized to stay through a transition
  • Resolve legal/environmental issues: Settle pending litigation, address compliance gaps
  • Optimize working capital: Ensure NWC levels are at normalized, sustainable levels (not artificially inflated or deflated)
  • Board-ready materials: Prepare a compelling management presentation and detailed CIM for the sale process

Timeline Considerations

Typical PE Hold Period: 4-6 years on average. The trend has been toward longer holds as PE firms focus more on operational value creation rather than financial engineering. Key timing factors:
  • IRR vs. MOIC trade-off: Shorter holds boost IRR (time-weighted), but longer holds may generate higher MOIC (total value). A 2.0x return in 3 years (26% IRR) looks better on IRR than 3.0x in 6 years (20% IRR), but the latter creates more total value.
  • Market conditions: Favorable debt and equity markets can accelerate exit timing
  • Fund lifecycle: Investments made in years 4-5 of the fund may need to be exited by year 10, constraining hold period
  • GP fund extensions: Most LPAs allow 1-2 year extensions beyond the initial 10-year life

10. PE Interview Questions — Top 20

PE interviews are among the most rigorous in finance. They test technical knowledge, deal experience, commercial judgment, and cultural fit. Below are the most commonly asked questions with detailed model answers.

Technical Questions

1. Walk me through an LBO model.

Model Answer: An LBO starts with determining the purchase price, typically expressed as a multiple of EBITDA. You then build the Sources and Uses table: sources include sponsor equity and various tranches of debt (senior secured, unsecured, mezzanine); uses include the purchase price, transaction fees, and financing fees. Sources must equal uses.

Next, you build the operating model -- projecting revenue, EBITDA, CapEx, working capital, and taxes over the hold period (typically 5 years). This feeds into the free cash flow available for debt service.

The debt schedule tracks each debt tranche, mandatory amortization, optional prepayments (cash sweeps), and interest expense. You waterfall the cash flows through mandatory payments first, then apply excess cash to paying down the most expensive debt.

Finally, at exit, you multiply the projected EBITDA by the exit multiple to get exit enterprise value, subtract remaining debt to get exit equity, and calculate MOIC and IRR. MOIC = exit equity / entry equity. IRR is the annualized return, approximately MOIC^(1/years) - 1.

2. What makes a good LBO candidate?

Model Answer: A good LBO candidate has several characteristics: (1) Strong, predictable cash flows to service debt -- this is the most important factor. (2) Defensible market position with competitive advantages. (3) Low capital intensity -- minimal CapEx requirements leave more cash for debt paydown. (4) Opportunities for operational improvement -- cost reduction, revenue acceleration, margin expansion. (5) Strong management team that can execute the business plan. (6) Tangible assets that can serve as collateral for debt. (7) A clear path to exit with multiple potential buyers. (8) Industry tailwinds -- secular growth trends.

In practice, PE firms love asset-light businesses with recurring revenue, such as software, healthcare services, business services, and insurance brokerage, because they generate high and predictable free cash flow.

3. How do you calculate IRR, and what is the relationship between IRR and MOIC?

Model Answer: IRR is the discount rate that makes the NPV of all cash flows (including the initial investment and exit proceeds) equal to zero. In an LBO context with no interim cash flows, the simplified formula is IRR = MOIC^(1/n) - 1, where n is the hold period in years.

MOIC is the absolute multiple of money returned (exit equity / invested equity). IRR is the time-weighted annualized return.

The key relationship: for a given MOIC, a shorter hold period results in a higher IRR. For example, a 2.0x MOIC over 3 years is a 26% IRR, but the same 2.0x over 5 years is only 15% IRR. This is why PE firms sometimes face a tension between maximizing MOIC (holding longer) and maximizing IRR (exiting earlier).

Most PE firms target a minimum of 2.0-2.5x MOIC and 20%+ IRR, though thresholds vary by strategy and market environment.

4. What drives returns in an LBO?

Model Answer: LBO returns are driven by four levers: (1) EBITDA growth through revenue increases and margin expansion -- this is the most sustainable and most valued source of returns. (2) Multiple expansion -- selling at a higher multiple than entry, which can be driven by scaling the business, improving earnings quality, or favorable market conditions. (3) Debt paydown -- as the company generates free cash flow and repays debt, equity value increases dollar-for-dollar. (4) Cash generation -- any excess cash accumulated on the balance sheet or dividends paid to equity holders during the hold period.

The best PE investments generate returns from all four levers. The worst-performing investments typically suffer from EBITDA deterioration or multiple compression, overwhelming the debt paydown benefit.

5. Walk me through the debt structure of an LBO. What are the different tranches?

Model Answer: LBO debt is layered by priority and cost. From cheapest/most senior to most expensive/junior: (1) Revolving Credit Facility -- senior secured, drawn as needed for working capital, SOFR + 200-300bps. (2) Term Loan A -- senior secured, amortizing over 5-6 years, held by banks. (3) Term Loan B -- senior secured, minimal amortization with a bullet at maturity, held by institutional investors (CLOs, funds), SOFR + 300-500bps. (4) Second Lien -- secured but subordinate to first lien, higher rate. (5) Senior Unsecured Notes / High-Yield Bonds -- no collateral, 6-10% fixed rate. (6) Mezzanine / Subordinated Debt -- most junior debt, 12-18%, may include PIK component and equity warrants.

The total leverage in an LBO is typically 4-6x EBITDA, with senior secured debt comprising the majority. The capital structure is designed to balance maximizing leverage (for equity returns) with maintaining adequate coverage and flexibility.

6. What is the difference between maintenance and incurrence covenants?

Model Answer: Maintenance covenants are tested continuously (usually quarterly) regardless of what the borrower is doing. Common examples include a maximum leverage ratio (Debt/EBITDA below 5.5x) and minimum interest coverage ratio (EBITDA/Interest above 2.0x). If the borrower trips a maintenance covenant, it triggers a default.

Incurrence covenants are only tested when the borrower takes a specific action, such as incurring new debt, making an acquisition, or paying a dividend. They are essentially permission tests: "You can do X only if you meet Y condition."

The trend in leveraged lending since 2013 has been toward "covenant-lite" structures where Term Loan B facilities have only incurrence covenants (no maintenance covenants), giving borrowers more flexibility. This benefits PE sponsors but provides less downside protection for lenders.

7. How does a dividend recapitalization work?

Model Answer: A dividend recapitalization is when a PE-owned company takes on additional debt (or refinances existing debt at a higher amount) and uses the proceeds to pay a special dividend to the equity holders (primarily the PE sponsor). It allows the PE firm to return capital to LPs and reduce equity at risk without selling the company.

For example, if a PE firm invested $200M in equity and the company has de-leveraged significantly, the company might raise $150M in new debt and pay it as a dividend. The PE firm recovers $150M of its $200M investment while still owning 100% of the equity. Even if the company subsequently goes to zero, the PE firm has limited its loss to $50M.

Dividend recaps are common in strong credit markets and for high-performing portfolio companies. They can be controversial because they increase the company's leverage, potentially putting it at greater risk, while benefiting the PE sponsor.

8. Explain the concept of multiple arbitrage in buy-and-build strategies.

Model Answer: Multiple arbitrage in a buy-and-build context occurs when a PE firm acquires add-on companies at lower EBITDA multiples than the platform company, thereby creating value through the multiple differential. Smaller companies trade at lower multiples because they have more risk, less diversification, and fewer buyers. When acquired and integrated into a larger platform, their earnings are "re-rated" at the platform's higher multiple.

For example, if a platform company is valued at 10x EBITDA and acquires a smaller competitor for 6x EBITDA ($10M EBITDA for $60M), the acquired EBITDA is immediately "worth" 10x = $100M within the platform. The PE firm has created $40M in equity value on a $60M investment just through the multiple differential, before any synergies.

This is why buy-and-build is one of the most popular PE strategies in fragmented industries like healthcare services, insurance brokerage, home services, and business services.

9. What is a Quality of Earnings report, and why is it important?

Model Answer: A Quality of Earnings (QoE) report is a financial due diligence study performed by an accounting firm that validates the target's reported earnings. It determines whether the EBITDA the seller is presenting is accurate, sustainable, and reflective of the ongoing business.

The QoE starts with reported EBITDA and makes two types of adjustments: add-backs (non-recurring expenses like litigation, restructuring, transaction costs) and subtractions (one-time revenues, unsustainable pricing, understated costs). It also analyzes revenue quality, working capital normalization, CapEx requirements, and debt-like items.

It is critical because the purchase price is directly derived from EBITDA (Price = EBITDA x Multiple). If the EBITDA is overstated by even $5M in a deal using an 8x multiple, the buyer is overpaying by $40M. Aggressive seller add-backs are one of the biggest risks in any PE deal.

10. What is the difference between enterprise value and equity value?

Model Answer: Enterprise value represents the total value of the operating business, independent of its capital structure. It is the value a buyer would pay to acquire the entire business and assume all its obligations. Equity value represents the residual value attributable to equity holders after all debt and debt-like obligations are subtracted.

The bridge: Equity Value = Enterprise Value - Net Debt (where Net Debt = Total Debt + Preferred Stock + Minority Interest - Cash). In an LBO, we think in terms of EV because the PE firm is buying the entire enterprise (using a combination of their equity and borrowed debt). At exit, the key calculation is: Exit Equity = Exit EV - Remaining Debt.

Behavioral / Fit Questions

11. Why private equity?

Model Answer Framework: Structure your answer around three themes: (1) Ownership mentality: "In PE, you are not just advising -- you own the business and are accountable for the outcome. I am excited by the idea of making an investment decision, working hands-on with management to implement a plan, and seeing the results of that work over several years." (2) Long-term value creation: "In banking, I worked on transactions that lasted weeks or months. In PE, I want to work on creating lasting value over 3-5+ years, thinking like an operator, not just a financial advisor." (3) Intellectual challenge: "PE combines financial modeling, strategic thinking, operational problem-solving, and people management. It requires a broader skill set than almost any other role in finance."

Tailor this to your background: if coming from IB, explain what you learned and how PE is the natural evolution. Be genuine and specific.

12. Walk me through a deal you worked on.

Framework (STAR + Technical):

  • Context: Industry, deal type, size, your role. "I worked on the sell-side of a $500M healthcare services carve-out for [bank] as a first-year analyst."
  • Situation/Challenge: What made this deal interesting or complex? "The challenge was that the target was being carved out of a larger entity with shared services, so we needed to build standalone financials."
  • Your Contribution: Be specific about what YOU did. "I built the standalone P&L, modeled the $15M in dis-synergies from the carve-out, and prepared the sensitivity analysis for the board presentation."
  • Outcome: What happened? "We ultimately sold to a PE firm at 11x EBITDA, a 20% premium to our initial expectations."
  • Lessons Learned: What did you take away? "I learned how critical working capital adjustments are in carve-outs and how to manage complex buyer due diligence requests."

Be prepared for deep follow-up questions: "What multiple did it trade at?" "What were the key diligence issues?" "How did you think about the standalone costs?" "What would you have done differently?"

13. Tell me about a time you had to persuade someone of your viewpoint.

What they are looking for: PE requires working with management teams, co-investors, lenders, and fellow deal team members. You need to demonstrate that you can make a clear, logical argument, support it with data, and influence outcomes without being combative.

Framework: (1) Describe the situation and what you believed. (2) Explain why others disagreed. (3) Describe how you built your case (data, analysis, precedent). (4) Show the outcome and what you learned.

14. What sector or industry are you most interested in, and why?

How to approach: Choose an industry you genuinely find interesting and can speak about intelligently. Demonstrate that you have done your homework. Structure: (1) Name the industry and explain the macro tailwinds. (2) Explain what makes businesses in this space attractive from a PE perspective (recurring revenue, fragmentation, growth). (3) Mention a specific deal or company you find interesting and why. (4) Connect it to the firm you are interviewing with, if relevant.

Example: "I am particularly interested in healthcare IT. The industry benefits from secular tailwinds like digitization of health records, regulatory compliance requirements, and an aging population driving healthcare spending. These businesses tend to have recurring revenue, high switching costs, and strong retention rates. I followed [Firm's] investment in [company] and was impressed by the buy-and-build strategy they executed."

15. Pitch me a stock or an investment idea.

Framework (for PE-style pitch):

  1. Company Overview: What does it do? Size, industry, market position.
  2. Investment Thesis: Why is it attractive? 2-3 key points (secular growth, market leader, operational improvement opportunity).
  3. Valuation: Current trading multiples vs. peers. Why is it undervalued?
  4. Value Creation Plan: What would you do as an owner? Specific operational levers.
  5. Key Risks: What could go wrong? How would you mitigate?
  6. Exit: Who would buy it and at what multiple?

Have 2-3 investment ideas prepared. Ideally, one that fits the firm's sector focus. Be prepared for pushback: "What if margins compress?" "Who is the most likely competitor to disrupt them?" "How do you know the growth rate is sustainable?"

16. If you could only look at two financial metrics to evaluate a potential LBO target, what would they be?

Model Answer: I would choose EBITDA margin and free cash flow conversion.

EBITDA margin tells me about profitability, competitive positioning, and pricing power. High-margin businesses (20%+) tend to have stronger moats, better unit economics, and more room to service debt. It also indicates the potential for operational improvement -- a 20% margin business has more room to expand to 25% than a 35% margin business has to reach 40%.

Free cash flow conversion (FCF as a % of EBITDA) tells me how much of the earnings translate into actual cash that can be used for debt paydown. A company with $100M EBITDA but 30% FCF conversion generates only $30M of cash, while a company with $50M EBITDA and 80% conversion generates $40M. In an LBO, cash flow is king because it drives debt paydown and ultimately equity returns.

17. How would a $10M increase in revenue vs. a $10M decrease in costs affect an LBO differently?

Model Answer: From a pure EBITDA perspective, a $10M cost decrease flows 100% to EBITDA, while a $10M revenue increase flows to EBITDA only at the incremental margin (say 40-60%). So $10M in cost savings might add $10M to EBITDA, while $10M in revenue adds only $4-6M to EBITDA.

However, there are important nuances: (1) Revenue growth is more sustainable and valued by buyers at exit (multiples are often higher for growing businesses). (2) Cost cuts have a ceiling (you cannot cut costs to zero), while revenue growth is theoretically unlimited. (3) Cost cuts are more certain and quicker to implement, making them attractive for near-term results. (4) At exit, a buyer will pay a multiple on EBITDA, so the $10M cost saving creates more enterprise value ($80-100M at 8-10x) than the $5M incremental EBITDA from revenue ($40-50M at 8-10x). But the revenue growth story supports a higher exit multiple.

In practice, the best PE investments combine both: cost discipline and revenue growth.

18. What happens to returns if you increase leverage from 4x to 6x EBITDA?

Model Answer: Increasing leverage amplifies equity returns in both directions. In a successful investment where the business grows and exits at a higher value, higher leverage means the PE firm put in less equity upfront, so the MOIC and IRR are higher. You are essentially doing more with less of your own money.

For example, a $500M deal with 4x leverage ($250M debt, $250M equity) that exits at $800M with $150M remaining debt gives you $650M exit equity, or 2.6x MOIC. The same deal with 6x leverage ($375M debt, $125M equity) that exits at $800M with $250M remaining debt gives you $550M exit equity, or 4.4x MOIC -- dramatically higher.

However, the downside is equally amplified. Higher leverage means higher interest expense, less cash flow cushion, and greater risk of covenant breach or default if the business underperforms. At 6x leverage, even a moderate EBITDA decline could wipe out the equity entirely. This is why the risk-return trade-off of leverage is one of the most important judgments in PE.

19. How do PE firms typically align management incentives?

Model Answer: PE firms use several mechanisms: (1) Management rollover equity: Require existing management to reinvest a significant portion of their sale proceeds (typically 25-75% of after-tax proceeds) back into the new entity. This ensures management has significant "skin in the game." (2) Management equity pool (MEP): Grant an additional equity pool (typically 10-20% of total equity) to management through options or restricted shares, often with vesting tied to time (3-5 years) and performance (EBITDA or IRR hurdles). (3) Cash bonus plans: Annual incentive compensation tied to EBITDA, revenue, or other operational KPIs. (4) Board representation: Management sits on the board alongside PE professionals, creating regular accountability. (5) Operating partners: PE firms place experienced industry executives alongside management to provide guidance and oversight.

The key insight: management's biggest payday comes from the equity upside at exit, not from salary or bonuses. This creates strong alignment with the PE firm's goal of maximizing exit value.

20. What are the biggest risks in an LBO?

Model Answer: The biggest risks fall into several categories:

  • Overpaying (entry multiple too high): If you pay 12x EBITDA and the market corrects to 8x, you are starting from a 33% hole. Valuation discipline is the single most important risk management tool.
  • Over-leveraging: Too much debt reduces cash flow flexibility and increases bankruptcy risk. A business that is viable at 4x leverage may not survive at 6x if revenue drops 10%.
  • Operational underperformance: Revenue misses, margin compression, loss of key customers or employees. The value creation plan may not execute as expected.
  • Macro/cyclical downturn: Economic recession, rising interest rates (increases floating-rate debt costs), or industry-specific downturn.
  • Interest rate risk: If a significant portion of debt is floating-rate and rates rise sharply, interest expense can consume all free cash flow.
  • Integration risk (for add-on acquisitions): Failed integrations can destroy value rather than create it.
  • Refinancing risk: Debt matures during an unfavorable credit market, forcing a distressed refinancing or equity injection.
  • Management risk: Key executives leave, underperform, or resist the PE firm's strategic direction.

PE firms mitigate these risks through thorough due diligence, conservative underwriting (downside cases that still protect capital), maintaining adequate liquidity, diversifying across investments, and active portfolio management.

PE Interview Knowledge Quiz

Q: In a European waterfall distribution, when does the GP start receiving carried interest?

A) After each profitable deal exit
B) After the fund returns 1.0x to LPs
C) After the fund returns all LP capital plus the preferred return across the entire fund
D) After the first year of management fees
In a European (whole-fund) waterfall, the GP does not receive carry until ALL LP capital has been returned AND LPs have received their preferred return (typically 8%) across the entire fund. This is the most LP-friendly structure. An American (deal-by-deal) waterfall allows the GP to earn carry on individual profitable deals, which is Answer A.

Q: A company has $50M EBITDA and you buy it at 8x. You use 5x leverage. After 5 years, EBITDA is $75M and you exit at 9x. Debt has been paid down to $150M. What is the approximate MOIC?

A) 2.5x
B) 3.6x
C) 4.0x
D) 5.0x
Purchase price = $50M x 8 = $400M. Debt = $50M x 5 = $250M. Equity = $400M - $250M = $150M. Exit EV = $75M x 9 = $675M. Exit Equity = $675M - $150M debt = $525M. MOIC = $525M / $150M = 3.5x. The closest answer is B (3.6x, accounting for slight rounding).

Q: Which of the following is NOT typically a red flag during financial due diligence?

A) Adjusted EBITDA 40% higher than reported EBITDA
B) Top customer representing 30% of revenue
C) Declining same-store sales masked by acquisition growth
D) EBITDA margins improving due to pricing increases and operating leverage
Options A, B, and C are all classic red flags. Excessive add-backs (A) suggest earnings quality issues. High customer concentration (B) creates dependency risk. Declining organic metrics (C) masked by acquisitions is concerning. Option D -- margins improving from pricing and operating leverage -- is actually a positive sign of business health and competitive strength.
