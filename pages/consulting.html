What Consultants Actually Do

Mark Complete

Management consultants are hired problem-solvers. Companies pay them to diagnose business problems, develop strategies, and implement solutions. Consulting is one of the most sought-after career paths because it provides unmatched exposure to industries, business problems, and senior executives, all within the first few years of your career.

Typical Project Lifecycle

1

Proposal / Business Development (1-2 weeks)

Partners respond to client RFPs (Request for Proposals) or pitch new work. Teams develop a proposal outlining the approach, timeline, team, and fees. A typical MBB strategy engagement costs $1-3M for a 3-month project.

2

Project Kickoff (Week 1)

Team aligns on the problem statement, key questions, hypotheses, and workplan. The engagement manager builds the workplan and issue tree. Day 1 hypothesis: you form an initial view of the answer, then test it with data.

3

Research & Analysis (Weeks 2-6)

Analysts conduct interviews (10-30 stakeholder interviews per project), build Excel models, analyze data, size markets, benchmark competitors, and survey customers. This is the core "doing the work" phase.

4

Synthesis & Midpoint Check-in (Weeks 4-6)

Team synthesizes findings, tests hypotheses against evidence, and presents progress to the client. This is a critical checkpoint: do findings support or refute the hypothesis? Pivot if needed.

5

Final Deliverable & Presentation (Weeks 7-8)

Build the final deck (often 50-100+ slides). Present recommendations to the CEO/Board. A typical final deck follows: Situation > Key Findings > Recommendations > Implementation Roadmap > Financial Impact.

6

Implementation Support (Optional, Weeks 9+)

Some engagements extend into implementation. The team helps the client execute on recommendations: building organizational capabilities, tracking KPIs, managing change, and ensuring the strategy actually gets done.

Typical Deliverables

  • PowerPoint Deck: The primary deliverable. Everything in consulting lives or dies by the deck. Each slide has one message, supported by evidence in the body.
  • Excel Model: Financial models, market sizing models, scenario analysis. Less complex than banking models but more insight-driven.
  • Interview Summary: Notes from stakeholder/customer/expert interviews, synthesized into themes and insights.
  • Implementation Roadmap: Gantt chart or workplan showing how to execute recommendations over 6-24 months.
  • Benchmark Analysis: How the client compares to peers on key metrics (margins, growth, efficiency ratios).

Structured Thinking & MECE

Mark Complete

Structured thinking is the single most important skill in consulting. It is the ability to break down a complex, ambiguous problem into smaller, manageable components in a logical way. The gold standard for structuring is MECE.

MECE: Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive

Mutually Exclusive (ME): Each category is distinct with no overlap. If something fits in one bucket, it should NOT fit in another. Think of it as: no double-counting.

Collectively Exhaustive (CE): All categories together cover 100% of the problem. Nothing is missed. Think of it as: no gaps.

Why MECE Matters

  • Prevents double-counting: If your revenue breakdown has overlapping segments, you will overcount and reach wrong conclusions.
  • Ensures completeness: If you miss a cost category, your profitability analysis is wrong.
  • Enables delegation: Each team member can work on a separate MECE bucket independently without stepping on each other's toes.
  • Builds credibility: Interviewers and clients can immediately tell if your thinking is structured. Sloppy structure = sloppy thinking.

MECE Examples

MECE (Good)

Segmenting customers by age:

  • 0-17 years
  • 18-34 years
  • 35-54 years
  • 55+ years

No overlap. No gaps. Every person fits in exactly one bucket.

NOT MECE (Bad)

Segmenting customers by "type":

  • Young professionals
  • Families
  • Millennials
  • High-income earners

A 30-year-old high-income millennial with kids fits in ALL four buckets. Not mutually exclusive, not useful.

Issue Trees: The Core Consulting Tool

An issue tree is a visual breakdown of a problem into sub-questions. It is the backbone of every consulting project and every case interview. Think of it as a decision tree for problem-solving.

Issue Tree Structure
Core Question
├── Branch 1 (Sub-question A)
│   ├── Sub-branch 1a
│   └── Sub-branch 1b
├── Branch 2 (Sub-question B)
│   ├── Sub-branch 2a
│   └── Sub-branch 2b
└── Branch 3 (Sub-question C)
    ├── Sub-branch 3a
    └── Sub-branch 3b

Top-Down vs Bottom-Up Approach

Top-Down (Hypothesis-Driven)

Start with a hypothesis (educated guess) about the answer, then build an issue tree to prove or disprove it. This is the preferred approach in consulting.

Example: "I believe the client's profit decline is driven by rising raw material costs" => Build tree to test cost drivers.

When to use: When you have enough context to form an initial view. Most consulting projects work this way.

Bottom-Up (Discovery-Driven)

Start by gathering data and evidence broadly, then let the patterns emerge to form your hypothesis. More exploratory.

Example: "I don't know why profits are declining, so let me analyze all revenue streams and cost categories first."

When to use: When the problem is truly ambiguous or you are in a completely new industry. Less efficient but sometimes necessary.

Practice: MECE Breakdowns

Practice 1: "Why is a retail store losing customers?" ▶

MECE Breakdown (Internal vs External):

Internal Factors (within the store's control):

  • Product: Assortment issues (wrong products, out-of-stock, quality decline, lack of new products)
  • Price: Prices too high vs competitors, fewer promotions/discounts
  • Experience: Poor customer service, store cleanliness, checkout speed, layout confusion
  • Location/Access: Parking issues, store hours, inconvenient location

External Factors (outside the store's control):

  • Competition: New competitor entered the market, existing competitors improved
  • Customer Behavior: Shift to online shopping, demographic changes in the area
  • Macro Environment: Economic recession reducing spending, regulatory changes

This is MECE because every possible reason fits in exactly one bucket, and together the buckets cover all possibilities.

Practice 2: "How can an airline increase profitability?" ▶

MECE Breakdown (Revenue vs Costs):

Increase Revenue:

  • Ticket Revenue: Increase ticket prices, improve yield management (dynamic pricing), increase load factor (fill more seats)
  • Ancillary Revenue: Baggage fees, seat selection fees, in-flight food/drinks, loyalty program partnerships, cargo revenue
  • Network Expansion: Add new routes, increase frequency on high-demand routes, code-sharing agreements

Reduce Costs:

  • Fuel Costs: Hedging strategies, more fuel-efficient aircraft, route optimization
  • Labor Costs: Crew scheduling optimization, renegotiate contracts, automation (check-in kiosks)
  • Maintenance: Standardize fleet (fewer aircraft types), predictive maintenance
  • Airport/Landing: Negotiate better gate fees, use secondary airports
  • Overhead: Shared services, IT consolidation, real estate optimization
Practice 3: "Why are employees leaving a company?" ▶

MECE Breakdown (Push vs Pull factors):

Push Factors (reasons to leave current employer):

  • Compensation: Base pay below market, poor bonus structure, limited equity/benefits
  • Career Development: No promotion path, limited learning opportunities, no mentorship
  • Work Environment: Toxic culture, poor management, excessive hours, lack of flexibility/remote work
  • Role Satisfaction: Boring work, misalignment with skills, lack of impact/meaning

Pull Factors (reasons to join another employer):

  • Better Offers: Higher comp, better title, more equity at startups/competitors
  • Industry/Market: Hot job market, recruiters actively poaching, industry shift to new sectors
  • Personal: Relocation for family, career change desire, entrepreneurship

This is MECE: every reason for leaving is either a push (something bad about current job) or pull (something attractive elsewhere), and together they cover all possible reasons.

Knowledge Check: MECE & Structure

Which of the following is a MECE way to segment a company's customers?

Small business, enterprise, loyal customers, new customers
Customers with annual revenue < $10M, $10M-$100M, $100M-$1B, > $1B
Tech companies, healthcare, fast-growing companies, Fortune 500
B2B customers, B2C customers, digital customers
Option B is MECE: revenue bands are mutually exclusive (a company can only be in one band) and collectively exhaustive (every possible revenue level is covered). Option A mixes size with loyalty. Option C overlaps (a Fortune 500 tech company fits two buckets). Option D overlaps (a B2B customer can also be digital).

What does "hypothesis-driven" mean in a consulting context?

You guess the answer and present it without validation
You wait until all data is collected before forming any opinion
You form an initial educated answer on Day 1, then systematically test it with data and analysis
You let the client tell you the answer and then confirm it with data
Hypothesis-driven means forming an initial view (Day 1 hypothesis) and then rigorously testing it. This is more efficient than open-ended exploration because it focuses the team's analysis on proving/disproving the hypothesis rather than boiling the ocean.

The Profitability Framework

Mark Complete

The profitability framework is the most common case type in consulting interviews and the most common project type in real consulting work. Roughly 40% of all case interviews involve some form of profitability analysis. Master this framework, and you have a strong foundation for all other case types.

Core Profitability Equation
Profit = Revenue - Costs

Revenue = Price x Volume
  By Segment: Revenue = Sum of (Price_i x Volume_i) for each product/segment/geography

Costs = Fixed Costs + Variable Costs
  By Category: COGS + Labor + Overhead + SG&A + Other

Revenue Side Deep-Dive

Revenue Decomposition Tree
Total Revenue
├── Product Line A
│   ├── Price per unit (has it changed?)
│   └── Volume (number of units sold, has it changed?)
├── Product Line B
│   ├── Price per unit
│   └── Volume
├── Product Line C
│   ├── Price per unit
│   └── Volume
└── Other Revenue (services, licensing, etc.)

Key questions to ask on the revenue side:

  • Has the decline been across all products/segments, or concentrated in one area?
  • Is it a price issue (prices went down) or a volume issue (selling fewer units)?
  • If volume is down, is it because of fewer customers, or existing customers buying less?
  • Has the mix shifted toward lower-margin products?
  • Are there geographic differences?

Cost Side Deep-Dive

Fixed vs Variable View

Fixed Costs (don't change with volume): Rent, salaries, insurance, depreciation, interest payments

Variable Costs (change with volume): Raw materials, direct labor per unit, packaging, shipping, sales commissions

Key insight: If volume dropped but fixed costs stayed the same, per-unit costs increased mechanically.

Functional Category View

COGS: Raw materials, direct labor, manufacturing overhead

SG&A: Sales force, marketing, admin, office costs

R&D: Product development, engineering

Other: Depreciation, interest, one-time charges

Full Worked Case: Restaurant Chain Profitability

Case Prompt

"Your client is FastBite, a national fast-casual restaurant chain with 200 locations across the United States. The CEO has hired you because profits have declined by 20% over the past 2 years, from $50M to $40M. Revenue is $500M. Diagnose the problem and recommend a fix."

Step 1: Clarify and Structure ▶

Ask clarifying questions first:

  • "When you say profits, do you mean operating profit or net income?" => Operating profit.
  • "Has revenue changed over the 2 years?" => Revenue has been roughly flat at ~$500M.
  • "Has the number of stores changed?" => Grew from 180 to 200 stores over 2 years.
  • "Is this an industry-wide trend or specific to FastBite?" => Competitors are doing fine. FastBite-specific issue.

Key insight from clarification: Revenue is flat but profits dropped 20%. This means the problem is on the COST side (costs went up), not the revenue side. But let's verify by quantifying both sides.

Structure the framework:

Profitability Breakdown
Profit = Revenue - Costs
Year 1: $50M = $500M Revenue - $450M Costs
Year 2: $40M = $500M Revenue - $460M Costs

Costs increased by $10M (from $450M to $460M). The question is: WHERE did costs increase?
Step 2: Analyze Revenue (Confirm Flat) ▶

Even though the interviewer said revenue is flat, dig one level deeper:

MetricYear 1 (180 stores)Year 2 (200 stores)Change
Total Revenue$500M$500M0%
Revenue per Store$2.78M$2.50M-10%
Average Transaction Size$12.50$12.00-4%
Transactions per Store per Day610571-6.4%

Insight: Total revenue is flat only because 20 new stores were added. On a per-store basis, revenue is down 10%. Each store is seeing fewer customers AND smaller orders. This means the revenue side IS part of the problem, just masked by new store openings.

Common Mistake

Many candidates would skip the revenue analysis because "revenue is flat." Always look at same-store (comparable) metrics. Total revenue can mask declining performance if the company is opening new locations.

Step 3: Analyze Costs (Identify the Drivers) ▶

Now break down the $10M cost increase:

Cost CategoryYear 1Year 2Change% of Revenue
Food Costs (COGS)$150M (30%)$160M (32%)+$10M+2pp
Labor$125M (25%)$130M (26%)+$5M+1pp
Rent & Occupancy$60M (12%)$65M (13%)+$5M+1pp
Marketing$25M (5%)$20M (4%)-$5M-1pp
G&A / Other$90M (18%)$85M (17%)-$5M-1pp
Total Costs$450M (90%)$460M (92%)+$10M+2pp

Key findings:

  • Food costs increased by $10M: This is the biggest driver. Food cost as a % of revenue went from 30% to 32%. Why? Possible reasons: commodity price increases, supplier contract renegotiation, menu mix shift, food waste increase.
  • Labor increased by $5M: Partly driven by 20 new stores (each needing staff) + minimum wage increases in several states.
  • Rent increased by $5M: Driven by 20 new stores. On a per-store basis, rent is flat, so this is just the cost of expansion.
  • Marketing was CUT by $5M: This may explain the declining same-store revenue. Less marketing => fewer customers per store.
Step 4: Root Cause Diagnosis ▶

The story is becoming clear. Three interrelated problems:

  1. Aggressive expansion without sufficient demand: Added 20 stores (+11%), but same-store revenue declined 10%. The new stores are cannibalizing existing store traffic and aren't in strong enough locations.
  2. Food cost inflation not passed through: Commodity costs rose (beef, chicken, avocado up 15-20% over 2 years), but menu prices only increased ~2%. The client absorbed the cost increase rather than passing it to customers.
  3. Marketing budget cut to fund expansion: The $5M marketing cut likely contributed to the same-store traffic decline. Less awareness = fewer customers walking in.
Quantifying the Impact
Total profit decline: -$10M
  Food cost inflation absorbed: ~-$7M (commodity prices up, menu prices flat)
  New store drag (underperforming): ~-$5M (20 stores below breakeven in year 1)
  Same-store traffic decline: ~-$3M (fewer transactions)
  Offset by: G&A savings +$5M
  Net impact: -$10M ✓
Step 5: Recommendations ▶

Recommendation 1: Implement a strategic price increase (Impact: +$5-7M)

  • Increase menu prices by 4-6% over the next 6 months, phased in gradually.
  • Focus increases on items with low price elasticity (drinks, sides, specialty items).
  • Benchmark: competitors have raised prices 5-8% over the same period, so FastBite has room.
  • Expected volume impact: -1 to -2% (fast-casual customers are relatively price-inelastic).
  • Net revenue impact: +$20-25M in revenue, dropping ~$5-7M to the bottom line after variable costs.

Recommendation 2: Renegotiate supplier contracts and optimize food costs (Impact: +$3-4M)

  • Consolidate suppliers (currently using 15 different food suppliers; reduce to 5-7).
  • Lock in 12-18 month fixed-price contracts for key commodities.
  • Implement waste reduction program: data shows 8% food waste vs 5% industry benchmark.
  • Simplify the menu: remove the 10 lowest-selling items to reduce ingredient complexity.

Recommendation 3: Pause expansion and reinvest in same-store growth (Impact: +$3-5M)

  • Halt new store openings for 12 months until existing stores stabilize.
  • Restore marketing budget to $25M (add back the $5M that was cut).
  • Invest in loyalty program to increase repeat visits (target: increase visits per customer from 2.1x/month to 2.5x/month).
  • Close or relocate the 3-5 worst-performing stores that are unlikely to break even.

Total estimated profit impact: +$11-16M, restoring profitability to above Year 1 levels within 18 months.

How to Present This in an Interview

"Based on my analysis, FastBite's profit decline is driven by three factors: food cost inflation that wasn't passed through to customers, aggressive expansion that diluted same-store performance, and a marketing cut that reduced customer traffic. I recommend three actions: first, raise menu prices 4-6% to recover food cost inflation; second, consolidate suppliers and reduce waste to save $3-4M; third, pause expansion and reinvest in same-store marketing. Together, these should add $11-16M to profits within 18 months, more than recovering the $10M decline."

Profitability Framework Quiz

A company's revenue grew 5% but profits declined. What is the most likely explanation?

The company is losing customers
Costs grew faster than 5%, outpacing revenue growth
The company lowered prices
The market is shrinking
If revenue grew 5% but profits declined, costs must have grown by MORE than 5%. This is the only mathematical explanation. Revenue grew, so customers and prices aren't necessarily the issue — the issue is that costs outpaced revenue growth.

In the FastBite case, what was the most critical "hidden" insight?

Food costs were too high
Marketing was cut
Labor costs increased
Total revenue was flat, but same-store revenue was down 10% — masked by new store openings
The most critical insight was that flat total revenue masked a 10% same-store decline. This changes the entire analysis: the problem isn't just costs — it's also revenue. Many candidates would accept "revenue is flat" at face value and miss this completely.

Market Entry Framework

Mark Complete

Market entry cases ask: "Should Company X enter Market Y?" This requires assessing whether the market is attractive, whether the company can win, and how they should enter. It is the second most common case type after profitability.

The Market Entry Framework

│   ├── Size (how big is the prize?)
│   ├── Growth rate (is it growing?)
│   ├── Profitability (are players making money?)
│   └── Trends (where is the market heading?)
├── 2. Competitive Landscape
│   ├── Porter's Five Forces analysis
│   ├── Key competitors & their strengths
│   └── Market concentration & barriers to entry
├── 3. Company Capabilities
│   ├── Do we have the right skills/assets?
│   ├── Brand transferability
│   └── Financial resources
├── 4. Entry Mode
│   ├── Organic (build from scratch)
│   ├── Acquisition (buy a player)
│   ├── Joint Venture / Partnership
│   └── Licensing / Franchising
└── 5. Financial Analysis
    ├── Required investment
    ├── Expected revenues & timeline
    ├── Breakeven analysis
    └── ROI vs alternatives

Porter's Five Forces is the gold standard framework for analyzing industry competitiveness. It tells you whether an industry is structurally attractive (high profits) or structurally challenging (low profits).

Full Worked Case: US Coffee Chain Entering India

Case Prompt

"Your client is BrewCraft, a US-based specialty coffee chain with 800 stores in the US and $2B in annual revenue. The CEO wants to expand internationally and is considering India as the first market. Should BrewCraft enter India, and if so, how?"

Step 1: Market Attractiveness ▶

Market Size: The Indian coffee market is approximately $2.5B and growing at 12-15% annually. The cafe/specialty coffee segment is ~$800M and growing faster at 20% annually, driven by urbanization and a young population.

Growth Drivers:

  • India has 1.4B people with a median age of 28 — the key coffee-drinking demographic
  • Rising disposable income: India's middle class is projected to double from 300M to 600M people by 2035
  • Cultural shift: younger Indians are moving from tea to coffee, especially in urban areas
  • Cafe culture is booming: cafes are social gathering points, co-working spaces, and status symbols

Market Profitability: Existing players (Cafe Coffee Day, Starbucks India, Blue Tokai) operate at 8-15% margins. The specialty/premium segment commands higher margins (15-20%) due to willingness to pay.

Assessment: Market is highly attractive. Large, fast-growing, profitable, with strong tailwinds.

Step 2: Competitive Landscape (Five Forces) ▶
ForceAssessmentImplication
Threat of New EntrantsMedium-High. Low capital required for a single cafe, but scaling requires significant investment. Brand matters in specialty coffee.Expect continued new entrants, but brand will be a moat at scale.
Supplier PowerLow. India is a major coffee producer (6th globally). Abundant local supply. Many suppliers.Favorable. Can source locally at good prices.
Buyer PowerMedium. Customers have many choices but are willing to pay premium for experience and brand.Differentiation is key. Must offer more than just coffee.
SubstitutesMedium. Tea is deeply embedded in Indian culture. Home coffee (instant) is cheap. Street-side chai is everywhere.Position as "experience" not just "beverage" to differentiate from substitutes.
Competitive RivalryMedium. Cafe Coffee Day (1,700+ stores), Starbucks India (350+ stores), Blue Tokai, Third Wave. But market is large and growing fast enough for multiple winners.Room to win but need a differentiated value proposition.

Assessment: Industry is moderately attractive. Not a winner-take-all market, but differentiation and execution will determine success.

Step 3: Company Capabilities ▶

Strengths to leverage:

  • Brand: BrewCraft has strong US brand recognition. "American coffee" has aspirational appeal for Indian consumers.
  • Supply Chain Expertise: Deep coffee sourcing relationships (can source Indian beans directly for freshness advantage).
  • Store Design & Experience: Proven ability to create "third place" environment. BrewCraft stores are known for ambiance.
  • Menu Innovation: Strong R&D team that can adapt menu to local tastes.

Gaps to address:

  • Local Market Knowledge: No experience operating in India. Consumer preferences, real estate, labor markets, regulations are all different.
  • Price Positioning: US prices ($5-7 per drink) won't work. Need to adapt to Indian purchasing power ($2-4 range for premium).
  • Regulatory Complexity: India has complex FDI regulations, land use laws, and food safety requirements.
  • Supply Chain Localization: Need local dairy, bakery, and food suppliers. Cold chain infrastructure is less developed.
Step 4: Entry Mode Selection ▶

Evaluate the four entry modes:

ModeProsConsRecommendation
Organic (build own stores)Full control, full profitsVery slow, expensive, no local knowledgeNot recommended alone
Acquire a local chainInstant scale, local knowledge, real estateHigh cost, integration risk, may not align with brandPossible but risky
Joint Venture with local partnerLocal knowledge, shared risk, regulatory navigationShared control, potential partner conflictsRECOMMENDED
FranchiseLow capital, fast scaleLow control over quality, brand riskNot recommended for premium brand

Recommended: Joint Venture with a local partner (e.g., Tata Group, Reliance Retail, or a local hospitality company). This mirrors the Starbucks model — Starbucks entered India as a 50/50 JV with Tata Group. The local partner provides real estate access, regulatory expertise, supply chain relationships, and cultural insights.

Step 5: Financial Analysis & Recommendation ▶
Financial Projections (5-Year)
Investment Required: $50M over 5 years (BrewCraft's 50% share of JV)

Phase 1 (Year 1-2): Open 25 stores in Mumbai, Delhi, Bangalore
  Revenue per store: $400K/year (lower than US due to pricing)
  Store-level margin: 15% => $60K profit per store
  Total: 25 stores x $60K = $1.5M store profit - $5M overhead = -$3.5M

Phase 2 (Year 3-4): Expand to 75 stores, enter Tier-2 cities
  Revenue per store: $500K (brand awareness growing)
  Store margin: 18% (scale benefits) => $90K per store
  Total: 75 stores x $90K = $6.75M store profit - $4M overhead = +$2.75M

Phase 3 (Year 5): 150 stores, approaching scale
  Revenue per store: $550K
  Store margin: 20% => $110K per store
  Total: 150 stores x $110K = $16.5M store profit - $5M overhead = +$11.5M

Cumulative 5-year: -$3.5M + $2.75M + $11.5M = ~$10.75M cumulative profit
Breakeven: Year 3-4
5-Year ROI on $50M investment: ~20% (including terminal value of growing business)
Final Recommendation

Yes, BrewCraft should enter India through a 50/50 Joint Venture with a major Indian conglomerate. The rationale:

  1. The market is large ($2.5B), fast-growing (12-15%), and structurally attractive
  2. BrewCraft's brand, experience design, and supply chain capabilities are transferable
  3. A JV model de-risks entry by leveraging local expertise
  4. Financial returns are attractive: breakeven in Year 3-4, 20%+ ROI over 5 years

Key risks to monitor: Currency fluctuation (rupee depreciation), regulatory changes, partner alignment, execution speed of real estate buildout.

Market Sizing

Mark Complete

Market sizing (also called "estimation" or "guesstimate") questions test your ability to break down an ambiguous question into logical components and arrive at a reasonable estimate. These appear in virtually every consulting interview. There is no "right" answer — interviewers evaluate your structure, logic, and ability to sanity-check.

Two Approaches

Top-Down Approach

Start with a large, known number and narrow down.

Structure: Total population => Segment that is relevant => % that uses the product => Average spend

Best for: Sizing entire markets, national-level questions.

Risk: Each filtering step introduces error that compounds.

Bottom-Up Approach

Start with a single unit and scale up.

Structure: Revenue per location => Number of locations. Or: price per unit x units per customer x number of customers.

Best for: Sizing specific businesses, revenue questions.

Risk: Assumptions about the "unit" may not be representative.

Market Sizing Practice Problems

Problem 1: How many golf balls fit in a school bus? ▶

Approach: Volume estimation

Step 1: Estimate the volume of a school bus.

  • A school bus is roughly 35 feet long, 6 feet wide (interior), and 6 feet tall (interior).
  • Convert to inches: 420 in x 72 in x 72 in = 2,177,280 cubic inches.
  • Account for seats and other objects taking up space. Seats, driver area, and engine compartment reduce usable volume by roughly 25%.
  • Usable volume: 2,177,280 x 0.75 = ~1,633,000 cubic inches.

Step 2: Estimate the volume of a golf ball.

  • A golf ball has a diameter of about 1.68 inches.
  • Volume of a sphere = (4/3) x pi x r^3 = (4/3) x 3.14 x (0.84)^3 = ~2.48 cubic inches.

Step 3: Account for packing efficiency.

  • Spheres don't pack perfectly. Random packing fills about 64% of the space.
  • Effective volume per golf ball: 2.48 / 0.64 = ~3.88 cubic inches.

Step 4: Calculate.

Calculation
Number of golf balls = Usable bus volume / Effective volume per ball
= 1,633,000 / 3.88
= ~420,000 golf balls

Sanity check: This is a commonly cited interview question with answers typically ranging from 300,000 to 500,000. Our estimate of ~420,000 is right in the middle of the expected range.

Problem 2: What is the market size for baby diapers in the US? ▶

Approach: Top-down, demand-based

Step 1: How many babies/toddlers are in diapers?

  • US births per year: ~3.6 million.
  • Children wear diapers from birth to about age 2.5 (30 months).
  • Total children in diapers at any time: 3.6M x 2.5 = ~9 million children.

Step 2: How many diapers does a child use per day?

  • Newborns (0-3 months): ~10 diapers/day
  • Infants (3-12 months): ~8 diapers/day
  • Toddlers (12-30 months): ~6 diapers/day
  • Weighted average: roughly 7 diapers/day

Step 3: Calculate total diapers consumed annually.

Calculation
Total diapers per year = 9 million children x 7 diapers/day x 365 days
= 9M x 2,555
= ~23 billion diapers per year

Step 4: Convert to market size in dollars.

  • Average price per diaper: ~$0.30 (ranges from $0.15 for store brands to $0.45 for premium brands like Pampers/Huggies).
  • Market size = 23 billion x $0.30 = ~$6.9 billion

Sanity check: Published US diaper market size estimates are typically in the $7-9 billion range. Our estimate of ~$7B is very reasonable. The difference likely comes from premium/eco-friendly diapers commanding higher prices and some rounding.

Problem 3: How many gas stations are in the US? ▶

Approach: Demand-based, bottom-up

Step 1: How much gasoline does the US consume?

  • ~280 million registered cars in the US.
  • Not all are driven daily. Assume ~80% are actively driven = 224 million active cars.
  • Average miles driven per year: ~13,500 miles.
  • Average fuel efficiency: ~25 miles per gallon.
  • Gallons per car per year: 13,500 / 25 = 540 gallons.
  • Total US gasoline consumption: 224M x 540 = ~121 billion gallons per year.

Step 2: How much gas does one gas station sell?

  • A typical gas station has 4-8 pumps. Let's say 6 pumps on average.
  • Each pump serves roughly 1 car every 8 minutes during operating hours (about 16 hours/day).
  • Cars per pump per day: (16 hours x 60 minutes) / 8 minutes = 120 cars per pump, but utilization is maybe 40% = ~48 cars per pump/day.
  • Cars per station per day: 48 x 6 pumps = 288 cars/day.
  • Average fill-up: ~10 gallons.
  • Gallons per station per day: 288 x 10 = 2,880 gallons.
  • Gallons per station per year: 2,880 x 365 = ~1.05 million gallons.

Step 3: Calculate number of stations.

Calculation
Number of gas stations = Total US consumption / Gallons per station per year
= 121 billion / 1.05 million
= ~115,000 gas stations

Sanity check: The actual number of gas stations in the US is approximately 145,000-150,000. Our estimate of ~115,000 is in the right ballpark. The gap is likely because many gas stations in rural areas have lower throughput than our average assumes, so more stations are needed to serve the same total demand.

Problem 4: What is the revenue of a single Starbucks store? ▶

Approach: Bottom-up, transaction-based

Step 1: Estimate transactions per day by daypart.

  • Morning rush (6 AM - 10 AM): Peak period. ~4 hours. Maybe 2 transactions per minute = 480 transactions.
  • Late morning (10 AM - 12 PM): Moderate. ~2 hours. Maybe 1 transaction per minute = 120 transactions.
  • Lunch/Afternoon (12 PM - 5 PM): Steady. ~5 hours. Maybe 0.8 transactions per minute = 240 transactions.
  • Evening (5 PM - 9 PM): Slower. ~4 hours. Maybe 0.5 transactions per minute = 120 transactions.
  • Total transactions per day: ~960 transactions. Let's round to ~500 on a typical day (weekdays average lower; not all locations are equally busy).

Step 2: Estimate average transaction size.

  • Average drink price: ~$5.50
  • ~30% of customers also buy food (average food item: $3.50)
  • Average transaction = $5.50 + (0.30 x $3.50) = $5.50 + $1.05 = ~$6.55
  • Round to ~$6.50 per transaction

Step 3: Calculate annual revenue.

Calculation
Daily revenue = 500 transactions x $6.50 = $3,250
Annual revenue = $3,250 x 365 days = ~$1,186,000

Approximately $1.2 million per store per year.

Sanity check: You can cross-check with a top-down approach. Starbucks US revenue is approximately $19-20B from about 16,000 US stores. That gives $19B / 16,000 = ~$1.2M per store. Our bottom-up estimate matches perfectly.

Problem 5: How large is the US wedding industry? ▶

Approach: Top-down, demand-based

Step 1: How many weddings happen per year in the US?

  • US population: 330 million. Roughly 50% are adults of "marrying age" (20-65) = 165 million.
  • Marriage rate: approximately 6.5 per 1,000 population (total population) = 330M x 6.5/1,000 = ~2.1 million marriages per year.
  • Alternatively: ~2 million weddings per year is a commonly cited figure.

Step 2: What does the average wedding cost?

Break it down by category:

CategoryAverage Cost% of Budget
Venue & Catering$12,00036%

Step 4: Sanity check and adjustments.

  • This estimate covers direct wedding spending. If we include the engagement ring market (~$7B), bridal showers, bachelor/bachelorette parties, and wedding-adjacent travel, the total ecosystem is closer to $80-90B.
  • Published estimates for the US wedding industry typically range from $70-80B for direct wedding spending.
  • Our estimate of ~$70B is well within the expected range.
Market Sizing Best Practices
  • Always state your assumptions: "I'm going to assume the US population is 330 million..." This lets the interviewer correct you if needed.
  • Round aggressively: Use 300M instead of 330M if it makes the math easier. The interviewer cares about your logic, not your arithmetic.
  • Sanity check at the end: Does your answer feel right? Per-capita, per-household, or compared to known benchmarks?
  • Show your structure first: Before calculating, lay out the components: "I'll calculate this as Number of X times Average Y per X."
  • Segment when possible: Breaking into segments (e.g., urban vs rural, age groups) shows sophistication and often gives a more accurate answer.

M&A Cases in Consulting

Mark Complete

M&A cases in consulting are different from investment banking M&A. In consulting, the question is: "Should our client acquire this target?" from a strategic perspective. In banking, the question is: "What is the target worth and how do we structure the deal?" Consulting M&A focuses on strategy and synergies; banking M&A focuses on valuation and execution.

The Consulting M&A Framework

M&A Decision Framework
Should Client acquire Target X?
├── 1. Strategic Rationale (WHY buy?)
│   ├── What capability/market does the target provide?
│   ├── How does this fit the client's strategy?
│   └── Is acquisition the best way to get this? (vs build/partner)
├── 2. Target Assessment (WHAT are we buying?)
│   ├── Financial health (revenue, margins, growth)
│   ├── Competitive position (market share, moat)
│   ├── Management team & culture
│   └── Customer base & contracts
├── 3. Synergies (WHAT value can we create?)
│   ├── Revenue synergies (cross-sell, new markets)
│   ├── Cost synergies (eliminate duplicates, scale)
│   └── Timeline to realize synergies
├── 4. Valuation (HOW MUCH to pay?)
│   ├── Standalone value
│   ├── + Synergy value
│   ├── - Integration costs
│   └── = Maximum price to pay
└── 5. Integration Risks
    ├── Cultural integration
    ├── Technology integration
    ├── Customer retention
    └── Key employee retention

Synergies Deep-Dive

Revenue Synergies (Harder to achieve)
  • Cross-selling: Sell Target's products to Client's customers (and vice versa)
  • Geographic expansion: Use Target's presence in new markets
  • Bundle/upsell: Create combined product offerings
  • Pricing power: Larger combined entity can command higher prices

Rule of thumb: only 30-50% of projected revenue synergies are typically achieved.

Cost Synergies (Easier to achieve)
  • Headcount reduction: Eliminate duplicate roles (finance, HR, IT, management)
  • Procurement: Combined purchasing power for better supplier terms
  • Facilities: Consolidate offices, warehouses, data centers
  • Technology: Standardize on one platform, eliminate redundant tools

Rule of thumb: 70-80% of projected cost synergies are typically achieved.

Operations & Cost Reduction

Mark Complete

Operations cases focus on improving efficiency: reducing costs, increasing throughput, optimizing supply chains, and streamlining processes. These cases test your ability to think about physical processes, capacity, and bottlenecks rather than just strategy.

Key Operations Concepts

Lean Principles

Lean manufacturing/operations focuses on eliminating waste. The 7 types of waste (TIMWOOD):

  1. Transportation — unnecessary movement of materials
  2. Inventory — excess stock sitting idle (ties up cash)
  3. Motion — unnecessary movement of people
  4. Waiting — idle time between steps
  5. Overproduction — making more than demand requires
  6. Overprocessing — doing more than the customer values
  7. Defects — errors that require rework or scrap

Pricing Strategy

Mark Complete

Pricing is one of the most powerful profit levers. A 1% increase in price (holding volume constant) typically increases operating profit by 8-11%, depending on the industry. Despite this, many companies underinvest in pricing strategy.

Three Core Pricing Approaches

ApproachDescriptionProsConsBest For
Cost-Plus Price = Cost + Markup %. Set a target margin on top of your cost. Simple, guarantees margin, easy to justify Ignores customer willingness to pay, leaves money on the table Commodity products, government contracts, cost-driven industries
Value-Based Price based on the economic value the customer receives, not your cost. Captures more value, aligns with customer perception, higher margins Hard to quantify value, requires deep customer understanding Differentiated products, B2B, pharma, luxury, SaaS
Competitive Price based on what competitors charge. Match, undercut, or premium. Easy to implement, market-validated Race to the bottom, ignores your unique value Commoditized markets, new entrants, price-sensitive segments

Price Elasticity of Demand

Price Elasticity
Price Elasticity = % Change in Quantity Demanded / % Change in Price

|Elasticity| > 1 = "Elastic" (quantity is sensitive to price; small price increase = big volume drop)
|Elasticity| < 1 = "Inelastic" (quantity is NOT sensitive to price; can raise prices without losing much volume)

Examples:
  Insulin: Elasticity ~ -0.1 (extremely inelastic — patients need it regardless of price)
  Gasoline: Elasticity ~ -0.3 (inelastic — people still need to drive)
  Restaurant meals: Elasticity ~ -1.5 (elastic — people will eat at home or switch restaurants)
  Airline tickets (leisure): Elasticity ~ -1.8 (very elastic — people skip vacations if too expensive)

Advanced Pricing Tactics

  • Bundling: Selling multiple products together at a discount vs individual prices. Increases total revenue per customer and reduces price sensitivity. Example: Microsoft Office 365 bundle vs buying Word, Excel, PowerPoint separately.
  • Tiering: Offering Good/Better/Best options. The middle option becomes the "anchor" and most customers choose it. Example: SaaS pricing (Basic $29, Pro $79, Enterprise $199 per month).
  • Freemium: Free basic version, paid premium version. Acquires users cheaply, then converts a percentage to paid. Typical conversion: 2-5%. Example: Spotify, Dropbox, Zoom.
  • Dynamic Pricing: Adjusting prices in real-time based on demand, time, or customer segment. Example: Uber surge pricing, airline ticket pricing, hotel room rates.
  • Penetration Pricing: Launch at a low price to gain market share quickly, then raise prices over time. Example: Amazon Prime's initial $79/year (now $139).
  • Price Skimming: Launch at a high price, then lower over time as demand from early adopters is exhausted. Example: New iPhone launches at $1,099, drops to $999 after 6 months.

Worked Case: Pharma Pricing Strategy

Case Prompt

"Your client is NovaCure Pharmaceuticals. They have developed a breakthrough drug for Type 2 diabetes that has just received FDA approval. In clinical trials, the drug reduced HbA1c levels by 2.1% vs 1.3% for the current standard of care (Metformin + a DPP-4 inhibitor). It also showed a 25% reduction in cardiovascular events. How should they price this drug?"

Full Case Solution ▶

Step 1: Understand the market.

  • ~37 million Americans have Type 2 diabetes. The addressable patient population (moderate-to-severe, not controlled by Metformin alone) is about 8 million.
  • Current standard of care (Metformin + DPP-4 inhibitor like Januvia) costs ~$400/month ($4,800/year).
  • GLP-1 drugs (competing class, e.g., Ozempic) are priced at $800-1,000/month.
  • The Type 2 diabetes drug market in the US is approximately $40B annually.

Step 2: Value-based pricing analysis.

Value Quantification
Clinical Superiority:
  HbA1c reduction: 2.1% vs 1.3% (62% better)
  Cardiovascular risk reduction: 25%

Economic Value of CV Risk Reduction:
  Average cost of a cardiovascular event (heart attack, stroke): ~$50,000
  Annual CV event risk in Type 2 diabetes patients: ~5%
  25% risk reduction => 1.25 percentage points lower risk per year
  Value per patient per year: $50,000 x 1.25% = $625 in avoided CV costs

Total value vs standard of care:
  Better efficacy + $625/year in avoided CV events

Step 4: Pricing recommendation.

  • Floor: $600/month ($7,200/year) — must be above DPP-4 inhibitors given superior efficacy.
  • Ceiling: $1,200/month ($14,400/year) — can justify a premium over Ozempic given superior HbA1c reduction and CV benefit.
  • Recommended price: $850-950/month ($10,200-$11,400/year)

Rationale: Price at a modest premium to GLP-1s (Ozempic at $900/month), reflecting superior clinical outcomes. The 25% CV risk reduction is a strong differentiator that insurers will value (it saves them money on hospitalizations).

Step 5: Market access strategy.

  • Insurer negotiations: Offer outcomes-based contracts: if real-world CV event rates don't match clinical trials, NovaCure rebates part of the cost. This reduces payer risk.
  • Tiering strategy: Aim for Tier 2 formulary status (preferred brand). Offer PBM rebates of 25-30% off list price to secure favorable formulary placement.
  • Patient assistance: Co-pay card program capping out-of-pocket costs at $25/month for commercially insured patients. This removes the price barrier at the patient level.
  • Net price after rebates: ~$600-650/month (still profitable given estimated COGS of $50-75/month).
Recommendation Summary

Launch at a list price of $900/month ($10,800/year), positioning slightly above Ozempic. Secure formulary access through 25-30% PBM rebates (net price ~$630/month). Differentiate on the CV risk reduction data — this is the key selling point to both physicians and payers. Projected peak annual revenue: $8M addressable patients x 15% market share x ~$7,500 net annual revenue = ~$9B peak US revenue.

Communication & Presentation

Mark Complete

Communication is as important as analysis in consulting. A brilliant insight that isn't communicated clearly is worthless. Consulting firms spend enormous effort training people to write clearly, present persuasively, and structure messages logically.

The Pyramid Principle (Barbara Minto)

Core Idea: Answer First, Then Support

The Pyramid Principle says: start with the answer (the conclusion), then provide the supporting arguments, then provide the evidence for each argument. This is the opposite of how most people communicate (which is: background => analysis => conclusion).

The pyramid structure:

Pyramid Structure
Level 1: THE ANSWER (one sentence, the key message)
  ├── Level 2: Supporting Argument 1
  │   ├── Level 3: Evidence/Data for Argument 1
  │   └── Level 3: More Evidence for Argument 1
  ├── Level 2: Supporting Argument 2
  │   ├── Level 3: Evidence for Argument 2
  │   └── Level 3: More Evidence for Argument 2
  └── Level 2: Supporting Argument 3
      ├── Level 3: Evidence for Argument 3
      └── Level 3: More Evidence for Argument 3
Bad Communication (Bottom-Up)

"We analyzed the market data and found that growth has slowed from 12% to 4%. We also looked at competitor pricing and found that three new entrants have undercut our prices by 15%. Our customer satisfaction scores have dropped from 4.2 to 3.6. Additionally, our cost structure has increased due to raw material inflation. Given all of this, we believe the client should consider a strategic repositioning."

The audience doesn't know where this is going until the last sentence. By then, they've already checked their phone.

Good Communication (Pyramid)

"The client should reposition from a cost leader to a premium provider. Three factors support this: First, the low-cost market is structurally declining as three new entrants have created a price war. Second, our customer research shows premium customers are underserved and willing to pay 30% more. Third, our brand strength in quality gives us permission to move upmarket. Let me walk you through each factor."

The audience knows the answer immediately and can follow the logic. Even if they only hear the first sentence, they got the key message.

The SCQA Framework

SCQA (Situation, Complication, Question, Answer) is a storytelling framework used to set up a presentation or document. It creates narrative tension that engages the audience.

ElementWhat It DoesExample (FastBite Case)
SituationStates something the audience already knows and agrees with. Establishes common ground."FastBite has grown from 100 to 200 stores over the past 5 years and is one of the leading fast-casual chains in the US."
ComplicationIntroduces the problem or change that disrupts the situation. Creates tension."However, despite this growth, operating profit has declined 20% from $50M to $40M over the past 2 years."
QuestionThe logical question that the complication raises. (Often implicit.)"What is driving the profit decline, and how can FastBite restore profitability?"
AnswerYour recommendation. The resolution to the tension."The decline is driven by food cost inflation, aggressive expansion, and marketing cuts. We recommend a three-part plan: price increases, supplier consolidation, and a growth pause — projected to add $11-16M in profits."

Slide Design Principles

The One-Message-Per-Slide Rule

Every consulting slide should convey exactly ONE message. The structure:

  • Title (Action Title): A complete sentence that IS the message. Not "Revenue Analysis" but "Revenue per store declined 10% despite flat total revenue due to new store openings."
  • Body: The evidence that supports the title. Charts, tables, bullet points — whatever proves the title is true.
  • Source: Where the data came from (bottom of slide).

The test: if someone reads ONLY the titles of your deck, they should be able to understand the entire storyline.

Bad Slide Title

"Revenue Breakdown by Segment"

This is a topic, not a message. It tells the audience what the slide is about, but not what they should learn from it.

Good Slide Title

"Enterprise segment drives 70% of revenue growth; SMB segment has stalled since Q3"

This IS the insight. The reader immediately knows the key takeaway.

The "So What?" Test

After every analysis, every slide, every data point, ask: "So what?" This test forces you to connect facts to implications and implications to actions.

Applying the "So What?" Test

Fact: "Customer churn increased from 5% to 8% this year."

So what? "This means we lost an additional $15M in recurring revenue."

So what? "If this trend continues, we'll miss our growth target by $45M over 3 years."

So what? "We need to invest $3M in a customer success program that can bring churn back to 5%, which would yield a 5x return on investment."

Keep asking "so what?" until you reach an actionable recommendation. Facts without implications are useless in consulting.

Executive Communication Tips

  • Lead with the answer: Executives are busy. Tell them the conclusion first. If they want details, they'll ask.
  • Use the Rule of 3: Group your supporting points into 3 arguments. The human brain processes groups of 3 easily: "There are three reasons..." is more compelling than "There are seven reasons..."
  • Quantify everything: "Revenue will increase significantly" is weak. "Revenue will increase by $25M (12%) within 18 months" is strong.
  • Anticipate questions: Before presenting, think: "What will the CFO ask? What will the CEO challenge? What's the weakest part of my argument?" Prepare answers in advance.
  • Be concise: If you can say it in 10 words, don't use 30. Cut jargon, cut filler, cut repetition.

What does MECE stand for and why does it matter?

Maximally Effective, Comprehensively Evaluated — it ensures analysis quality
Market Entry, Competitive Evaluation — it's a market analysis framework
Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive — it ensures no overlaps and no gaps in your problem breakdown
Management Engineering, Cost Efficiency — it's an operations framework
MECE (Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive) is the foundational structuring principle. Mutually Exclusive means no overlaps (nothing counted twice). Collectively Exhaustive means no gaps (nothing missed). This ensures your problem breakdown is complete and logically sound.

A company's revenue grew 10% but profit margin declined from 15% to 12%. Which is the most likely root cause?

The company lost market share
Costs grew faster than revenue, possibly due to mix shift toward lower-margin products or cost inflation
The company raised prices too much
Competitors entered the market
Revenue grew but margins shrank. This means costs grew even faster than revenue (more than 10%). Likely culprits: cost inflation, mix shift to lower-margin products/customers, diseconomies of scale during rapid growth, or acquisition of a lower-margin business.

When should you recommend a Joint Venture over an acquisition for market entry?

When speed is the #1 priority
When you need full control over operations
When the target market is small and easily manageable
When you need local expertise, want to share risk, or face regulatory barriers that require a local partner
JVs are best when you need a local partner for regulatory, cultural, or expertise reasons. They share risk but also share control. Acquisitions are better for speed and control. JVs are the preferred mode for entering markets like India, China, and the Middle East where local knowledge is critical.

What is the most impactful profit lever for most companies?

A 1% price increase (typically increases operating profit by 8-11%)
A 1% volume increase
A 1% reduction in fixed costs
A 1% reduction in variable costs
Price is the strongest profit lever because price increases flow directly to the bottom line (no associated cost increase). A 1% price increase typically boosts operating profit by 8-11%. A 1% volume increase is less impactful because it comes with variable costs. Cost reductions also help but are harder to sustain.
