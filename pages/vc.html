What Is Venture Capital?

Mark Complete

Venture capital (VC) is a form of private equity financing provided to early-stage, high-potential startup companies in exchange for equity ownership. Unlike traditional lending or public-market investing, VC targets companies that are pre-profit (often pre-revenue) but possess the potential for exponential growth. The fundamental bet in venture capital is that a small number of portfolio companies will generate outsized returns that more than compensate for the many that fail.

The Core VC Thesis

Venture capital exists because traditional financial institutions (banks, public markets) cannot efficiently fund high-risk, high-reward startups. Banks require collateral and cash flow; public markets require a track record. VC fills this gap by providing patient capital to companies building the future, accepting that most investments will fail in exchange for the possibility of 100x+ returns on winners.

How a VC Fund Works

A venture capital fund is structured as a limited partnership with a finite life, typically 10 years with optional 1-2 year extensions. The mechanics are straightforward but the implications are profound:

VC Fund Lifecycle Example

Acme Ventures Fund III — $200M Fund

  • Years 1-3 (Investment Period): The GP deploys capital into 20-30 companies. Initial checks of $2-8M each, reserving 50-60% for follow-on investments in winners.
  • Years 3-5 (Building Period): Portfolio companies grow, some fail, the GP makes follow-on investments in the strongest performers. The GP provides board guidance, helps with recruiting, and facilitates introductions.
  • Years 5-7 (Early Harvest): Some companies begin to exit through acquisitions. The portfolio shape becomes clearer — 2-3 companies emerge as potential fund-makers.
  • Years 7-10+ (Harvest): Major exits occur through IPOs or large acquisitions. The GP distributes proceeds to LPs. Remaining companies are either exited or written off.

The Power Law

The single most important concept in venture capital is the power law distribution of returns. Unlike normal distributions where outcomes cluster around the mean, VC returns follow a power law where a tiny fraction of investments generate the vast majority of returns.

Power Law in Practice

In a typical VC fund of 30 investments:

  • 10-15 companies (33-50%): Will fail completely — return $0 or pennies on the dollar
  • 5-10 companies (17-33%): Will return approximately 1-3x invested capital — "the living dead" that survive but don't generate meaningful returns
  • 3-5 companies (10-17%): Will return 3-10x — solid performers that contribute to fund returns
  • 1-2 companies (3-7%): Will return 10-50x+ — these are the fund-makers that drive the entire fund's performance

The top-performing investment in a great fund often returns more than all other investments combined. This is why VCs cannot afford to play it safe — they need to swing for the fences on every investment.

Power Law Example: A $100M Fund

Imagine a $100M fund that makes 25 investments of $4M each:

The VC Ecosystem

The venture capital landscape is composed of many different types of investors, each playing a distinct role at different stages:

  • Angel Investors: High-net-worth individuals who invest $25K-$500K of personal capital in very early-stage companies. Often former founders or executives with domain expertise. They move fast and can write checks based on personal conviction alone.
  • Accelerators/Incubators: Programs like Y Combinator, Techstars, and 500 Startups that invest small amounts ($125K-$500K) for 5-10% equity, combined with mentorship and community. YC's batch model has produced some of the most valuable startups in history.
  • Micro VCs / Pre-Seed Funds: Funds under $50M that focus on the earliest institutional round. They often take the first board seat and work closely with founders before product-market fit.
  • Seed Funds: Dedicated seed-stage funds ($50M-$250M) that lead seed rounds of $1-4M. They typically aim for 10-15% ownership and may or may not take board seats.
  • Series A Specialists: Funds that focus primarily on leading Series A rounds. This is considered the most competitive stage because it's the first round where institutional diligence is rigorous. Firms like Benchmark, Union Square Ventures, and Foundry Group historically focused here.
  • Multi-Stage Funds: Large platforms like Andreessen Horowitz, Sequoia, and Lightspeed that invest across seed through growth. They have dedicated teams for each stage and can support companies throughout their lifecycle. Fund sizes often exceed $1B.
  • Growth Equity: Late-stage investors (General Atlantic, Insight Partners, Tiger Global) who invest in proven companies scaling rapidly. Check sizes range from $25M to $200M+. Lower risk than early-stage but also lower potential multiples.
  • Corporate Venture Capital (CVC): Investment arms of large corporations (Google Ventures, Intel Capital, Salesforce Ventures) that invest for both financial returns and strategic value. They can offer startups distribution partnerships, technical expertise, and acquisition potential.
  • Crossover Funds: Public market investors (Fidelity, T. Rowe Price, mutual funds) that participate in late-stage private rounds, typically pre-IPO. They provide validation of public market readiness and can become anchor investors in IPOs.
  • Sovereign Wealth Funds: Government-backed investment vehicles (Temasek, GIC, Saudi PIF, Mubadala) that increasingly participate in large growth rounds and late-stage investments.

Career Tip

If you're recruiting for VC, understand that the job varies dramatically by fund stage. At a seed fund, you'll evaluate hundreds of early-stage companies and focus on founder quality and market potential. At a growth fund, you'll build detailed financial models and conduct rigorous due diligence on proven businesses. Know which type excites you and target accordingly.

Q: In a typical VC fund, what drives the majority of total returns?

Correct: C. The power law dictates that VC returns are driven by a tiny number of outsized winners. The single best investment in a top-performing fund often returns more than all other investments combined. This is why VCs seek companies with the potential for massive scale, not just moderate success.

Startup Valuation Methods

Mark Complete

Valuing startups is fundamentally different from valuing mature companies. Traditional DCF models fall apart when a company has no cash flows, no revenue, and sometimes no product. Instead, the VC ecosystem has developed a toolkit of valuation approaches, each suited to different stages and situations.

Pre-Money and Post-Money Valuation

These are the foundational concepts of startup financing. Every term sheet, every fundraise, every cap table depends on understanding these two numbers.

Core Valuation Formulas

Post-Money Valuation = Pre-Money Valuation + Investment Amount

Investor Ownership % = Investment Amount / Post-Money Valuation

Pre-Money Valuation = Post-Money Valuation - Investment Amount

Price Per Share = Pre-Money Valuation / Pre-Money Shares Outstanding

Worked Example: Series A Fundraise

TechCo is raising a $10M Series A. The lead investor proposes a $30M pre-money valuation.

  • Pre-Money Valuation: $30M
  • Investment Amount: $10M
  • Post-Money Valuation: $30M + $10M = $40M
  • Investor Ownership: $10M / $40M = 25%

If TechCo had 10M shares outstanding pre-money:

  • Price Per Share: $30M / 10M shares = $3.00/share
  • New Shares Issued: $10M / $3.00 = 3,333,333 shares
  • Total Post-Money Shares: 10M + 3.33M = 13,333,333 shares
  • Verification: 3.33M / 13.33M = 25% ownership. Correct.

The Option Pool Shuffle

Investors almost always require that an employee option pool be created or expanded before the investment (included in the pre-money valuation). This effectively lowers the true pre-money valuation for existing shareholders.

Example: A $30M pre-money with a 15% option pool expansion means the founders' effective pre-money is only $25.5M (85% of $30M). The remaining $4.5M in pre-money value is allocated to the unissued option pool. Always negotiate whether the option pool comes from the pre-money or post-money calculation — this has a material impact on founder dilution.

Comparable Transactions Method

The most commonly used method for seed through growth-stage companies. You identify recent fundraising rounds of comparable startups and use their valuations as benchmarks.

Worked Example: Comp-Based Valuation

You're valuing a Series A SaaS company with $2M ARR growing 200% YoY.

Step 1: Identify comparable recent rounds:

CompanyStageARR at RoundGrowth RatePost-MoneyARR Multiple
Comp ASeries A$1.5M180%$45M30x
Comp BSeries A$2.5M250%$100M40x
Comp CSeries A$1.8M150%$36M20x
Comp DSeries A$3M200%$75M25x

Step 2: Select appropriate multiple range. Median multiple is 27.5x ARR. Given 200% growth (above median comps), a 25-35x range is reasonable.

Step 3: Apply to target. $2M ARR x 25-35x = $50M - $70M post-money valuation.

Berkus Method

Developed by angel investor Dave Berkus, this method assigns up to $500K of value for each of five key risk factors. Best suited for pre-revenue startups where financial metrics don't exist yet. The maximum pre-money valuation under this method is $2.5M.

DCF for Startups

Why DCF Usually Doesn't Work for Early-Stage Startups

Traditional DCF analysis requires forecasting future cash flows — something nearly impossible for a pre-revenue startup where the range of outcomes spans from $0 to billions. The discount rate for early-stage companies would need to be extremely high (40-70%+), making distant cash flows nearly worthless. Additionally, terminal value dominates the calculation, and estimating terminal value for an unproven company is speculative at best.

When DCF can work: For Series B+ companies with meaningful revenue, established unit economics, and a clearer growth trajectory, a DCF can provide a useful cross-check on market-based valuations. Even then, it should be one data point among many, not the primary valuation method.

Revenue Multiples for Later-Stage

For growth-stage companies with significant revenue, forward revenue multiples become the primary valuation tool. The appropriate multiple depends on growth rate, market size, unit economics, and market conditions.

Revenue Growth RateTypical ARR Multiple (NTM)Stage
>200% YoY30-80x+ NTM RevenueSeries A-B hypergrowth
100-200% YoY20-40x NTM RevenueSeries B-C
50-100% YoY10-25x NTM RevenueSeries C-D / Growth
30-50% YoY8-15x NTM RevenueLate-stage / Pre-IPO

Multiple Compression

Multiples compress significantly in risk-off environments. A company that might command 40x ARR in a hot market might only get 15-20x in a downturn. Always consider the macro environment when using comp-based multiples, and stress-test valuations across different multiple scenarios.

Q: A startup raises $5M at a $15M pre-money valuation with a 10% option pool created pre-money. What is the effective pre-money valuation for existing shareholders?

Correct: B. The 10% option pool is carved out of the pre-money valuation: $15M x 90% = $13.5M effective pre-money for existing shareholders. The remaining $1.5M of pre-money value is allocated to the unissued option pool. Post-money is $20M, the investor gets 25% ($5M/$20M), the option pool gets 10%, and existing shareholders effectively own 65%.

Term Sheets — Complete Breakdown

Mark Complete

The term sheet is the most critical document in a VC financing. While not legally binding (except for the no-shop clause and confidentiality), it sets the framework for the definitive legal agreements. A term sheet typically runs 5-10 pages and covers economic terms, control terms, and other provisions.

The Two Things That Matter Most

Experienced investors and founders agree: the two most important terms in any deal are valuation (how much of the company you give up) and board composition (who controls the company). Everything else is important but secondary. Founders who optimize for valuation while giving up board control often regret it later.

Economic Terms

Valuation and Price

The term sheet specifies the pre-money valuation, which combined with the investment amount determines the post-money valuation and investor ownership. It also specifies the price per share, share class (typically "Series A Preferred Stock"), and the total shares to be issued.

Option Pool

As discussed in the valuation section, investors typically require that the company set aside 10-20% of fully diluted shares for a new or expanded employee option pool. The critical negotiation point is whether this pool is included in the pre-money or post-money calculation.

Liquidation Preference

This is arguably the most impactful economic term after valuation. Liquidation preference determines who gets paid first, and how much, when the company is sold or goes through a liquidation event (acquisition, dissolution, or sometimes IPO).

1x Non-Participating Preferred (Most Common / Founder-Friendly)

Investors get the greater of: (a) 1x their invested capital back, or (b) their pro-rata share of proceeds as if converted to common stock.

Example: $10M invested for 25% ownership

Scenario 1: $30M exit

  • Option A (preference): Investor gets $10M back (1x), remaining $20M goes to other shareholders
  • Option B (convert): Investor gets 25% x $30M = $7.5M
  • Investor chooses Option A: $10M (preference is better)
  • Founders/others split: $20M

Scenario 2: $100M exit

  • Option A (preference): Investor gets $10M
  • Option B (convert): Investor gets 25% x $100M = $25M
  • Investor chooses Option B: $25M (conversion is better)
  • Founders/others: $75M

The breakeven point where conversion equals preference: $10M / 25% = $40M exit value. Above $40M, the investor converts; below $40M, they take their preference.

1x Participating Preferred (Investor-Friendly / "Double Dip")

Investors get 1x their money back FIRST, then ALSO participate pro-rata in the remaining proceeds alongside common stockholders. This is sometimes called "double dipping" because the investor gets their money back and then shares in the upside.

Example: $10M invested for 25% ownership

Scenario 1: $30M exit

  • Step 1 — Preference: Investor gets $10M off the top
  • Step 2 — Remaining: $30M - $10M = $20M
  • Step 3 — Participation: Investor gets 25% x $20M = $5M additional
  • Investor total: $10M + $5M = $15M (50% of proceeds for 25% ownership!)
  • Founders/others: $15M

Scenario 2: $100M exit

  • Step 1 — Preference: $10M
  • Step 2 — Remaining: $90M
  • Step 3 — Participation: 25% x $90M = $22.5M
  • Investor total: $10M + $22.5M = $32.5M
  • Founders/others: $67.5M

Compared to non-participating, the investor gets $32.5M vs. $25M at a $100M exit. Participating preferred always outperforms non-participating — the extra cost comes entirely from founders/common shareholders.

Capped Participating Preferred (Compromise)

Participating preferred with a cap on total returns (typically 2-3x). The investor participates until they've received the cap amount, then their preferred converts to common and they participate only on a pro-rata basis going forward.

Example: $10M invested, 25% ownership, 3x cap on participation

$30M exit: Preference ($10M) + participation (25% x $20M = $5M) = $15M. Under 3x cap ($30M), so full participation applies. Investor gets $15M.

$100M exit: Participating would yield $32.5M, but that's above the 3x cap ($30M). Converting to common gives 25% x $100M = $25M. Investor takes the better of cap ($30M) or conversion ($25M) = $30M.

$200M exit: Cap gives $30M, conversion gives 25% x $200M = $50M. Investor converts: $50M.

Anti-Dilution Protection

Anti-dilution provisions protect investors if the company raises a subsequent round at a lower valuation (a "down round"). The protection works by adjusting the conversion price of the investor's preferred stock, effectively giving them more common shares upon conversion.

Full Ratchet vs. Weighted Average Anti-Dilution

Full Ratchet: The conversion price is reduced to the price of the new (lower) round, regardless of how many shares are issued. This is extremely punitive to founders and rarely used in competitive markets.

Broad-Based Weighted Average: The standard approach. The conversion price is adjusted based on a weighted average that considers both the old price and the new price, weighted by the number of shares at each price. The formula:

New Conversion Price = Old Price x [(Outstanding Shares + New Money / Old Price) / (Outstanding Shares + New Shares Issued)]

Or equivalently:

NCP = OCP x (A + B) / (A + C)

Where: A = shares outstanding pre-round, B = shares that would have been issued at old price, C = shares actually issued at new price

Anti-Dilution Example

Series A investor bought at $2.00/share. Company does a down round at $1.00/share, issuing 5M new shares. 10M shares were outstanding before the down round.

Full Ratchet: Conversion price drops from $2.00 to $1.00. Investor's shares effectively double.

Weighted Average:

  • A (shares outstanding) = 10M
  • B (money raised / old price) = $5M / $2.00 = 2.5M
  • C (new shares issued) = 5M
  • NCP = $2.00 x (10M + 2.5M) / (10M + 5M) = $2.00 x 12.5/15 = $1.667

Under weighted average, the conversion price drops to $1.667 (not $1.00). Much less dilutive to founders than full ratchet.

Dividends

Most VC term sheets include a dividend provision, but in practice, dividends are rarely paid to startup investors. The two main structures:

  • Non-cumulative dividends: "If and when declared by the Board." Essentially meaningless — the board never declares them because the company reinvests all cash into growth.
  • Cumulative dividends: Accrue at a set rate (typically 6-8% annually) and are added to the liquidation preference. A $10M investment with 8% cumulative dividends would have a liquidation preference of $14.7M after 5 years. This is investor-friendly and increases over time.

Control Terms

Board Composition

Board composition determines who controls the company. Typical structures:

  • Seed/Early: 3-person board — 2 founders, 1 investor (or 2 founders, 1 independent)
  • Series A: 5-person board — 2 founders, 2 investors, 1 independent mutually agreed
  • Series B+: 5-7 person board — composition shifts as more investors join; founders may become minority

The independent board member is often the most important seat. Whoever controls the selection of the independent director effectively controls the board. Negotiate this carefully.

Protective Provisions (Investor Veto Rights)

Protective provisions require investor consent (usually a majority or supermajority of the preferred class) for certain company actions. Standard protective provisions include:

  • Issuing shares senior to or on par with the existing preferred
  • Changing the certificate of incorporation or bylaws
  • Increasing or decreasing authorized share count
  • Selling the company or substantially all assets
  • Declaring or paying dividends
  • Taking on debt above a threshold (e.g., $500K)
  • Changing the board size
  • Liquidating or dissolving the company

Watch Out: Overly Broad Protective Provisions

Some investors try to add protective provisions that give them veto power over day-to-day operations, such as: hiring/firing key executives, annual budgets, contracts above a threshold, or changing business direction. These can effectively give a minority investor control of the company. Founders should push back on anything that goes beyond the standard list above.

Drag-Along Rights

Drag-along rights allow a specified majority (typically the board + a majority of preferred + a majority of common) to force all shareholders to vote in favor of a sale. This prevents minority shareholders from blocking an acquisition that the majority approves. Important for ensuring a clean exit.

Information Rights

Investors typically receive quarterly financial statements, annual audited financials, annual budgets, and monthly management reports. Major investors (those holding above a threshold, often $1M+ invested) get more detailed reporting. This is generally non-controversial.

Other Key Terms

Pro-Rata Rights (Right to Maintain Ownership)

Pro-rata rights allow existing investors to invest their proportional share in future rounds to maintain their ownership percentage. For example, if an investor owns 20% and the company raises a new round, they can invest enough to keep 20% ownership. This is highly valuable because it lets investors double down on winners.

Right of First Refusal (ROFR) and Co-Sale Rights

ROFR: The company (and sometimes investors) have the right to purchase shares before a founder or employee can sell them to a third party. This prevents unwanted shareholders from joining the cap table.

Co-Sale (Tag-Along): If a founder sells shares, investors can participate in the sale on a pro-rata basis at the same price and terms. This prevents founders from cashing out while investors remain locked in.

No-Shop Clause

One of the only legally binding provisions in the term sheet. It prevents the company from soliciting or entertaining competing offers for a specified period (typically 30-60 days) while the deal is being finalized. This protects the investor's time and legal expenses during due diligence.

Founder Vesting

Investors typically require that founder shares be subject to vesting, even for founders who have been working on the company for years. Standard is 4-year vesting with a 1-year cliff, sometimes with acceleration on certain events (change of control). If founders have been working for 2 years pre-investment, they might negotiate for 2 years of credit toward vesting.

Negotiation Tip: Pick Your Battles

Don't fight every term. Focus your negotiation energy on: (1) valuation, (2) board composition, (3) liquidation preference structure, and (4) the option pool size/placement. Accept market-standard terms for everything else. Fighting too many points signals inexperience and can damage the relationship with your investors.

Q: An investor puts in $10M for 20% of a company with 1x participating preferred (no cap). At a $40M exit, how much does the investor receive?

Correct: D. With 1x participating preferred: Step 1 — Investor receives $10M preference off the top. Step 2 — Remaining proceeds: $40M - $10M = $30M. Step 3 — Investor participates: 20% x $30M = $6M. Total: $10M + $6M = $16M. The investor receives 40% of a $40M exit despite owning only 20% — this is the "double dip" effect of participating preferred.

Cap Table Modeling

Mark Complete

The capitalization table (cap table) is the definitive record of a company's equity ownership structure. It tracks every shareholder, share class, option grant, warrant, and convertible instrument. Understanding cap tables is essential for anyone in VC — it's how you model dilution, returns, and exit scenarios.

Why Cap Tables Matter

The cap table answers the single most important question in startup finance: who owns what? Every fundraise, option grant, and secondary transaction changes the cap table. Mistakes compound over time and can create legal and financial headaches that derail future fundraising or exits.

Building a Cap Table Through Multiple Rounds

Full Worked Example: TechStartup Inc.

Founding (Day 0): Two co-founders split 10,000,000 shares equally.

Q: A founder owns 50% of a company pre-seed. After a seed round (20% to investors, 10% option pool) and a Series A (25% to investors, pool topped up to 15%), approximately what percentage does the founder own?

Then at Series A, the 36% is diluted further to ~27% (25% to new investors plus pool expansion). Dilution is cumulative and compounds with each round.

VC Fund Economics

Mark Complete

Understanding how a VC fund makes money is essential both for VC professionals and for founders negotiating with them. The economics of a fund shape VC behavior — why they push for large outcomes, how they allocate time across portfolio companies, and what return thresholds they need to hit.

Fee Structure: "2 and 20"

The standard VC fee structure mirrors hedge funds and PE: a management fee plus carried interest.

VC Fund Fee Structure

Management Fee: Typically 2-2.5% of committed capital per year during the investment period (years 1-5), then 2% of invested capital or net asset value during the harvest period (years 6-10). Pays for salaries, office, travel, and operations.

Carried Interest (Carry): 20-30% of net profits above the hurdle rate (if any). This is the GP's share of investment gains.

Hurdle Rate: Some funds require a minimum return to LPs (typically 8% IRR) before carry kicks in. Many top-tier VC funds have no hurdle — carry is calculated from the first dollar of profit.

GP Commit: GPs invest 1-5% of the fund alongside LPs, ensuring alignment of interests.

Fund Economics Example: $200M Fund

Management Fees (over 10-year fund life):

  • Years 1-5: 2% x $200M = $4M/year = $20M total
  • Years 6-10: Assume 1.5% on declining base, roughly $2M/year = $10M total
  • Total management fees: ~$30M

Investable Capital: $200M committed - $30M fees = $170M available to invest

This means 15% of LP capital goes to fees, not investments. In a $50M micro fund, fees consume an even larger percentage.

Carry Calculation (assuming 3x gross fund return):

  • Total distributions: $170M invested x 3x = $510M gross returns
  • Return to LPs first: $200M committed capital returned
  • Net profit: $510M - $200M = $310M
  • Carry (20% of profit): 20% x $310M = $62M to GP
  • Remaining to LPs: $310M - $62M = $248M
  • Total LP return: $200M capital + $248M profit = $448M = 2.24x net to LPs

Portfolio Construction

How a VC fund allocates its investable capital across companies is one of the most important strategic decisions.

Reserve Strategy

Most VC funds reserve 40-60% of their capital for follow-on investments in winning portfolio companies. A typical allocation:

  • Initial investments (40-60% of fund): First checks into new companies
  • Follow-on reserves (40-60% of fund): Pro-rata and additional investment in winners

The reserve strategy is critical because of the power law. When you identify a winner, you want to invest as much as possible. Running out of reserves when your best company is raising their next round is a costly mistake. Conversely, being too conservative with initial capital means you make fewer bets and reduce your chances of finding a winner.

Fund Math: Why VCs Need 3x+ Returns

Working Backwards from LP Expectations

LPs expect top-quartile VC funds to return 3x net (after fees and carry) over the fund's 10-year life, which translates to roughly 12-15% net IRR.

To deliver 3x net to LPs, what gross multiple does the fund need?

  • Fund size: $200M
  • Target net return to LPs: 3x = $600M
  • LP receives: $600M
  • GP carry (20% of profits): Let profit = P. LPs get 80% of P + $200M capital = $600M. So 0.8P = $400M, P = $500M.
  • GP carry: 20% x $500M = $100M
  • Total distributions needed: $600M + $100M = $700M
  • Investable capital (after fees): ~$170M
  • Required gross multiple: $700M / $170M = ~4.1x gross

So to deliver 3x net to LPs, the fund needs to generate approximately 4x gross on invested capital. This is why VCs are relentless about finding companies that can return 10-100x — they need those outliers to pull the overall portfolio to a 4x+ gross.

Vintage Year Returns

VC fund performance varies dramatically by "vintage year" (the year the fund begins investing). This is driven by several factors:

  • Entry valuations: Funds that invest during bubble periods pay high prices and often underperform
  • Exit environment: Funds that mature during strong IPO/M&A markets benefit from higher exit valuations
  • Technology cycles: Some vintages benefit from major platform shifts (mobile, cloud, AI)
  • Capital supply: When too much money chases too few deals, returns compress across the industry

Benchmark Reference

Historically, top-quartile US VC funds have returned approximately 2.5-3.5x net to LPs. Top-decile funds return 4-5x+ net. Median VC funds often struggle to return 1.5-2x net — barely covering LPs' opportunity cost. This is why LP selection of GPs (manager selection) is itself a critical skill. The spread between top-quartile and bottom-quartile VC is far wider than in most other asset classes.

Q: A $300M VC fund charges 2% management fee (years 1-10 for simplicity) and 20% carry. The fund returns $900M gross. How much carry do the GPs earn?

Correct: C. Total committed capital: $300M. Gross returns: $900M. Net profit: $900M - $300M = $600M. Carry: 20% x $600M = $120M. Note: management fees come from committed capital and are already accounted for in what's available to invest. Carry is calculated on the profit over committed capital returned to LPs.

Q: A SaaS company has $500K starting MRR, adds $200K in expansion, loses $30K to contraction, and $20K to churn. What is the Net Revenue Retention rate?

Correct: B. NRR = (Starting MRR + Expansion - Contraction - Churn) / Starting MRR = ($500K + $200K - $30K - $20K) / $500K = $650K / $500K = 130%. This means the existing customer base generated 30% more revenue than it did at the start of the period, even after accounting for customers who left or downgraded. A 130% NRR is considered excellent — it means the company would grow 30% annually even with zero new customers.

Due Diligence for Startups

Mark Complete

Due diligence is the investigation process that occurs after a VC decides they're interested in a company but before they commit capital. The depth and rigor of diligence varies by stage — a seed investment might involve a few days of research, while a growth-stage deal could take 4-8 weeks of intensive work.

Team Assessment

At the early stage, team assessment is the single most important diligence workstream. VCs evaluate:

  • Founder-Market Fit: Why are these specific founders uniquely positioned to win in this market? Do they have domain expertise, proprietary insights, or unfair advantages?
  • Technical Capability: Can the technical founders actually build the product? Review their track record, technical background, open-source contributions, and previous work.
  • Complementary Skills: Does the founding team cover the key functions needed? Typical ideal: a builder (CTO/technical), a seller (CEO/business), and a domain expert.
  • Resilience and Grit: Has the team navigated adversity before? Startups will inevitably face near-death experiences. Reference calls with former colleagues, co-founders, and managers are essential.
  • Coachability: Are the founders open to feedback and willing to adapt? Stubborn founders who can't take input from their board will struggle as the company scales.
  • Reference Checks: Back-channel references (people the founders did NOT provide as references) are often more revealing than provided references. Talk to former reports, peers, and managers.

Market Sizing: TAM, SAM, SOM

Market Sizing Framework

TAM (Total Addressable Market): The total revenue opportunity if the product achieved 100% market share globally. This represents the ceiling.

SAM (Serviceable Addressable Market): The portion of TAM that the company can realistically target given its business model, geography, and current product capabilities.

SOM (Serviceable Obtainable Market): The portion of SAM the company can realistically capture in the near term (3-5 years). This is what goes into the financial model.

Market Sizing Example: AI-Powered Legal Document Review

Top-Down Approach:

  • TAM: Global legal services market = $900B+. Legal document review is ~10% = $90B TAM.
  • SAM: US and UK corporate legal departments and law firms that use technology solutions = ~$20B.
  • SOM: Mid-market law firms (50-500 attorneys) adopting AI tools within 5 years = ~$3B.

Bottom-Up Approach (preferred by VCs):

  • Target customer: Mid-market law firm with 100-500 attorneys
  • Number of target firms in US: ~5,000
  • Average annual contract value: $100K-$300K
  • Realistic penetration in 5 years: 10-15% = 500-750 firms
  • SOM: 500 firms x $200K ACV = $100M ARR

VCs prefer bottom-up because it forces you to make specific, testable assumptions about customer count, pricing, and penetration. Top-down TAM numbers are easy to inflate and hard to verify.

Product-Market Fit Indicators

Product-market fit (PMF) is the degree to which a product satisfies strong market demand. VCs look for these signals:

  • Organic demand: Customers are finding the product without heavy sales/marketing spend
  • High retention: Users/customers keep coming back and expanding their usage
  • Word of mouth: Customers recommend the product unprompted (high NPS, k-factor > 0.5)
  • Pull vs. push: The company is struggling to keep up with demand (backlog, waitlist) rather than struggling to find customers
  • Low churn: Once customers adopt, they rarely leave
  • Expansion revenue: Existing customers naturally use more over time (NRR > 110%)
  • Short sales cycles: Deals close quickly because the need is urgent
  • Sean Ellis Test: >40% of users say they would be "very disappointed" if the product went away

Competitive Analysis

VCs evaluate the competitive landscape to understand:

  • Direct competitors: Who else is solving this exact problem? What are their strengths and weaknesses?
  • Indirect competitors: What workarounds do customers currently use (spreadsheets, manual processes, incumbent software)?
  • Incumbent threat: Could a large player (Google, Microsoft, Salesforce) build this? If so, what's the startup's moat?
  • Defensibility/Moat: Network effects, data advantages, switching costs, regulatory barriers, brand, proprietary technology
  • Competitive dynamics: Is the market winner-take-all, or can multiple players coexist? What determines who wins?

Technology Assessment

  • Architecture review: Is the technology built to scale? Technical debt assessment.
  • IP review: Patents, trade secrets, proprietary algorithms. Is the technology defensible?
  • Build vs. buy risk: Could a competitor replicate this technology quickly?
  • Security and compliance: SOC 2, HIPAA, GDPR compliance as relevant to the market.
  • Technical team quality: Engineering talent, development velocity, code quality.

Customer Reference Calls

Talking to actual customers is one of the most valuable diligence activities. VCs typically speak to 5-15 customer references, including both company-provided references and independently sourced ones. Key questions:

  • What problem does this product solve for you?
  • What would you use if this product didn't exist?
  • How has the product changed your workflow or outcomes?
  • What's your biggest complaint or wish for improvement?
  • Would you recommend this to a peer? Why or why not?
  • How does this compare to alternatives you've evaluated?
  • If the price doubled, would you still use it?

Financial Model Review

  • Revenue assumptions: Are growth projections realistic? Based on bottoms-up analysis or top-down aspiration?
  • Unit economics: Do the LTV/CAC ratios work? Will they improve or degrade at scale?
  • Cash flow projections: When does the company reach breakeven? How much capital will it need to get there?
  • Scenario analysis: What happens in bull, base, and bear cases?
  • Capital efficiency: How much revenue per dollar raised? Compared to peer companies?

Legal Due Diligence

  • Corporate structure: Cap table accuracy, previous round documentation, outstanding convertible notes/SAFEs
  • IP ownership: All IP properly assigned to the company (not held personally by founders)
  • Employment matters: Proper contractor vs. employee classification, offer letters, invention assignment agreements
  • Litigation: Any pending or threatened lawsuits
  • Regulatory: Compliance with relevant industry regulations
  • Material contracts: Key customer agreements, vendor dependencies, partnership agreements

Red Flags in Due Diligence

  • Founders who are evasive about metrics or provide inconsistent numbers
  • High customer concentration (one customer > 30% of revenue)
  • Key person risk with no succession plan
  • Messy cap table or undocumented prior investments
  • IP not properly assigned to the company
  • Founders who have not vested or have unusual equity arrangements
  • Customer references that are lukewarm or reveal issues not disclosed by the company
  • Cohort metrics that show declining quality (newer cohorts worse than older ones)

Q: Which market sizing approach do VCs generally prefer and why?

Correct: B. Bottom-up market sizing starts with specific customer segments, estimated customer counts, realistic pricing, and achievable penetration rates. It produces a more credible and defensible estimate because each assumption can be tested against real data. Top-down approaches (taking a percentage of a large market) are easy to manipulate and often produce unrealistically large numbers. VCs want both, but bottom-up carries more weight in their analysis.

Stages of VC Investing

Mark Complete

Each stage of venture financing corresponds to a different level of company maturity, risk, and investor expectations. Understanding what's expected at each stage is crucial for both founders timing their fundraise and VCs calibrating their evaluation criteria.

Pre-Seed

Pre-Seed Stage Profile

  • Company Status: Idea stage to early prototype. May have no product, no revenue, and sometimes no full-time team yet.
  • What Investors Evaluate: Founder quality (80%+ of the decision), market insight, initial vision. Can these founders build something people want?
  • Typical Round Size: $250K - $2M
  • Typical Valuation: $2M - $10M pre-money
  • Typical Investors: Angels, accelerators, pre-seed funds, friends and family
  • Instrument: Often SAFE notes or convertible notes (not priced equity)
  • Milestones to Next Stage: Build MVP, find initial users, validate core hypothesis

Seed

Seed Stage Profile

  • Company Status: Working product, early users/customers. May have initial revenue but not yet proven repeatable sales.
  • What Investors Evaluate: Product quality, early traction signals, founder-market fit, market size, team composition.
  • Typical Round Size: $1M - $5M
  • Typical Valuation: $5M - $20M pre-money
  • Typical Investors: Seed-stage VC funds, super angels, some multi-stage funds
  • Key Metrics: User growth, engagement, early retention, qualitative customer feedback, waitlist demand
  • Milestones to Next Stage: Demonstrate product-market fit, reach $500K-$2M ARR (for SaaS), build core team, establish repeatable sales motion

Series A

Series A Stage Profile

  • Company Status: Product-market fit achieved. Proven that customers want the product and will pay for it. Now need to scale the go-to-market engine.
  • What Investors Evaluate: Quantitative metrics (ARR, growth rate, retention, unit economics), market size validation, competitive positioning, scalability of the business model, team's ability to recruit.
  • Typical Round Size: $5M - $20M
  • Typical Valuation: $15M - $60M pre-money
  • Typical Investors: Series A-focused VCs, multi-stage funds
  • Key Metrics: $1M-$5M ARR, >100% YoY growth, positive unit economics (or clear path), <5% monthly churn, NRR >100%
  • Milestones to Next Stage: Scale to $5-15M ARR, prove go-to-market scalability, build management team, demonstrate path to Series B metrics

The Series A Crunch

The transition from Seed to Series A is the most difficult fundraising step. Thousands of companies raise seed funding, but the bar for Series A is dramatically higher. Estimates suggest only 15-25% of seed-funded companies successfully raise a Series A. The primary reason: inability to demonstrate clear product-market fit with quantitative evidence. VCs see hundreds of Series A pitches for every investment they make.

Series B

Series B Stage Profile

  • Company Status: Scaling. Product-market fit is clear, go-to-market is working, now the company needs capital to accelerate growth, expand to new segments, or enter new markets.
  • What Investors Evaluate: Revenue trajectory, unit economics at scale, market leadership position, management team depth, path to profitability (or sustainable growth).
  • Typical Round Size: $15M - $50M
  • Typical Valuation: $50M - $200M pre-money
  • Key Metrics: $5M-$20M ARR, >100% YoY growth, NRR >110%, LTV/CAC >3x, burn multiple <2x
  • Milestones to Next Stage: Reach $20-50M ARR, build executive team, establish market leadership, improve capital efficiency

Series C and Beyond

Late-Stage (Series C/D/E+) Profile

  • Company Status: Market leader or clear contender. Significant revenue, established brand, expanding into adjacent markets or geographies.
  • What Investors Evaluate: Market leadership, financial performance vs. public comps, path to IPO or large strategic exit, management team's public-company readiness.
  • Typical Round Size: $50M - $500M+
  • Typical Valuation: $200M - $10B+ pre-money
  • Key Metrics: $50M+ ARR, sustainable growth (50%+ YoY), improving margins, approaching profitability or FCF positive
  • Typical Investors: Growth equity firms, crossover funds (public market investors), sovereign wealth funds, late-stage specialists

Q: At which stage is quantitative product-market fit (measurable ARR, retention, unit economics) typically expected?

Correct: C. Series A is where VCs expect quantitative evidence of product-market fit: measurable ARR ($1-5M), demonstrated retention (NRR, churn), and at least directional unit economics (LTV/CAC). At seed, qualitative signals of PMF may be sufficient. Pre-seed is primarily about the team and the idea. By Series B/C, PMF should be well-established and the focus shifts to scaling and efficiency.

VC Deal Sourcing & Decision Making

Mark Complete

Understanding how VCs find, evaluate, and ultimately decide on investments is critical knowledge — whether you're recruiting into VC, building a startup, or working alongside venture-backed companies.

How VCs Source Deals

Deal flow — the pipeline of potential investments — is the lifeblood of a VC firm. The best firms see the best deals early, often before other firms. Sources include:

  • Warm referrals from portfolio founders (highest quality): Founders who have been funded by a VC often refer the best entrepreneurs in their network. These referrals convert at the highest rate because they come pre-vetted.
  • Other VCs (co-investment referrals): VCs who lead a round often invite other trusted VCs to co-invest. This is a major source of deal flow for firms that are not leading rounds themselves.
  • Accelerator demo days: Programs like Y Combinator, Techstars, and others showcase batches of startups. Top VCs attend or review every batch.
  • Inbound from founders: Cold outreach from founders. Quality varies enormously but some breakout companies do come through inbound channels.
  • Proactive thesis-driven sourcing: VCs develop investment theses about emerging markets and proactively seek companies building in those spaces. This involves attending conferences, reading research, and building relationships in target sectors.
  • Talent tracking: Following exceptional engineers, product leaders, and executives who leave big companies to start their own ventures.
  • Angel/scout networks: Many VCs employ scouts — experienced operators who source deals in exchange for a small carry allocation or per-deal economics.

The Investment Process

While every firm operates differently, most follow a variation of this pipeline:

Stage 1: Sourcing and Initial Screening

Volume: A typical VC partner might see 500-1000+ companies per year through various channels.

Initial filter: Within minutes, the partner decides whether the company warrants a meeting. Filters include: market relevance, stage fit, founder background, competitive landscape, and whether the company fits the fund's thesis.

Pass rate: 80-90% of inbound are passed on at this stage. The most common reasons: wrong stage, wrong sector, insufficient differentiation, or team doesn't seem strong enough.

Stage 2: First Meeting

Format: 30-60 minute meeting (often virtual). The founder presents the company, and the VC asks probing questions.

What VCs assess: Founder's ability to articulate the problem, market insight, product differentiation, early traction, team, and vision. First impressions of founder quality matter enormously.

Outcome: If interested, the VC requests a follow-up with additional data (metrics, deck, financial model). Of the companies that get a first meeting, roughly 20-30% move forward.

Stage 3: Deep Dive / Partner Meeting

Duration: 1-4 weeks of deeper work.

Activities: Multiple meetings with the full founding team, product deep dive/demo, metric review, market analysis, reference calls (both provided and backchannel), competitive analysis.

Internal: The sponsoring partner presents the deal to other partners, gauges interest, and identifies concerns. Some firms have formal "partner meetings" where the deal is discussed at length.

Pass rate: About 50% of companies that reach this stage move forward to the next.

Stage 4: Investment Committee (IC)

Format: Formal presentation to the full partnership. The sponsoring partner presents the investment thesis, opportunity, risks, and proposed terms. Other partners challenge assumptions and identify risks.

Decision framework: Firms vary — some require unanimous consent, others majority vote, and some give individual partners allocation they can deploy independently.

Key questions: Can this be a fund-returner? What's the realistic path to 10x+? What could kill this company? Is now the right time to invest? Are we the right partner for this company?

Outcome: Approve, reject, or conditional approval (pending additional diligence on specific concerns).

Stage 5: Term Sheet and Negotiation

Timing: If approved, the VC issues a term sheet, typically within 24-72 hours of IC approval. Speed matters — competitive deals can be lost to faster-moving firms.

Negotiation: Key terms (valuation, board composition, liquidation preference, option pool) are negotiated. Most other terms are market-standard and generate little debate.

Exclusivity: Once signed, the no-shop clause prevents the company from soliciting competing offers for 30-60 days.

Stage 6: Closing

Duration: 2-6 weeks after term sheet signing.

Activities: Lawyers draft definitive legal agreements (Stock Purchase Agreement, Investors' Rights Agreement, Right of First Refusal, Voting Agreement, Certificate of Incorporation). Legal diligence is completed. Final business diligence items are resolved.

Closing: Documents are signed and funds are wired. The investor typically joins the board or takes a board observer seat.

The Investment Memo

The investment memo is the internal document that makes the case for (or against) an investment. It's the primary artifact of the VC decision-making process and a skill that every VC analyst and associate must master.

Anatomy of an Investment Memo

  1. Executive Summary: 2-3 sentences on what the company does, why it matters, and the proposed investment.
  2. Company Overview: Product description, business model, stage, key metrics.
  3. Market Opportunity: TAM/SAM/SOM analysis, market trends, timing thesis.
  4. Team: Founder backgrounds, key hires, team gaps, reference call takeaways.
  5. Product & Technology: What makes it differentiated? How defensible is the technology?
  6. Traction & Metrics: Revenue, growth, retention, unit economics. Cohort analysis.
  7. Competition: Competitive landscape, positioning, moat analysis.
  8. Business Model & Unit Economics: How the company makes money, LTV/CAC, margins, path to profitability.
  9. Financial Projections: 3-5 year financial model with bull/base/bear cases.
  10. Deal Terms: Proposed valuation, round size, key terms, cap table impact.
  11. Return Analysis: Expected return scenarios at various exit valuations. How does this investment perform in the context of the fund?
  12. Key Risks: Honest assessment of what could go wrong. Mitigation strategies for each risk.
  13. Investment Recommendation: Clear recommendation with rationale.

What Makes a VC Say "Yes"

After analyzing thousands of investment decisions, the factors that consistently drive positive investment outcomes include:

The Investment Decision Framework

Must-Have Criteria (any deal-breakers = pass):

  • Exceptional founding team with relevant experience and demonstrated ability to execute
  • Large addressable market ($1B+ TAM for early-stage; proven for later-stage)
  • Clear product differentiation — why will customers choose this over alternatives?
  • Business model that can scale — path to significant revenue with improving unit economics
  • Reasonable valuation relative to stage and traction

Strong Positive Signals (not required but increase conviction):

  • Founder has deep personal connection to the problem (lived the pain)
  • Product already has organic traction without heavy marketing spend
  • Strong network effects or data moats that compound over time
  • Customers are so enthusiastic they serve as evangelists
  • Market timing is right — structural tailwinds (regulatory change, technology shift, behavioral change)
  • Proprietary distribution advantage (built-in audience, channel partnerships, viral mechanics)
  • Capital efficient — can achieve significant milestones with modest funding

The "Regret Minimization" Test

Many top VCs use a version of this mental model: "If I pass on this company and it becomes a massive success, how much will I regret it?" The best investments are the ones where missing out would feel devastating — companies that are clearly going to reshape their industry. When a VC feels this level of conviction, they move fast and compete hard to win the deal.

Common Reasons VCs Pass
  • "Market too small" — The addressable market isn't large enough to support a venture-scale outcome
  • "Too early" — The company hasn't de-risked enough for the VC's stage preference
  • "Team concerns" — The team lacks key capabilities or references raised flags
  • "Competitive dynamics" — Incumbents or well-funded competitors make the path too difficult
  • "Valuation too high" — The price doesn't allow sufficient return potential
  • "Not my area of expertise" — The VC doesn't feel they can add enough value or evaluate the opportunity well
  • "Timing is wrong" — The market isn't ready yet, or the window has already closed
  • "Unit economics don't work" — The cost to acquire and serve customers is too high relative to revenue

Important: A "pass" is rarely personal. Most passed companies are good companies that simply don't fit the specific VC's mandate, stage focus, or return requirements. The best founders treat passes as data points and move on.

Q: Out of 1000 companies a VC partner might see in a year, approximately how many will the firm ultimately invest in?

Correct: D. Most VC firms invest in fewer than 1% of the companies they see. A typical firm making 4-8 new investments per year with 3-5 partners might invest in 5-10 companies out of the 2000-5000+ the firm collectively evaluates. The funnel is extremely selective: 1000 seen, 100-200 get a first meeting, 20-40 get a deep dive, 10-15 reach IC, and 5-10 receive a term sheet. The selectivity is what makes VC returns possible.
