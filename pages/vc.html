What Is Venture Capital?

Mark Complete

Venture capital (VC) is a form of private equity financing provided to early-stage, high-potential startups in exchange for equity ownership. Unlike banks (which require collateral and cash flow) or public markets (which require a track record and regulatory compliance), VC fills the financing gap by funding companies that are pre-profit and often pre-revenue but possess the potential for exponential growth.

The fundamental bet in venture capital is radically different from other asset classes: VCs accept that the majority of their investments will fail, because the small number of breakout winners generate returns large enough to compensate for all the losses and then some.

The Power Law

VC returns follow a power law distribution, not a normal (bell curve) distribution. In a typical fund of 25-30 investments:

  • 50-70% of investments will return 0-1x (total loss or break-even)
  • 15-25% will return 1-5x (modest winners)
  • 5-10% will return 5-20x (strong performers)
  • 1-3 investments will return 20-100x+ (the fund-makers)

The top 1-2 deals often return more than all other investments combined. This is why VCs seek companies that can return 10-100x, not 2-3x -- a 2x return on a single deal does almost nothing for the fund.

Fund Structure: GP / LP Model

A VC fund is structured as a limited partnership, identical in legal form to PE funds but with different economic dynamics:

RoleWho They AreCapital ContributionResponsibilities
General Partner (GP) The VC firm itself 1-5% of fund (GP commit) Investment decisions, deal sourcing, board seats, portfolio management, fundraising
Limited Partners (LPs) Endowments, pensions, SWFs, fund-of-funds, family offices 95-99% of fund Provide capital, no control over individual investment decisions, limited liability

Fund Lifecycle

A VC fund typically has a 10-year life with optional 1-2 year extensions, divided into distinct phases:

PhaseTimelineKey Activities
FundraisingYear 0-1GP raises commitments from LPs, holds first and final close
Investment PeriodYears 1-4Deploy capital into 20-30 startups via capital calls as deals close
Portfolio ManagementYears 2-8Follow-on investments in winners, board work, operational support, reserve management
HarvestingYears 5-10+Exits via M&A, IPO, or secondary sales; distributions returned to LPs

VC vs. Private Equity

While both are private capital strategies, VC and PE differ fundamentally in nearly every dimension:

DimensionVenture CapitalPrivate Equity
Company StageEarly-stage, high-growthMature, cash-flow-generating
Ownership StakeMinority (10-30% per round)Controlling (51-100%)

The VC Ecosystem

The modern VC ecosystem includes several interconnected players, each serving a different role in the startup lifecycle:

  • VC Firms: Sequoia, Andreessen Horowitz (a16z), Benchmark, Accel, Lightspeed, Founders Fund, Bessemer, Greylock
  • Angel Investors: High-net-worth individuals investing $25K-$500K at the earliest stages, often former founders themselves
  • Accelerators: Y Combinator, Techstars -- provide $125K-$500K plus mentorship for 5-10% equity in structured 3-month programs
  • Corporate VC (CVC): Google Ventures, Intel Capital, Salesforce Ventures -- strategic investors aligned with their parent company's business interests
  • Micro VCs: Sub-$100M funds focused on pre-seed/seed, often run by solo GPs or small teams
  • Crossover Funds: T. Rowe Price, Fidelity, Tiger Global -- public market investors who also do late-stage private rounds
VC Fund Return Outcomes: Illustrative Distribution ▶

Consider a hypothetical $100M fund that invests $3M in each of 25 companies ($75M deployed, $25M in fees):

Key Insight for Interviews

When asked "how is VC different from other investing?" the single most important answer is the power law. It drives every behavior in the VC industry: why VCs seek huge markets (small markets cannot produce fund-returning outcomes), why they prefer high growth over profitability (maximizing the upside of winners matters more than avoiding losses), and why they write small initial checks with large reserves (to double down on the companies showing breakout potential).

Why do VC returns follow a power law distribution rather than a normal distribution?

Because VCs invest equal amounts in every deal, so returns are evenly distributed
Because most startups generate moderate 2-3x returns that average out
Because most startups fail or return little, while a small number of winners generate outsized returns that dominate total fund performance
Because VC funds use leverage to amplify returns on every deal equally
VC returns are driven by the power law: the majority of investments return 0-1x, but the rare breakout winners (10-100x+) generate nearly all the fund's profits. This is fundamentally different from PE, where returns are more normally distributed. A single company like an early investment in a breakout winner can return an entire fund multiple times over.

Startup Valuation Methods

Mark Complete

Valuing startups is fundamentally different from valuing mature companies. Traditional DCF analysis breaks down when a company has no cash flows, no history, and uncertain survival. Instead, VCs use a combination of methods that balance quantitative analysis with qualitative judgment.

Pre-Money and Post-Money Valuation

These are the most fundamental concepts in startup financing. Every conversation about fundraising begins here:

Pre-Money / Post-Money Relationship
Post-Money Valuation = Pre-Money Valuation + Investment Amount
Investor Ownership % = Investment Amount / Post-Money Valuation
Founder Ownership % = Pre-Money Valuation / Post-Money Valuation
Price Per Share = Pre-Money Valuation / Pre-Money Shares Outstanding
New Shares Issued = Investment Amount / Price Per Share

Worked Example: Series A Pricing

A startup has 10M shares outstanding and raises a $5M Series A at a $15M pre-money valuation.

  • Post-Money = $15M + $5M = $20M
  • Investor Ownership = $5M / $20M = 25%
  • Founder/Existing Ownership = $15M / $20M = 75%
  • Price Per Share = $15M / 10M shares = $1.50/share
  • New Shares Issued to Investor = $5M / $1.50 = 3,333,333 shares
  • Total Post-Money Shares = 10M + 3.33M = 13,333,333 shares

Sanity check: 3.33M / 13.33M = 25%. The math is internally consistent.

Comparable Transactions Method

The most common method for companies with some traction. Look at recent fundraising rounds by similar companies at similar stages:

  • Identify 5-10 comparable companies (same sector, stage, geography, business model)
  • Gather their round sizes, valuations, and key metrics at time of raise
  • Calculate implied multiples (e.g., EV/ARR, EV/GMV, price per user)
  • Adjust for growth rate differentials, market size, team quality, and competitive position

Comp-Based Valuation Example

Five comparable B2B SaaS companies recently raised Series A rounds at $2M ARR. Their pre-money valuations ranged from $30M to $50M, implying 15x-25x ARR multiples. The median was 20x ARR.

Your target company has $2.5M ARR growing 3x YoY with 130% NRR (above-average retention). Applying a premium to the median: 25x ARR x $2.5M = $62.5M pre-money valuation.

The Berkus Method (Pre-Revenue)

Developed by angel investor Dave Berkus for valuing pre-revenue companies where traditional metrics do not yet exist:

Risk FactorMax ValueWhat It Measures
Sound Idea (Basic Value)$500KAttractiveness of the concept and intellectual property
Prototype / Technology$500KReduction of technology risk -- does the product work?
Quality Management Team$500KReduction of execution risk -- can this team deliver?
Strategic Relationships$500KReduction of market risk -- partnerships, early customers, advisors
Product Rollout / Sales$500KReduction of production/financial risk -- evidence of demand

Maximum pre-money valuation under this method: $2.5M. Best suited for pre-seed and very early seed-stage companies where there is no revenue to anchor a valuation.

Revenue Multiple Method

For companies with revenue, VCs most commonly apply a multiple to ARR (Annual Recurring Revenue). The appropriate multiple depends heavily on growth rate:

Revenue-Based Valuation
Pre-Money Valuation = ARR x Revenue Multiple
Revenue Multiple is driven by: growth rate, net retention, market size, gross margins, competitive position
Growth Rate (YoY)Typical ARR MultipleCommon Stage
3x+ (tripling annually)30-80x ARRSeries A / B
2-3x (doubling to tripling)15-40x ARRSeries B / C
50-100% annual growth10-20x ARRSeries C / D
<50% annual growth5-15x ARRLate-stage / pre-IPO

VC Method (Target Return-Based)

Some VCs back into a valuation by starting with their required return and working backward from an expected exit value:

VC Method Valuation
Required Ownership = Investment / (Expected Exit Value / Target Return Multiple)
Post-Money Today = Investment / Required Ownership
Pre-Money Today = Post-Money - Investment

VC Method Worked Example

A VC wants to invest $5M and needs a 10x return. They believe the company could exit at $300M in 5-7 years.

  • Required proceeds at exit = $5M x 10 = $50M
  • Required ownership at exit = $50M / $300M = 16.7%
  • Accounting for ~50% dilution from future rounds: need 16.7% / 0.5 = 33.3% today
  • Post-money today = $5M / 33.3% = $15M
  • Pre-money = $15M - $5M = $10M

The VC method is useful as a sanity check: if the implied pre-money is well below what the founder is asking, the deal may not work for the VC's return requirements.

Interview Tip

When asked to value a startup, always state your assumptions explicitly. There is no single "right" answer -- interviewers want to see your framework, the method you chose and why, and how you adjust for company-specific factors. Start with the method most appropriate for the company's stage and support it with comparable data points.

A startup raises $8M at a $32M pre-money valuation. What percentage of the company do the new investors own?

25% -- because $8M is 25% of $32M
20% -- because post-money is $40M and $8M / $40M = 20%
32% -- based on a dilution adjustment factor
8% -- because the investment is $8M out of total capital raised
Post-Money = Pre-Money + Investment = $32M + $8M = $40M. Investor Ownership = Investment / Post-Money = $8M / $40M = 20%. A very common mistake is dividing by the pre-money valuation instead of the post-money, which would incorrectly give 25%. Always remember: ownership is calculated against the post-money valuation.

Term Sheets & Key Provisions

Mark Complete

A term sheet is a non-binding document outlining the key economic and governance terms of a proposed investment. While it is not a final legal contract, it sets the framework for definitive agreements and is extremely difficult to renegotiate once signed. Understanding term sheet provisions is critical because they determine how value is distributed at every future event -- fundraising, exits, and liquidation.

Liquidation Preferences

The single most important economic term in any venture deal. Liquidation preferences determine who gets paid first and how much when the company is sold, merged, or liquidated (collectively called a "liquidity event").

Three Types of Liquidation Preference

  • 1x Non-Participating Preferred (Standard): Investor gets back 1x their investment OR converts to common stock and shares pro-rata -- whichever yields more. This is the most founder-friendly and most common structure in Silicon Valley.
  • 1x Participating Preferred ("Double Dip"): Investor gets back 1x their investment AND then participates in remaining proceeds pro-rata alongside common stockholders. This is investor-favorable and significantly reduces founder proceeds in moderate exits.
  • 1x Participating with Cap (e.g., 3x cap): Same as participating, but the investor's total payout is capped at a specified multiple (e.g., 3x their investment). A compromise between the two extremes.

Liquidation Preference Comparison at Different Exit Values

Setup: Investor invests $10M for 25% of the company.

At a $60M exit:

  • Non-Participating: Max($10M preference, 25% x $60M = $15M) = $15M (investor converts)
  • Participating: $10M + 25% x ($60M - $10M) = $10M + $12.5M = $22.5M
  • Participating w/ 3x Cap: Min($22.5M, 3 x $10M = $30M) = $22.5M (below cap)

At a $200M exit:

  • Non-Participating: Max($10M, 25% x $200M = $50M) = $50M
  • Participating: $10M + 25% x $190M = $10M + $47.5M = $57.5M
  • Participating w/ 3x Cap: Min($57.5M, $30M) = $30M (cap binds -- investor converts to common for $50M instead)

At a $20M exit (bad outcome):

  • Non-Participating: Max($10M, 25% x $20M = $5M) = $10M (takes preference)
  • Participating: $10M + 25% x $10M = $12.5M
  • Founders receive only $7.5-$10M of a $20M exit -- this is why liq prefs matter at moderate exits

Negotiation Dynamics

Term sheet negotiation is a function of leverage. Leverage depends on: how many term sheets the founder has (competitive dynamics), how "hot" the company is perceived, the founder's track record, and current market conditions. In a competitive fundraise, founders can push for non-participating preferred, minimal protective provisions, and founder-friendly board structures. In a difficult market, investors have more leverage to include participation, stronger anti-dilution, and additional governance rights. Always understand the market context when evaluating a term sheet.

Anti-Dilution Protection

Protects investors if the company raises a future round at a lower valuation (a "down round"). Anti-dilution adjusts the conversion price of preferred stock, effectively giving the investor more shares:

TypeHow It WorksImpact on Founders
Full RatchetConversion price resets entirely to the new lower price, as if the investor originally paid the lower priceExtremely dilutive to founders; rare in modern deals
Broad-Based Weighted AverageConversion price adjusted using a formula that weights the size of the down round relative to total sharesMore moderate dilution; the industry standard
Broad-Based Weighted Average Anti-Dilution Formula
New Conversion Price = Old Price x [(A + B) / (A + C)]

Where:
A = Shares outstanding pre-new round (fully diluted)
B = Shares the new round's money would buy at the OLD price
C = Shares actually issued in the new (down) round at the LOWER price

Since C > B (more shares issued at lower price), the ratio is < 1, reducing the conversion price.

Anti-Dilution Example

Series A investor bought at $2.00/share. Company does a down round at $1.00/share. 10M shares outstanding, new round issues 5M shares.

  • A = 10M shares
  • B = ($1.00 x 5M) / $2.00 = 2.5M shares (what new money would buy at old price)
  • C = 5M shares (actually issued)
  • Full Ratchet: New price = $1.00 (reset entirely)
  • Weighted Average: New price = $2.00 x (10M + 2.5M) / (10M + 5M) = $2.00 x 0.833 = $1.67

The weighted average approach is much less punitive to founders than full ratchet.

Other Critical Term Sheet Provisions

Board Composition & Governance ▶

Board composition determines who controls the company's strategic direction. Typical structures evolve over time:

  • Seed: 3 seats -- 2 founders, 1 investor (or 2 founders + 1 independent)
  • Series A: 5 seats -- 2 founders, 2 investors, 1 independent
  • Series B+: May expand to 7 seats with additional investor and independent seats

Board control matters enormously for decisions like hiring/firing the CEO, approving an acquisition, raising new rounds, and pivoting the business.

Protective Provisions (Investor Veto Rights) ▶

Protective provisions give preferred stockholders veto power over specific company actions, regardless of board composition. Standard protective provisions require investor approval to:

  • Issue new equity or create senior/pari-passu preferred stock
  • Sell the company or substantially all assets
  • Take on debt above a threshold
  • Change the certificate of incorporation
  • Pay dividends or repurchase shares
  • Increase or decrease board size

Even a minority investor with protective provisions can effectively block a sale if they believe the price is too low.

Pro-Rata Rights & Founder Vesting ▶

Pro-Rata Rights: The contractual right to invest in future funding rounds to maintain your ownership percentage. Critical for early investors who want to double down on winners. If a seed investor owns 10% and the company raises a Series A, pro-rata rights allow them to invest enough to maintain their 10% stake.

Founder Vesting: The industry standard is 4-year vesting with a 1-year cliff:

  • 1-Year Cliff: No shares vest until the first anniversary. If a founder leaves before the cliff, they forfeit all unvested shares.
  • Monthly Vesting: After the cliff, shares vest in equal monthly installments over the remaining 36 months.
  • Acceleration: Some founders negotiate single-trigger (vesting accelerates on acquisition) or double-trigger (accelerates on acquisition + termination) acceleration clauses.

An investor puts in $10M for 20% of a startup with 1x participating preferred. The company sells for $40M. How much does the investor receive?

$8M -- simply 20% of $40M
$16M -- $10M preference plus 20% of the remaining $30M ($6M)
$10M -- just the liquidation preference since it exceeds the pro-rata share
$20M -- 2x the liquidation preference as a guaranteed minimum
With 1x participating preferred, the investor first receives their $10M back (the liquidation preference), then participates in the remaining proceeds pro-rata. Remaining = $40M - $10M = $30M. Investor's pro-rata share = 20% x $30M = $6M. Total = $10M + $6M = $16M. This is substantially more than the $8M they would get by simply converting to common stock (20% x $40M). This "double dip" is why participating preferred is investor-favorable.

Cap Table Modeling

Mark Complete

A capitalization table (cap table) is the master record of who owns what in a company. It tracks every share, option grant, warrant, and convertible instrument, showing how ownership evolves across funding rounds. Building and analyzing cap tables accurately is one of the most testable and practical skills in VC interviews.

Building a Cap Table Through Multiple Rounds

Multi-Round Cap Table: Founding Through Series A

Step 1 -- Founding: Two co-founders split 10M shares 50/50. They also create a 15% employee option pool (1.76M shares), bringing total shares to 11.76M.

Step 2 -- Seed Round: Raise $2M at $8M pre-money valuation.

  • Post-money = $8M + $2M = $10M
  • Investor ownership = $2M / $10M = 20%
  • New shares = 11.76M x (20% / 80%) = 2,940,000
  • Total shares post-seed = 14,700,000

Step 3 -- Series A: Raise $8M at $32M pre-money valuation.

  • Post-money = $32M + $8M = $40M
  • Investor ownership = $8M / $40M = 20%
  • New shares = 14.7M x (20% / 80%) = 3,675,000
  • Total shares post-Series A = 18,375,000

Founder B5,000,00042.5%5,000,00034.0%5,000,00027.2%

Key Cap Table Concepts

  • Dilution: Each new round dilutes all existing shareholders proportionally. Founder A went from 42.5% to 34.0% to 27.2% across two rounds.
  • Option Pool Shuffle: VCs often require expanding the option pool in the pre-money valuation, meaning founders (not the new investors) bear the dilution from the pool expansion.
  • Fully Diluted Shares: Includes all outstanding shares plus all options, warrants, convertible notes, and SAFEs as if they were converted/exercised. This is the denominator VCs use for ownership calculations.

Waterfall Analysis at Exit

A waterfall analysis shows how exit proceeds flow through the capital structure to each class of shareholder, respecting liquidation preferences and conversion rights.

Exit Waterfall: $100M Acquisition

Using the cap table above. Assume Series A has 1x non-participating preferred ($8M preference) and Seed has 1x non-participating preferred ($2M preference).

Step 1: Each preferred class compares its liquidation preference to its as-converted pro-rata share:

  • Series A: Preference = $8M vs. Convert = 20.0% x $100M = $20M. Converts to common (converting is better).
  • Seed: Preference = $2M vs. Convert = 16.0% x $100M = $16M. Converts to common (converting is better).

Step 2: Everyone converts to common. Distribute $100M pro-rata by share count:

  • Founder A: 27.2% x $100M = $27.2M
  • Founder B: 27.2% x $100M = $27.2M
  • Seed Investors: 16.0% x $100M = $16.0M (8.0x return on $2M invested)
  • Series A Investors: 20.0% x $100M = $20.0M (2.5x return on $8M invested)

Exit Waterfall: $15M Acquisition (Bad Outcome)

Same cap table and preferences. At a low exit value, preferences dominate:

Step 1: Compare preference vs. conversion:

  • Series A: Preference = $8M vs. Convert = 20.0% x $15M = $3M. Takes preference.
  • Seed: Preference = $2M vs. Convert = 16.0% x $15M = $2.4M. Takes preference (though close).

Step 2: Distribute remaining proceeds to common shareholders:

  • Remaining after preferences = $15M - $8M - $2M = $5M
  • Common shareholders (founders + option pool) hold 64% of total shares = 100% of remaining common
  • Founder A: ~42.5% of common = $2.1M
  • Founder B: ~42.5% of common = $2.1M
  • Option Pool: ~15% of common = $0.8M

At a $15M exit, founders who built the company for years receive only ~$2M each. This illustrates why liquidation preferences matter enormously at moderate-to-low exit values.

When Preferences Matter

In the $100M exit above, everyone converts and preferences are irrelevant. But at $15M, preferences consume $10M of a $15M exit, leaving very little for common shareholders. The "breakpoint" where all investors convert to common depends on the total preference stack relative to the exit value. As a rule of thumb: if exit value > 3-4x the total preference stack, all investors will convert.

A founder owns 40% of a company pre-Series A. The Series A raises $10M at a $30M pre-money valuation. What is the founder's ownership after the round?

40% -- ownership does not change when new money comes in
25% -- $10M / $40M = 25% dilution applied directly to the founder's stake
30% -- the founder's 40% is diluted proportionally: 40% x ($30M / $40M) = 30%
35% -- 40% minus half of the new investor's 25% stake
Post-money = $30M + $10M = $40M. New investor gets $10M / $40M = 25%. All existing shareholders are diluted proportionally by the same factor: (1 - 25%) = 75%. The founder's new ownership = 40% x 75% = 30%. Equivalently, the founder's number of shares has not changed, but the total share count increased, reducing their percentage from 40% to 30%.

VC Fund Economics

Mark Complete

Understanding how a VC fund makes money -- and how much actually gets invested -- is essential for grasping why VCs behave the way they do. The economics of the fund dictate investment strategy, portfolio construction, and the relentless pursuit of outlier returns.

Management Fees & Carried Interest

GP Compensation Structure (Standard "2 and 20")
Management Fee = ~2% of committed capital per year (investment period), stepping down to ~1.5-2% of invested capital (harvest period)
Carried Interest ("Carry") = 20% of profits above the hurdle rate
Hurdle Rate = Typically 8% preferred return to LPs before carry kicks in
GP Commit = 1-5% of fund size (GP's own capital, invested alongside LPs)

Fund Economics Worked Example

A $200M fund with a 10-year life, 2% management fee, 20% carry, 8% hurdle rate.

  • Total Management Fees: ~$200M x 2% x 5 years (investment period) + $200M x 1.5% x 5 years (harvest) = $20M + $15M = $35M
  • Capital Available for Investment: $200M - $35M = ~$165M (roughly 82% of the fund)
  • If fund returns 3x gross ($600M total value):
  • LPs get their $200M capital back first
  • LPs receive 8% preferred return (hurdle): ~$16M/year x 5 avg years = ~$80M
  • Remaining profit = $600M - $200M - $80M = $320M
  • GP Carry: 20% x $320M = $64M
  • LP Share of Remaining: 80% x $320M = $256M
  • Total GP Compensation: $35M fees + $64M carry = ~$99M

Why VCs Need 3x+ Fund Returns

The Fund Return Math

After management fees consume ~18-20% of committed capital, the fund needs to generate 3x+ gross returns on invested capital just to deliver an attractive ~2.5x net return to LPs. This is the minimum for a "good" fund. Consider the math:

  • $200M fund invests ~$165M after fees
  • To return 3x net to LPs ($600M), need $600M in gross proceeds
  • $600M / $165M invested = 3.6x gross MOIC needed
  • Top-quartile VC funds return 3-5x net MOIC to LPs
  • This is why VCs only pursue investments with 10x+ return potential -- you need massive winners to compensate for all the zeros

Portfolio Construction

How a VC fund allocates capital is directly driven by the return math and the power law:

Strategy ElementTypical RangeWhy It Matters
Number of Investments20-35 companiesEnough diversification to maximize probability of hitting at least 1-2 breakout winners
Initial Check Size1-3% of fund per dealSmall enough to leave room for follow-on investment in winners
Reserve Ratio40-60% of investable capitalCapital reserved for follow-on rounds in top-performing companies
Follow-on Strategy2-3x initial checkDouble and triple down on winners; let losers go without additional capital
Ownership Target10-20% at entryNeed meaningful ownership to generate fund-level returns from winners

Common Misconception

Many people think VCs invest the entire fund amount. In reality, 15-25% is consumed by management fees over the fund's life. A $100M fund may only invest $75-85M in actual startups. This fee drag significantly impacts the return math and is a frequent interview topic. Additionally, reserves for follow-on investments mean that only 40-60% of the investable capital goes into initial checks.

Key Return Metrics for VC Funds

MetricDefinitionWhat "Good" Looks Like
MOIC (Multiple on Invested Capital)Total Value / Total Invested Capital3x+ net MOIC for top-quartile funds
IRR (Internal Rate of Return)Annualized return accounting for timing of cash flows>20% net IRR for top-quartile
DPI (Distributions to Paid-In)Cash actually returned to LPs / Capital called>1.0x means LPs have received their money back
TVPI (Total Value to Paid-In)(Distributions + Remaining Value) / Capital called>2.0x for a good fund; includes unrealized gains
RVPI (Residual Value to Paid-In)Remaining unrealized portfolio value / Capital calledHigh early in fund life, should decrease as exits occur

MOIC vs. IRR

MOIC and IRR can tell different stories. A fund that returns 3x over 5 years has a much higher IRR than one that returns 3x over 10 years, even though the MOIC is identical. VCs often prefer MOIC because it is simpler and less sensitive to timing, but LPs care deeply about IRR because it reflects the time value of money. In interviews, demonstrate understanding of both metrics and when each is most informative.

A $300M VC fund charges 2% management fees over 10 years. Approximately how much capital is actually available for investments?

$300M -- management fees are paid from investment returns, not the fund itself
$294M -- only the first year's 2% fee ($6M) is deducted
$270M -- 10% is reserved for total fees
$240M -- approximately $60M goes to management fees (~2% x $300M x 10 years), leaving ~$240M for investments
Management fees are charged on committed capital annually throughout the fund's life. Over 10 years, total fees approximate 2% x $300M x 10 = $60M (though in practice fees may step down after the investment period). This leaves roughly $240M (80%) for actual investments. The key insight: a meaningful portion of every fund never gets deployed into startups.

Startup Metrics & KPIs

Mark Complete

VCs evaluate startups through specific metrics that vary by business model. Knowing which metrics matter for each type of business -- and what "good" looks like at each stage -- is essential for both investing and interviewing.

SaaS Metrics (Software-as-a-Service)

Core SaaS Formulas
ARR (Annual Recurring Revenue) = MRR x 12
MRR (Monthly Recurring Revenue) = Sum of all active monthly subscriptions
Net Revenue Retention (NRR) = (Beginning MRR + Expansion - Contraction - Churn) / Beginning MRR
CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost) = Total Sales & Marketing Spend / New Customers Acquired
LTV (Lifetime Value) = ARPU x Gross Margin / Monthly Churn Rate
LTV:CAC Ratio = LTV / CAC (target: >3x for healthy business)
CAC Payback = CAC / (Monthly ARPU x Gross Margin) in months
Burn Multiple = Net Cash Burn / Net New ARR (lower is better)

What "Good" Looks Like in SaaS

  • NRR > 120%: Existing customers expand faster than they churn -- the single strongest indicator of product-market fit
  • LTV:CAC > 3x: Each customer generates 3x+ more value than the cost to acquire them
  • CAC Payback < 18 months: Acquisition costs are recovered quickly, enabling faster reinvestment
  • Gross Margins > 70%: The high margins characteristic of software businesses (vs. 30-50% for services)
  • Burn Multiple < 2x: Efficient growth -- spending less than $2 in burn to generate $1 of new ARR. Below 1x is exceptional.
  • Rule of 40: Revenue growth rate (%) + profit margin (%) should exceed 40 for a healthy SaaS business at scale

SaaS Metrics Worked Example

A B2B SaaS company has the following profile:

  • MRR: $500K (ARR = $6M)
  • Monthly gross churn: 1.5% of MRR
  • Monthly expansion: 3% of MRR
  • CAC: $15,000 per customer
  • Average contract value (ACV): $24,000/year ($2,000/month)
  • Gross margin: 80%

NRR = (100% - 1.5% + 3%)^12 = (101.5%)^12 = ~120% annually

LTV = ($2,000 x 80%) / 1.5% = $1,600 / 0.015 = $106,667

LTV:CAC = $106,667 / $15,000 = 7.1x (excellent)

CAC Payback = $15,000 / ($2,000 x 80%) = $15,000 / $1,600 = 9.4 months (strong)

Vanity Metrics to Avoid

Some metrics look impressive but lack substance. VCs are trained to see through these:

  • Total registered users (instead of active users) -- most may be inactive
  • Gross revenue (instead of net revenue) -- hides refunds, discounts, and chargebacks
  • Downloads (instead of retention) -- easy to acquire installs, hard to retain users
  • GMV (instead of take-rate revenue) -- the platform may keep only 5% of the headline number
  • Total addressable market without a credible path to capture -- big TAM means nothing without a realistic go-to-market

A SaaS company started the year with $5M MRR. During the year it added $2M in expansion revenue from existing customers, lost $500K to contraction, and $300K to churn. What is the Net Revenue Retention (NRR)?

124% -- ($5M + $2M - $0.5M - $0.3M) / $5M = $6.2M / $5M
140% -- ($5M + $2M) / $5M, only counting expansion without subtracting losses
84% -- ($5M - $0.5M - $0.3M) / $5M, treating expansion as new (not existing) revenue
110% -- using ending MRR as the denominator instead of beginning MRR
NRR = (Beginning MRR + Expansion - Contraction - Churn) / Beginning MRR = ($5M + $2M - $0.5M - $0.3M) / $5M = $6.2M / $5M = 124%. NRR above 120% is considered excellent and signals that existing customers are spending more over time -- even without acquiring any new customers, the company's revenue would grow 24% annually. This is the strongest indicator of product-market fit in SaaS.

Due Diligence

Mark Complete

VC due diligence is fundamentally different from PE due diligence. While PE firms audit detailed financial statements, VC diligence focuses on team, market, and product because early-stage companies have limited financial history. The goal is not to verify numbers but to assess the probability that this team can build a massive business in this market.

Team Assessment

At pre-seed and seed stage, the team is the single most important factor. Many VCs say they invest primarily in people. Key evaluation criteria:

  • Founder-Market Fit: Does this team have unique insight, domain expertise, or an unfair advantage in this specific market?
  • Technical Capability: Can they build the core product without outsourcing critical technology?
  • Track Record: Have they built companies before? Prior exits, even small ones, signal execution ability.
  • Recruiting Ability: Can they attract top-tier talent? The first 10 hires define the company.
  • Resilience & Grit: Startups face constant setbacks. Can this team persevere through adversity?
  • Coachability: Do they incorporate feedback and iterate, or are they rigid and defensive?
  • Storytelling: Can they articulate a compelling vision that inspires employees, customers, and investors?

Market Sizing: TAM / SAM / SOM

Market Sizing Framework
TAM (Total Addressable Market) = Total revenue opportunity if you captured 100% market share globally
SAM (Serviceable Addressable Market) = The segment of TAM your current product and go-to-market can realistically reach
SOM (Serviceable Obtainable Market) = The share of SAM you can realistically capture in 3-5 years given competition

Market Sizing Example: B2B Compliance Software

Top-Down Approach:

  • TAM: $50B -- total global compliance software market (all industries, geographies, segments)
  • SAM: $8B -- U.S. mid-market financial services compliance (your initial product focus)
  • SOM: $400M -- 5% of SAM, achievable in 5 years given competitive dynamics and sales capacity

Bottom-Up Approach (preferred by sophisticated VCs):

  • 15,000 mid-market financial services firms in the U.S.
  • Average compliance software budget: $100K-$500K/year
  • Target the 5,000 firms spending $200K+ = $1B obtainable market
  • Capture 10% in 5 years = $100M ARR opportunity

VCs want TAM large enough ($1B+) that even a modest market share generates a venture-scale outcome. But credibility matters more than big numbers -- overstating the market is a red flag.

Product-Market Fit Signals

How VCs Assess Product-Market Fit

  • Organic Growth: Users arriving without paid marketing; strong word-of-mouth referrals
  • High Retention: Users stick around -- D30 > 20% for consumer, NRR > 100% for SaaS
  • Short Sales Cycles: Enterprise customers buy within weeks, not months of deliberation
  • Inbound Demand: More demand than the team can handle -- a "pull" market rather than a "push" market
  • Sean Ellis Test: Survey users: "How would you feel if you could no longer use this product?" If >40% say "very disappointed," you likely have PMF
  • Negative Churn: Revenue from existing customers grows faster than revenue lost to churn (NRR > 100%)

Competitive Analysis Framework

VCs want to understand the competitive landscape in detail. A strong competitive analysis addresses:

How to Assess Competitive Moats ▶
Moat TypeDescriptionExamples
Network EffectsProduct becomes more valuable as more people use itSocial networks, marketplaces, payment platforms
Switching CostsIt is costly or painful for customers to move to a competitorEnterprise SaaS with deep integrations, ERP systems
Proprietary DataUnique data assets that improve the product and are hard to replicateML-driven products, data aggregators
Economies of ScaleCost advantages that increase with sizeCloud infrastructure, logistics platforms
Brand / TrustStrong brand creates preference and reduces customer acquisition costsConsumer fintech, healthcare

The strongest businesses have multiple reinforcing moats. A marketplace with network effects AND proprietary data AND high switching costs is far more defensible than one with just a single moat.

Red Flags in Due Diligence

Common Deal-Breakers

  • Founders who cannot clearly articulate their unit economics or key metrics
  • Revenue concentrated in 1-2 customers (customer concentration risk)
  • Burn rate accelerating significantly faster than revenue growth
  • High churn disguised by aggressive new customer acquisition ("leaky bucket")
  • Co-founder conflicts, unclear roles, or misaligned equity splits
  • TAM analysis that conflates adjacent or unrelated markets to inflate the number
  • Inability to name specific competitors or dismissing all competition as irrelevant
  • Vague or evasive answers about why previous investors passed
  • Technology built entirely by contractors with no in-house engineering talent

A startup claims a $30B TAM based on the entire global healthcare market. Their product is a telemedicine app specifically for dermatology in the United States. What is the most likely issue?

$30B is too small -- the global healthcare market is actually much larger
The TAM is correct because telemedicine could eventually expand to serve all of healthcare
The SAM should equal the TAM since software can scale globally at zero marginal cost
The TAM conflates the entire healthcare market with the much smaller addressable market for U.S. dermatology telemedicine, destroying credibility
This is a classic and common market sizing error. The startup's actual TAM is the U.S. dermatology telemedicine market, which might be $1-3B rather than $30B. Conflating a broad industry category with the specific addressable segment undermines the founder's credibility with experienced VCs. The best founders size their market bottom-up from real customer data and are honest about the actual addressable opportunity.

Understanding what is expected at each stage -- and how the investment thesis changes -- is critical for both investors and founders.

What Changes Across Stages

Early Stage (Pre-Seed / Seed): Betting on People & Vision ▶

At the earliest stages, there is little or no quantitative data to evaluate. VCs are primarily assessing the founding team and market opportunity. Key questions include: Is this a world-class team with relevant domain expertise? Is the market large enough and growing? Is the timing right -- why now?

Investments are smaller ($250K-$5M), ownership targets are higher (10-20%), and the risk of total loss is the greatest. Due diligence is primarily qualitative: reference calls on the founders, assessment of the competitive landscape, and conviction about market timing.

Typical investors: Angel investors, pre-seed and seed-focused funds, accelerators (YC, Techstars), micro VCs.

Venture Stage (Series A / B): Betting on Traction & Scalability ▶

By Series A, the company must demonstrate product-market fit and a repeatable go-to-market motion. VCs shift from evaluating pure potential to evaluating actual performance: revenue growth rate, retention metrics, and unit economics.

Series B is about proving the business can scale efficiently. Can you add sales reps and maintain productivity? Can you expand to new customer segments? Do unit economics hold or improve at scale?

Round sizes increase ($5M-$60M), valuations are anchored to revenue multiples, and the investor base shifts to multi-stage VCs with deeper pockets for follow-on investment.

Typical investors: Sequoia, Andreessen Horowitz, Bessemer, Accel, Lightspeed, sector-focused funds.

Growth Stage (Series C+ / Growth Equity): Betting on Market Leadership ▶

Late-stage venture investing resembles private equity more than traditional VC. Companies at this stage have significant revenue ($20M-$100M+ ARR), established management teams, and clearer paths to profitability or an IPO.

Investors focus on competitive moats, market leadership position, path to public markets, and margin expansion. Due diligence becomes more financial and operational -- similar to PE-style analysis. Round sizes are large ($50M-$500M+) and ownership stakes are typically smaller (5-15%).

Typical investors: General Atlantic, Tiger Global, Coatue, T. Rowe Price, Fidelity, sovereign wealth funds, and late-stage specialists.

Convertible Instruments at Early Stages

At pre-seed and seed, many deals use convertible instruments instead of priced equity rounds:

InstrumentHow It WorksKey Terms

SAFE Conversion Example

An investor puts $500K into a SAFE with a $5M valuation cap and 20% discount. The company later raises a Series Seed at $10M pre-money valuation ($1.00/share).

  • Cap-based conversion: $500K / ($5M cap / total shares) = conversion at $0.50/share = 1,000,000 shares
  • Discount-based conversion: $500K / ($1.00 x 80%) = $500K / $0.80 = 625,000 shares
  • Investor takes the better deal: 1,000,000 shares (cap-based, since it yields more shares)

The valuation cap protects early investors from excessive dilution if the company's valuation increases significantly before the priced round.

Interview Tip

When asked about different stages in an interview, demonstrate awareness that each stage requires fundamentally different skill sets from investors: early-stage investing is about pattern recognition, founder assessment, and conviction under uncertainty; growth-stage investing is about financial modeling, market analysis, and competitive positioning. The best VCs specialize in a stage because the skills do not fully transfer.

A B2B SaaS company has $2M ARR, 130% NRR, and is growing 3x year-over-year. Which funding stage are they most likely raising?

Pre-Seed -- they are still very early with minimal traction
Series A -- $1-3M ARR with strong growth and retention indicates proven PMF, ready to scale
Series C -- 3x growth rate means they are already at significant scale
Growth Equity -- 130% NRR means they should be targeting an IPO
$2M ARR with 3x year-over-year growth and 130% NRR is a textbook Series A company. They have demonstrated product-market fit (evidenced by high NRR and rapid growth) and have enough revenue to show a repeatable pattern, but need capital to scale the go-to-market engine. Pre-seed/seed companies typically have less than $1M ARR, while Series C+ companies usually have $20M+ ARR.

Mark Complete

How VCs find, evaluate, and decide on investments is the operational engine of the business. The best VCs build repeatable systems for discovering exceptional companies before they become obvious to the market, and they develop disciplined frameworks for making high-stakes decisions under extreme uncertainty.

The Investment Process

A typical VC deal takes 2-8 weeks from first meeting to term sheet, though some firms move in days for competitive situations. The process includes:

  1. Sourcing & Screening (Ongoing): A fund may review 1,000-3,000+ opportunities per year. Most are screened out within minutes based on stage, sector, geography, or a quick assessment of the pitch materials.
  2. First Partner Meeting (30-60 min): An initial meeting between the founder(s) and one or two partners. The focus is on understanding the team, product, market opportunity, and basic traction. The partner forms an initial view on whether to continue.
  3. Deep Dive (1-3 weeks): Follow-up meetings, product demonstrations, customer reference calls, market analysis, competitive mapping, and preliminary financial review. The deal lead builds conviction.
  4. Partnership Meeting: The sponsoring partner presents the opportunity to the full partnership. Some firms require unanimous consent, others allow a single partner's strong conviction to carry the vote.
  5. Term Sheet: If approved, the firm issues a term sheet outlining valuation, investment amount, governance terms, and key provisions.
  6. Confirmatory Due Diligence & Close (2-4 weeks): Legal review, background checks, cap table verification, and finalization of definitive legal agreements. Funds are wired at close.
VC Decision-Making Cultures: Consensus vs. Conviction ▶

How VC firms make investment decisions varies significantly and shapes the types of deals they do:

  • Consensus Model: All or most partners must agree to invest. Reduces risk of bad deals but can lead to "consensus mediocrity" -- passing on polarizing companies that turn out to be the biggest winners. Firms like Benchmark are known for this approach.
  • Champion Model: A single partner with strong conviction can push a deal through, sometimes even against skepticism from colleagues. This model captures more outlier investments but can lead to more losses. Many firms at a16z and Founders Fund operate closer to this model.
  • Hybrid: Most firms land somewhere in between -- a sponsoring partner drives the deal, but needs support from at least one or two other partners to proceed.

Neither model is strictly better. The consensus model tends to produce more consistent but lower-ceiling returns, while the champion model has higher variance but may capture more power-law outcomes.

What Makes VCs Say Yes

The Investment Decision Framework

Despite the complexity of each deal, most VC investment decisions ultimately come down to conviction across three dimensions:

  • Team: Are these the right people to build this company? Do they have founder-market fit, the grit to persevere through inevitable setbacks, and the charisma to recruit an exceptional team?
  • Market: Is this a large ($1B+), growing market with structural tailwinds? Can this company become the category leader?
  • Product / Business: Does the product solve a real, painful problem in a way that is 10x better than existing alternatives? Are unit economics attractive or plausibly achievable?

The best investments are strong on all three. Most experienced VCs will invest when two of three are exceptional and the third is at least acceptable. The weight shifts by stage: at seed, team dominates; by Series B, metrics and market position carry more weight.

Why VCs Pass on Deals

Common Reasons for Passing

  • Market too small: Even if the company succeeds completely, the potential exit does not generate a meaningful return for the fund
  • Team concerns: Lack of relevant domain expertise, co-founder tension, weak ability to recruit top talent
  • No defensible moat: No network effects, switching costs, proprietary data, or other barriers to competition
  • Poor or unclear unit economics: CAC too high, margins too thin, or no credible path to profitability
  • Timing mismatch: Too early (market not ready) or too late (incumbents already dominant with entrenched advantages)
  • Valuation: Price too high relative to the current traction and remaining risk
  • Portfolio conflict: Already invested in a competing company in the same space

The VC Funnel: Typical Annual Pipeline

A mid-stage venture fund might see the following pipeline in a typical year:

  • 2,000+ inbound pitches, referrals, and sourced opportunities reviewed
  • 500 receive an initial screening call or first meeting with a partner
  • 100 advance to a deep-dive second meeting with additional team members
  • 30 are formally presented at partnership meetings for discussion
  • 8-12 receive term sheets from the fund
  • 6-10 investments are ultimately completed (some term sheets are declined)

This implies a roughly 0.3-0.5% conversion rate from initial contact to funded investment. Selectivity is extreme -- which is why VCs say "no" to 99%+ of the opportunities they see.

Building a VC Career

How to Break into Venture Capital ▶

VC recruiting is highly unstructured compared to banking or consulting. Common paths include:

  • Post-MBA Associate: 2-3 years of banking, consulting, or operating experience + top MBA. The most structured path, but still highly competitive.
  • Pre-MBA Analyst: 2-year programs at firms like a16z, Bessemer, or Insight. Usually recruited from banking, consulting, or top startups.
  • Operator Track: Former startup founders or executives who join VC based on operating experience and industry knowledge.
  • Scout / Venture Fellow: Part-time roles where you source deals for a fund. Good for building a track record.

Key skills to develop: Market mapping, financial modeling (cap tables, waterfalls, fund models), clear written communication (investment memos), and building a network of founders and operators.

A VC firm sees 2,000 deals per year and invests in 10. A partner advocates strongly for a company with an exceptional founding team and enormous TAM, but the company has very limited current revenue metrics. The most likely outcome at the partnership meeting is:

Automatic approval -- exceptional founding teams always receive funding
Automatic rejection -- limited metrics indicate the company is not investment-ready
Intensive debate where the partnership weighs team and market strength against metric weakness, with the conviction level of the sponsoring partner playing a critical role
The deal is unconditionally tabled until metrics improve, with no further action
VC partnership meetings involve rigorous debate, especially for early-stage deals where metrics are inherently limited. A strong sponsoring partner with high personal conviction can push a deal through even with imperfect data, particularly at the seed or early Series A stage where the investment thesis is primarily about team and market, not metrics. The outcome depends heavily on the firm's decision-making culture: some are consensus-driven (requiring broad agreement), while others empower individual partners to make conviction-based bets.
