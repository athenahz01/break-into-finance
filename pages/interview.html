Interview Prep

The complete guide to landing offers in IB, PE, VC, and Consulting. Every question. Every framework. Every template you need.

1

Your Story — "Tell Me About Yourself"

Mark Complete

This is the most important question in any finance interview. You'll be asked it first, and it sets the tone for everything that follows. A great story makes interviewers want to hire you. A mediocre one makes them tune out.

The Past → Present → Future Framework

Structure your answer in 60-90 seconds using three parts:

  • Past (20 sec): Where you've been — education, key experiences that led you here. Pick 1-2 things that build credibility.
  • Present (20 sec): What you're doing now and what sparked your interest in finance. This is your "pivot point."
  • Future (20 sec): Why THIS specific role at THIS specific firm. Be concrete — not "I'm interested in finance" but "I want to work on healthcare M&A because..."

Key rule: Every sentence should answer the implicit question "so why should I hire you for THIS job?"

Framework for Non-Traditional Backgrounds

If you're coming from outside finance, your story needs to do extra work. You must:

  1. Acknowledge your background — don't hide it, own it as a strength
  2. Draw a clear bridge — connect your skills to what the role requires
  3. Show you've done the work — mention specific steps (courses, modeling, networking) that prove commitment
  4. Explain "why now" — what clicked that made you pursue this path

Example Stories

Example 1: Career Changer from Tech (→ IB) ▶
The Story

"I spent three years as a product manager at a mid-stage SaaS company, where I was responsible for our analytics platform that processed $200M in transaction data. That role gave me deep experience in financial data analysis, cross-functional execution, and making data-driven decisions under tight deadlines.

About a year ago, I led the financial analysis for a potential acquisition target — working closely with our CFO and the bankers from [Bank]. That experience was a turning point. I realized I was most energized by the strategic and financial aspects of the deal, not the product roadmap. Since then, I've completed the BIWS financial modeling program, built DCF and LBO models from scratch, and had over 30 informational conversations with bankers across the Street.

I'm drawn to [Bank]'s TMT group specifically because of your work on [recent deal]. My tech operating experience gives me a differentiated perspective when evaluating technology companies, and I'm eager to apply that lens to advising clients on their most important strategic decisions."

Why This Works
  • Quantifies past experience ($200M) — shows scale
  • Clear pivot moment (the acquisition) — not vague "always been interested"
  • Concrete proof of commitment (modeling course, 30 conversations)
  • Specific about firm and group — shows research
  • Frames non-traditional background as advantage, not weakness
Example 2: Liberal Arts Grad (→ Consulting) ▶
The Story

"I studied economics and philosophy at [University], where I developed strong analytical and communication skills. My senior thesis analyzed market concentration in US healthcare — I built a dataset of 2,000 hospital M&A transactions and found that post-merger price increases averaged 20% in concentrated markets.

After graduation, I joined a healthcare nonprofit where I led a cost reduction initiative that saved $1.2M annually by restructuring our vendor contracts. That project — diagnosing a complex problem, gathering data, building a recommendation, and presenting it to the board — felt exactly like consulting work.

I'm particularly excited about [Firm]'s healthcare practice because of your recent work on [public case/report]. I want to help healthcare organizations solve their hardest strategic problems, and I believe my combination of sector knowledge and analytical rigor would make me effective from day one."

Example 3: Career Changer from Accounting (→ PE) ▶
The Story

"I spent two years in the audit practice at [Big 4 Firm], where I led engagements for middle-market companies across industrials and business services — the exact sectors where your fund invests. I developed a deep understanding of financial statements, quality of earnings adjustments, and what drives working capital for these types of businesses.

What I loved most about audit was the detective work — digging into why margins shifted, finding the stories behind the numbers. But I wanted to use that analytical skill set to make investment decisions, not just verify historical financials. Over the past year, I built several LBO models from 10-Ks, passed the CFA Level 1, and completed an internship at a search fund where I screened 50+ potential acquisitions.

[Fund] stands out to me because of your operational approach to value creation — I noticed your portfolio company [X] grew EBITDA 3x during your hold period. I want to be on the team that identifies those opportunities and drives that kind of transformation."

Handling "Why Finance?" and "Why Our Firm?"

"Why Finance?" — Framework ▶

Never say: "I want to make money" / "I've always been interested in markets" / "I'm a numbers person."

Instead, tell a specific story about a moment when you realized finance was your path. The best answers follow this pattern:

  1. Specific experience that exposed you to finance (a class, a project, a conversation, a deal)
  2. What you found compelling about that experience (the analytical rigor, the strategic thinking, the client impact)
  3. Why that's a better fit than your alternatives
Strong Answer

"During my junior year, I took a corporate finance class where we valued a real company as our final project. I spent weeks building a DCF model for Nike — and I was completely hooked. What drew me in wasn't just the math, but the fact that you had to develop a deep understanding of the business, its competitive position, and its future trajectory to arrive at a number. Since then, I've spent every free moment learning about financial modeling and capital markets. Investment banking appeals to me specifically because of the combination of rigorous analytical work and the opportunity to advise companies on decisions that shape their futures."

"Why Our Firm?" — Framework ▶

You need 2-3 specific, genuine reasons. The formula:

  1. Culture/people reason: "I spoke with [Name], who told me about [specific aspect of culture]. That resonated because..."
  2. Platform/deal/practice reason: "Your [specific group/practice] has done impressive work on [specific deal/project]. I want to work on those types of transactions because..."
  3. Development reason: "Your [training program / mentorship structure / lean staffing model] appeals to me because I want [specific development outcome]."
Never Say These
  • "Because you're a top firm" — obvious, empty
  • "Because of your prestige/brand" — makes you sound status-obsessed
  • "I heard the culture is great" — too vague to be credible
  • Anything you could say about every other firm in the same category

Accounting Technical Questions

Mark Complete

These are the bread-and-butter questions for IB, PE, and even some consulting interviews. You MUST be able to answer these fluently — not just correctly, but clearly and confidently. Practice saying these out loud.

Q1: Walk me through the three financial statements. ▶

Answer: "The three financial statements are the Income Statement, the Balance Sheet, and the Cash Flow Statement.

The Income Statement shows a company's revenues, expenses, and profitability over a period of time. It starts with Revenue, subtracts Cost of Goods Sold to get Gross Profit, subtracts operating expenses to get Operating Income (EBIT), then accounts for interest and taxes to arrive at Net Income.

The Balance Sheet shows a company's financial position at a specific point in time. It follows the equation Assets = Liabilities + Shareholders' Equity. Assets include things like cash, receivables, inventory, and PP&E. Liabilities include payables, debt, and deferred revenue. Equity includes common stock, retained earnings, and APIC.

The Cash Flow Statement reconciles Net Income to the actual cash generated during the period. It has three sections: Cash from Operations (starts with Net Income and adjusts for non-cash items and working capital changes), Cash from Investing (CapEx, acquisitions), and Cash from Financing (debt issuance/repayment, equity issuance, dividends). The ending cash balance ties to the Balance Sheet."

Interview Tip

Keep this under 60 seconds. Interviewers want to see you understand the structure — they don't want a lecture. Be ready for follow-ups about how they link.

Q2: How do the three financial statements link together? ▶

Answer: "They link in several key ways:

  1. Net Income from the Income Statement flows into Retained Earnings on the Balance Sheet and is the starting point for the Cash Flow Statement.
  2. Depreciation & Amortization reduces Net Income on the IS, reduces PP&E (or intangible assets) on the BS, and gets added back on the CFS as a non-cash charge.
  3. Capital Expenditures don't appear on the IS (they're capitalized), but increase PP&E on the BS and appear as a cash outflow in Cash from Investing on the CFS.
  4. Changes in Working Capital (AR, Inventory, AP) appear on the BS and are reflected in Cash from Operations on the CFS. For example, if AR increases, that's a use of cash even though the revenue was recognized on the IS.
  5. Debt issuance/repayment changes the debt balance on the BS, appears in Cash from Financing on the CFS, and the interest expense flows through the IS.
  6. The ending cash balance on the CFS becomes the cash line on the BS."
Q3: A company buys a $100 piece of equipment. Walk me through all 3 statements. ▶

Answer: "At the time of purchase, assuming cash payment:

Income Statement: No immediate impact. Capital expenditures are not expensed — they're capitalized on the Balance Sheet. However, going forward, you'll see annual depreciation expense (say $10/year over 10 years straight-line), which will reduce Operating Income and Net Income.

Balance Sheet: Cash decreases by $100, PP&E increases by $100. Total assets remain the same. No change to liabilities or equity at this point.

Cash Flow Statement: Cash from Investing decreases by $100 (CapEx). Overall cash is down $100.

In Year 1 and beyond (depreciation impact):

  • IS: Depreciation expense of $10 reduces pre-tax income by $10, and net income by $10 × (1 - tax rate) = $7.50 (assuming 25% tax).
  • BS: PP&E decreases by $10 (accumulated depreciation). Retained earnings decreases by $7.50 (lower net income). Cash increases by $2.50 (the tax savings). Note: total assets decline by $7.50 = retained earnings decline, so BS balances.
  • CFS: Net income down $7.50, but D&A of $10 is added back in CFO, so net cash from operations is UP $2.50 (the tax shield). That $2.50 increase in cash ties to the BS."
Common Mistake

Many candidates forget the tax shield effect. Depreciation is a non-cash expense that saves you real taxes. The net cash impact of $10 depreciation at a 25% tax rate is +$2.50 in cash flow from operations.

Q4: What happens if Accounts Receivable goes up by $10? ▶

Answer: "An increase in Accounts Receivable means you recognized revenue but haven't collected the cash yet.

  • IS: Revenue was already recognized (accrual accounting), so no additional impact on the IS from the AR change itself.
  • BS: AR (current asset) increases by $10. If this came from a sale, you'd also see retained earnings increase from the net income effect of that sale.
  • CFS: The $10 increase in AR is a use of cash — it reduces Cash from Operations. This is because you earned revenue but didn't receive cash. The CFS adjusts for this by subtracting increases in AR from Net Income."
Working Capital Rule

Increase in current asset = use of cash. Increase in current liability = source of cash. Remember: AR up → cash down. AP up → cash up. Inventory up → cash down.

Q5: What is working capital and why does it matter? ▶

Answer: "Working capital is Current Assets minus Current Liabilities. It measures a company's short-term liquidity — its ability to fund day-to-day operations.

The key operational components are Accounts Receivable + Inventory - Accounts Payable (sometimes called 'net operating working capital').

It matters because:

  1. Cash flow impact: Growing companies often need MORE working capital (more inventory, more AR), which consumes cash even when the company is profitable.
  2. Valuation impact: Changes in working capital directly affect Free Cash Flow, which is what we discount in a DCF.
  3. Operational efficiency: Lower working capital relative to revenue = more efficient business. This is measured by the Cash Conversion Cycle (DSO + DIO - DPO).
  4. M&A impact: In acquisitions, the buyer and seller negotiate a 'target working capital' level. Deviations lead to purchase price adjustments at closing."
Q6: Cash vs accrual accounting? ▶

Answer: "Under cash accounting, you recognize revenue when cash is received and expenses when cash is paid. Under accrual accounting, you recognize revenue when it's earned and expenses when incurred, regardless of when cash changes hands. This follows the matching principle — matching revenues to the expenses that generated them. Virtually all public companies use accrual accounting because it gives a more accurate picture of financial performance. The Cash Flow Statement exists to reconcile accrual-basis Net Income back to actual cash flows."

Q7: How does depreciation affect the three statements? ▶

Answer: "Depreciation is a non-cash expense that allocates the cost of a tangible asset over its useful life.

  • IS: Depreciation expense reduces EBIT and therefore Net Income. If depreciation is $10 and the tax rate is 25%, Net Income falls by $7.50.
  • CFS: D&A is added back in Cash from Operations because it's non-cash. Starting with Net Income (down $7.50), adding back $10 D&A → net CFO increase of $2.50 (the tax shield).
  • BS: PP&E decreases by $10 (accumulated depreciation). Cash increases by $2.50. Retained earnings decreases by $7.50. Assets down $7.50 = Equity down $7.50."
Q8: What is goodwill and how is it created? ▶
Goodwill
Goodwill = Purchase Price - Fair Value of Net Identifiable Assets

For example, if you buy a company for $500M and its net identifiable assets are worth $350M, you'd record $150M in goodwill. The premium reflects intangible value like brand, customer relationships, and intellectual property. Goodwill is not amortized under US GAAP — instead, it's tested for impairment annually. If the fair value drops below carrying value, goodwill is written down."

Q9: What is deferred revenue? Give an example. ▶

Answer: "Deferred revenue is a liability that arises when a company receives cash before delivering goods or services.

Example: A SaaS company receives $120K upfront for a 12-month subscription. At payment: Cash +$120K, Deferred Revenue +$120K. Each month, $10K is recognized: Revenue +$10K, Deferred Revenue -$10K. Deferred revenue is important in M&A because acquirers must evaluate the fair value of assumed obligations — under purchase accounting, this often gets written down, temporarily reducing post-acquisition revenue."

Q10: Capitalizing vs expensing a cost? ▶

Answer: "When you capitalize, you put it on the BS as an asset and expense it gradually via D&A. When you expense, you hit the IS immediately. Capitalize when benefit spans multiple periods; expense when benefit is only current period.

Impact: Capitalizing → higher net income today, lower later (depreciation). Expensing → lower net income today, no future D&A. Total expense over asset life is the same — it's a timing difference. Capitalizing increases CFO because CapEx is in CFI — this is why some companies are incentivized to capitalize aggressively."

Q11: Can a company have negative shareholders' equity? ▶

Answer: "Yes. This happens through: (1) Accumulated losses — years of negative retained earnings; (2) Large share buybacks — companies like McDonald's and Starbucks have repurchased so many shares that treasury stock exceeds other equity; (3) Leveraged recaps — taking on debt to pay dividends exceeding book equity. Negative equity doesn't necessarily mean trouble — McDonald's has it but is extremely profitable."

Q12: Why might two companies with the same net income have different cash flows? ▶

Answer: Several reasons: (1) Different D&A levels, (2) Different working capital dynamics (growing AR/inventory consumes cash), (3) Different CapEx intensity, (4) Different revenue recognition (deferred revenue), (5) Non-cash charges (SBC, impairments), (6) Different debt repayment schedules. This is why investors look at Free Cash Flow, not just Net Income — cash is king."

Q13: What is a deferred tax liability/asset? ▶

DTL: You paid LESS tax today than book tax expense — you'll owe more in the future. Most common cause: accelerated depreciation for tax purposes vs straight-line for books.

DTA: You paid MORE tax today — you'll owe less in the future. Common causes: NOL carryforwards, and expenses recognized for books before they're tax-deductible (warranty reserves, bad debt reserves). DTAs may require a valuation allowance if the company can't generate enough future taxable income.

Q14: If you could use only one financial statement, which? ▶

Answer: "The Cash Flow Statement, because: (1) It starts with Net Income, giving you the IS in summary; (2) Working capital and CapEx changes reflect BS movements; (3) It shows actual cash generation — you can't fake cash; (4) It tells you if the business is self-funding; (5) FCF is what determines value. That said, the BS is also defensible — it shows everything you own and owe, and you can infer profitability from retained earnings changes."

Q15: What is EBITDA and why do we use it? ▶

Answer: "EBITDA = Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization. It's a proxy for operating cash flow. We use it because it's: (1) Capital structure neutral (excludes interest), (2) Tax neutral, (3) Depreciation-policy neutral, (4) Close to cash flow for low-CapEx businesses.

Limitations: Ignores CapEx (some businesses need massive reinvestment), ignores working capital changes, can be manipulated. Warren Buffett: 'Does management think the tooth fairy pays for capital expenditures?'"

Q16: Walk me through a basic merger model. ▶

Steps:

  1. Calculate purchase price (target equity value + premium)
  2. Determine funding mix (cash, debt, stock)
  3. Calculate goodwill (purchase price - fair value of net identifiable assets)
  4. Adjust acquirer's IS: add target NI, subtract after-tax interest on new debt, subtract lost interest on cash, add after-tax synergies, subtract incremental D&A from write-ups
  5. Calculate new share count (add shares if stock consideration)
  6. Compute pro forma EPS
  7. Compare to standalone — higher = accretive, lower = dilutive
Q17: Common non-recurring add-backs in adjusted EBITDA? ▶
  • Restructuring charges (severance, facility closures)
  • Litigation settlements
  • Stock-based compensation (debatable)
  • One-time transaction costs (M&A fees, IPO costs)
  • Asset impairment charges
  • Gain/loss on asset sales
  • Startup costs for new facilities
  • PE management fees

Key question: is it truly non-recurring? A company with restructuring charges every year isn't really "non-recurring." In PE, the QoE analysis scrutinizes every add-back.

Q18: How do you calculate Free Cash Flow? ▶
Unlevered FCF (used in DCF)
UFCF = EBIT × (1 - Tax Rate) + D&A - CapEx - ΔNet Working Capital
Levered FCF (available to equity)
LFCF = Net Income + D&A - CapEx - ΔNWC - Mandatory Debt Repayment

UFCF is pre-debt — available to ALL capital providers. Discount at WACC. LFCF is post-debt — what's left for equity. Discount at cost of equity.

Q19: $200M revenue, 20% EBITDA margin, $15M D&A, $10M CapEx, 25% tax, NWC up $5M. What's UFCF? ▶
  • EBITDA = $200M × 20% = $40M
  • EBIT = $40M - $15M = $25M
  • NOPAT = $25M × (1 - 25%) = $18.75M
  • Add back D&A: +$15M
  • Subtract CapEx: -$10M
  • Subtract ΔNWC: -$5M
  • UFCF = $18.75M
Q20: Why add back D&A and subtract CapEx separately? ▶

Because they represent fundamentally different things: D&A reflects PAST capital investments being expensed — backward-looking. CapEx reflects CURRENT investments — forward-looking. For growing companies, CapEx > D&A. For declining companies, D&A > CapEx. The difference is "net investment" or "growth CapEx." This distinction matters enormously for valuation.

Valuation Technical Questions

Mark Complete
Q1: Walk me through a DCF. ▶
  1. Project UFCF for 5-10 years (EBIT(1-t) + D&A - CapEx - ΔNWC)
  2. Calculate WACC — blended cost of debt and equity. Cost of equity via CAPM = Rf + β(Rm - Rf)
  3. Terminal Value — Gordon Growth (FCF × (1+g) / (WACC - g)) or Exit Multiple (EBITDA × multiple)
  4. Discount back — PV of each year's FCF plus PV of terminal value at WACC
  5. Bridge EV → equity value per share — subtract net debt, minority interest, preferred; divide by diluted shares
Q2: Enterprise Value vs Equity Value? ▶
EV Bridge
EV = Equity Value + Total Debt + Preferred Stock + Minority Interest - Cash

EV = total value to ALL capital providers. Equity Value = value to equity holders only. Like a house: Equity = down payment, EV = full price (equity + mortgage), Cash = money in the couch.

Matching principle: EV multiples (EV/EBITDA, EV/Revenue) use pre-interest metrics. Equity multiples (P/E, P/B) use post-interest metrics. Never mix them.

Q3: How do you calculate WACC? ▶
WACC
WACC = (E/V) × Re + (D/V) × Rd × (1 - t)
Re = Rf + β × (Rm - Rf) [CAPM]

Typical: 8-12% for most companies. Tech with no debt: 10-14%. Utilities with heavy debt: 5-7%.

Q4: When would you NOT use a DCF? ▶
  • Early-stage/pre-revenue — no meaningful cash flows to project
  • Highly cyclical — hard to project stable growth
  • Banks/financials — debt is core business; use dividend discount model or P/B
  • Distressed companies — cash flows unpredictable; use liquidation analysis
  • No good projections — garbage in, garbage out
Q5: Which valuation method typically gives the highest value? ▶

Generally, from lowest to highest:

  1. 52-week trading range (lowest)
  2. Trading comps (no control premium)
  3. DCF (depends on assumptions)
  4. Precedent transactions (highest — includes 20-40% control premium)

But it varies with specific assumptions and market conditions. The value of multiple methodologies is seeing convergence/divergence. Presented as a "football field" chart.

Q6: What makes a good LBO candidate? ▶
  1. Stable, predictable cash flows
  2. Strong market position / competitive moat
  3. Low CapEx requirements → more FCF for debt paydown
  4. Opportunities for operational improvement
  5. Strong or installable management team
  6. Tangible asset base (collateral)
  7. Low existing leverage
  8. Clear exit path (strategics, IPO, secondary)
  9. Non-cyclical business

Classic sectors: business services, healthcare services, industrials, food & beverage, software.

Q7: Walk me through an LBO. ▶
  1. Sources & Uses: Purchase price (EBITDA × entry multiple). Fund with 60-70% debt, 30-40% equity.
  2. Operating model: Project revenue, EBITDA, CapEx, working capital for 5 years.
  3. Debt schedule: Mandatory amortization, cash sweep, interest for each tranche.
  4. FCF: EBITDA - taxes - CapEx - ΔNWC - interest - mandatory amortization = cash for optional paydown.
  5. Exit: Exit multiple × terminal EBITDA. Subtract remaining debt = exit equity.
  6. Returns: IRR = (Exit Equity / Entry Equity)^(1/years) - 1. MOIC = Exit/Entry. Targets: 20%+ IRR, 2.5x+ MOIC.
Q8: Walk me through accretion/dilution. ▶
  1. Start with acquirer's standalone NI and EPS
  2. Add target's NI
  3. Subtract financing cost: lost interest (cash), new interest (debt), or diluted shares (stock)
  4. Add after-tax synergies
  5. Subtract incremental D&A from write-ups
  6. Pro forma EPS = combined NI / new shares
  7. If pro forma > standalone = accretive; if lower = dilutive

Rule of thumb: If acquirer P/E > target P/E, all-stock deal is accretive.

Q9: How do you select comparable companies? ▶

Based on: (1) Same industry/sector, (2) Similar size (0.5-2x), (3) Similar growth profile, (4) Similar margins, (5) Same geography, (6) Similar business model, (7) Similar capital structure. Start with 10-15, narrow to 5-8 most similar. Include rationale for inclusion/exclusion.

Q11: Terminal value methods? ▶
Gordon Growth
TV = Final FCF × (1 + g) / (WACC - g) — g typically 2-3%
Exit Multiple
TV = Final Year EBITDA × Exit Multiple

Use both and cross-check. Exit Multiple more common in practice. If exit multiple implies perpetuity growth > GDP, assumptions may be too aggressive. TV is typically 60-80% of total EV — that's why sensitivity analysis is critical.

Q12: Quick math — $100M EBITDA, 10x EV/EBITDA, $200M debt, $50M cash, 50M shares. Price? ▶
  • EV = $100M × 10 = $1,000M
  • Equity Value = $1,000M - $200M + $50M = $850M
  • Share price = $850M / 50M = $17.00

Behavioral Questions — STAR Method

Mark Complete
The STAR Framework
  • Situation: Set the scene (1-2 sentences)
  • Task: YOUR specific role/responsibility
  • Action: What YOU did (not "we")
  • Result: Quantifiable outcome. Always end with a number.

Target: 60-90 seconds. Prepare 6-8 stories that flex across question types.

Tell me about a time you led a team. ▶

What they test: Leadership, delegation, motivation, conflict resolution.

Key: Show you can lead without authority. Finance roles require influence, not just management. Mention how you got buy-in, not just how you assigned tasks. End with a quantified result.

Tell me about a time you failed. ▶

Rules:

  1. Pick a REAL failure, not a humble-brag ("I worked too hard")
  2. Take genuine ownership
  3. Spend 70% on what you LEARNED and how you changed
  4. Show the behavioral change that resulted

Template: "In [role], I [mistake]. Impact was [consequence]. Root cause: [assessment]. I learned [lesson]. Since then, I've [specific change], applied by [example]."

Tell me about working under extreme pressure. ▶

What they test: Stress tolerance (crucial in IB/PE), prioritization, composure.

Show how you stayed organized and made good decisions under pressure, not just fast ones. IB and PE involve brutal hours — they want evidence you thrive, not just survive.

Dealing with a difficult person. ▶

Rules: Never badmouth the person. Focus on resolution, not conflict. Show empathy. End with maintained relationship.

Template: "I worked with [person] who [behavior]. Rather than [reactive], I [proactive — e.g., 1:1 meeting, asked questions]. Discovered [underlying concern]. Agreed to [solution]. Result: [outcome + relationship intact]."

Greatest weakness? ▶

Name a REAL but non-fatal weakness. Show self-awareness, specific steps to address it, and measurable improvement.

Good: "I tend to want to perfect work before sharing it. I've addressed this by setting draft deadlines and sharing at 80% for directional feedback."

Bad: "I work too hard" (fake), "I'm bad with numbers" (disqualifying).

Why should we hire you? ▶

3 pillars in 60 seconds:

  1. Relevant skill: "[Specific skill] maps to [role need]. Evidence: [example]."
  2. Differentiator: "[Unique combination] means I can [specific value-add]."
  3. Fit/motivation: "[Specific culture element at firm] aligns with [how I work]."

More Questions to Prepare

  • Time you persuaded someone who disagreed with you
  • Decision with incomplete information
  • Most significant accomplishment
  • When you went above and beyond
  • Receiving and responding to critical feedback
  • Managing competing priorities
  • Learning something quickly
  • Demonstrating attention to detail
  • Complex team problem-solving
Prep Strategy

Prepare 6-8 STAR stories. Map each to 3-4 question types. Practice out loud. Time yourself — over 90 seconds = cut details. Record yourself and watch back.

Why investment banking specifically? ▶

Structure: (1) Specific exposure that sparked interest, (2) What draws you — analytical rigor + strategic decisions + steep learning curve, (3) Why not alternatives.

Never say: Money, exit ops, prestige, "seems interesting," anything sounding like IB is a stepping stone.

What do you know about our recent deals? ▶

Prep: Go to bank's website → "Recent Transactions." Pick 2-3 in your target group. For each: buyer/seller, deal size, rationale, your opinion. Read WSJ, FT, Bloomberg, league tables.

Pitch me a stock. ▶
  1. Overview (15 sec): "[Company] is a [$X revenue] [industry] company that [what they do]."
  2. Thesis (45 sec): 2-3 catalysts why it's mispriced.
  3. Valuation (30 sec): "Trades at [X]x, [Y]% discount to peers. DCF → fair value [$Z], [%] upside."
  4. Risks (15 sec): 1-2 risks + mitigation.
Don't

Pick a mega-cap everyone knows. Skip valuation. Ignore risks. Pick something you can't defend with numbers.

PE-Specific Interview Prep

Mark Complete

PE interviews are more intensive: fit + technicals, deal walk-throughs, LBO modeling tests (2-3 hrs), and case studies with CIMs.

Walk me through a deal you've worked on. ▶
  1. Context (30 sec): Deal type, buyer/seller, size, your role.

VC-Specific Interview Prep

Mark Complete

VC interviews are less technical, more about market intuition, pattern recognition, and founder empathy.

What would you invest in and why? ▶
  1. Thesis: "I'm excited about [sector] because [macro trend + insight]."
  2. Company: Market (TAM + tailwind), Product (differentiation, PMF), Team (founder-market fit), Traction (metrics).
  3. Risks: 1-2 + mitigation.
  4. Valuation: "At [last round], entry seems [view] because [comparison]."
Prep

Follow 3-5 sectors deeply. Read TechCrunch, The Information, Newcomer, Stratechery. Know 2-3 companies per sector with real metrics.

How do you evaluate a startup pitch? ▶
  1. Team (40%): Domain expertise, startup experience, grit?
  2. Market (30%): TAM large enough? Timing right? Secular tailwind?
  3. Product (20%): Differentiation? PMF evidence (retention, NPS)?
  4. Business model (10%): Unit economics trajectory? Scalability?

Key questions to ask: "Why did you start this?", "What's your unfair advantage?", "What does retention look like?", "Biggest risk?", "How will you use this capital?"

Market sizing (TAM/SAM/SOM) ▶
  • TAM: Total revenue if 100% global share. Top-down.
  • SAM: Portion you can target with current product/GTM.
  • SOM: What you can capture in 3-5 years given competition.

VCs prefer bottom-up: # potential customers × ACV = SAM. Validate with top-down research.

Example: B2B restaurant inventory SaaS: 660K US restaurants → 200K targetable → $5K ACV = $1B SAM → 10% near-term penetration = $100M SOM.

Consulting Case Interview Prep

Mark Complete
Case Interview Format

25-35 minutes. Business problem → clarify → structure → analyze data → recommend.

Scoring: Structured thinking (40%), Analytics (25%), Business judgment (20%), Communication (15%).

The 5-Step Process

1

Listen & Clarify (2 min)

Repeat problem back. Ask 2-3 questions on objective, scope, timeline.

2

Structure (2-3 min)

"Let me take a minute to structure." Build MECE framework with 3-4 buckets TAILORED to this case.

3

Analyze (15-20 min)

Work through buckets, asking for data. Do math cleanly. State the "so what?" for every finding.

4

Synthesize (1 min)

"Let me pull together what I've found." Summarize key findings.

5

Recommend (2 min)

Lead with answer: "I recommend [X]." Support with 2-3 data points. Add risks + next steps.

Practice Case: Profitability Decline

Case: GreenGro organic grocery — profit down 30% despite revenue growth. ▶

Clarify

200 stores, 25 states, $2B revenue, all regions affected, 6-month fix timeline.

Structure

Revenue growing but profit declining → cost or margin mix issue:

  1. Revenue quality: Growth from volume, price, or new stores? Mix shift?
  2. Cost structure: COGS and OpEx trends?
  3. Store-level economics: New stores dilutive?
  4. Competitive dynamics: Price pressure?

Analysis

Revenue: $1.7B → $2.0B. Same-store +3%. 20 new stores. Avg revenue/store flat.

Cost data: COGS: 62% → 67% ($100M hit). Labor: 18% → 19.5% ($30M). New store costs: $15M. Total: ~$145M impact.

COGS root cause: Organic supplier costs +15% (2pp), prepared foods mix shift (1.5pp), shrinkage 3% → 5% (1.5pp).

Recommendation

  1. Renegotiate suppliers + diversify sourcing → $40-60M
  2. Fix shrinkage (demand forecasting, cold chain) → $30M
  3. Optimize labor scheduling (traffic-based) → $15-20M

Total: $85-110M recovery in 12-18 months. Pilot in 10 stores first.

Case Math Tips
  • Round: $487M → "roughly $500M"
  • State approach first: "I'll multiply X by Y to get Z"
  • Sense-check: "Does $2.50/customer sound right? About a coffee — yes"
  • Percentages: 15% of $400M = 10%($40M) + 5%($20M) = $60M
  • Growth: Rule of 72 → 72/growth% = years to double
